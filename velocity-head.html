2. Determine the theoretical velocity generated by a 10-m hydraulic head?


The theoretical velocity generated by a 10-m hydraulic head can be determined using the Bernoulli's equation which relates the pressure, velocity, and height of a fluid.

The Bernoulli's equation states that the total energy of a fluid is constant along a streamline. Mathematically, it is expressed as:

P + (1/2)ρv² + ρgh = constant

where P is the pressure, ρ is the density of the fluid, v is the velocity of the fluid, g is the acceleration due to gravity, h is the height, and the constant represents the total energy of the fluid.

Assuming that the fluid is water at room temperature and pressure, we can use the following values:

ρ = 1000 kg/m³ (density of water)
g = 9.81 m/s² (acceleration due to gravity)

Using the Bernoulli's equation, we can solve for the velocity v:

P₁ + (1/2)ρv₁² + ρgh₁ = P₂ + (1/2)ρv₂² + ρgh₂

Since we are considering a 10-m hydraulic head, h₁ = 10 m and h₂ = 0.

Therefore, the equation simplifies to:

(1/2)ρv₁² + ρgh₁ = (1/2)ρv₂²

Solving for v₁, we get:

v₁ = √(2gh₁)

Substituting the given values, we get:

v₁ = √(2 × 9.81 m/s² × 10 m)

v₁ ≈ 14.0 m/s

Therefore, the theoretical velocity generated by a 10-m hydraulic head is approximately 14.0 m/s.

12. calculate the velocity of a train heading 1857km 68°SW of bicol in 2hrs.​


Answer:

velocity_SW = (1857 km / 2 hours) x cos(112°) = - 926.2 km/h

Explanation:

To calculate the velocity of the train, we need to use the formula:

velocity = distance / time

where distance is the distance traveled by the train and time is the time taken to travel that distance.

In this case, the distance traveled by the train is 1857 km and the time taken is 2 hours.

To find the component of the velocity in the south-west direction, we need to multiply the velocity by the cosine of the angle between the velocity vector and the south-west direction. The angle between the velocity vector and the south-west direction is 180° - 68° = 112°.

So, the velocity of the train in the south-west direction is:

velocity_SW = (1857 km / 2 hours) x cos(112°) = - 926.2 km/h

Note that the negative sign indicates that the velocity is in the opposite direction to the south-west direction

In Instantaneous Velocity and Speed and Average and Instantaneous Acceleration we introduced the kinematic functions of velocity and acceleration using the derivative. By taking the derivative of the position function we found the velocity function, and likewise by taking the derivative of the velocity function we found the acceleration function. Using integral calculus, we can work backward and calculate the velocity function from the acceleration function, and the position function from the velocity function.

Kinematic Equations from Integral Calculus

Let’s begin with a particle with an acceleration a(t) is a known function of time. Since the time derivative of the velocity function is acceleration,

\[\frac{d}{dt}v(t)=a(t),\]

we can take the indefinite integral of both sides, finding

\[\int \frac{d}{dt}v(t)dt=\int a(t)dt+{C}_{1},\]

where C1 is a constant of integration. Since

\[\int \frac{d}{dt}v(t)dt=v(t)\]

, the velocity is given by

\[v(t)=\int a(t)dt+{C}_{1}.\]

Similarly, the time derivative of the position function is the velocity function,

\[\frac{d}{dt}x(t)=v(t).\]

Thus, we can use the same mathematical manipulations we just used and find

\[x(t)=\int v(t)dt+{C}_{2},\]

where C2 is a second constant of integration.

We can derive the kinematic equations for a constant acceleration using these integrals. With a(t) = a a constant, and doing the integration in (Figure), we find

\[v(t)=\int adt+{C}_{1}=at+{C}_{1}.\]

If the initial velocity is v(0) = v0, then

\[{v}_{0}=0+{C}_{1}.\]

Then, C1 = v0 and

\[v(t)={v}_{0}+at,\]

which is (Equation). Substituting this expression into (Figure) gives

\[x(t)=\int ({v}_{0}+at)dt+{C}_{2}.\]

\[x(t)={v}_{0}t+\frac{1}{2}a{t}^{2}+{C}_{2}.\]

If x(0) = x0, we have

\[{x}_{0}=0+0+{C}_{2};\]

27. a bicycle is heading west it goes 500m in 50s what is its velocity]


The velocity of the bicycle is 10 m/s, units - meter per second (m/s), direction - west

Explanation:

In this problem, we are to solve the velocity of the bicycle including its units and direction.

Formula to use

v = d/t

where

v     is the velocity, the unit is meter per second (m/s)

d     is the displacement, the unit is in meter (m)

t      is the time, the unit is in seconds (s)

Given information

d = 5000 m

t = 500 s

v = ?

Solving the problem

Let us solve the velocity of the bicycle using the given formula

v = d/t

Then, substitute the given data

v = 5000m / 500s

v = 10 m/s

Therefore, the velocity of the bicycle is 10 m/s, the unit is meter per second and the direction is going to the west.
