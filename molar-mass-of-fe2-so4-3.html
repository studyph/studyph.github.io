2. How many moles of Fe2(SO4)3 are present in 300 grams of Fe2(SO4)3 ?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of Fe₂(SO₄)₃.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{molar mass} & = \text{2(55.85 g) + 3(32.07 g) + 12(16.00 g)} \\ & = \text{399.9 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of Fe₂(SO₄)₃ by using dimensional analysis.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{Fe}_2(\text{SO}_4)_3 & = \text{300 g} \: \text{Fe}_2(\text{SO}_4)_3 \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{Fe}_2(\text{SO}_4)_3}{\text{399.9 g} \: \text{Fe}_2(\text{SO}_4)_3} \\ & = \boxed{\text{0.750 mol}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, there is 0.750 mol of Fe₂(SO₄)₃ in 300 g of Fe₂(SO₄)₃.

13. Determine ΔH for the reaction Fe2(SO4)3 → Fe2O3 + 3SO3 ΔH =____given these data:2Fe + 1½O2 → Fe2O3 ΔH = −1,650 kJ/mol 3S + 4½O2 → 3SO3 ΔH = −792 kJ/mol 2Fe + 3S + 6O2 → Fe2(SO4)3 ΔH = −2,583 kJ/mol​


[tex]\tt{\huge{\blue{Explanation:}}}[/tex]

Our goal here is to calculate the enthalpy change for the decomposition of Fe₂(SO₄)₃ to form Fe₂O₃ and SO₃. The reaction does not occur directly, however, so we must use an indirect route using the information given by Equations (a), (b), and (c). The equations are:

(a) 2Fe + (3/2)O₂ → Fe₂O₃ ∆H = -1,650 kJ/mol

(b) 3S + (9/2)O₂ → 3SO₃ ∆H = -792 kJ/mol

(c) 2Fe + 3S + 6O₂ → Fe₂(SO₄)₃ ∆H = -2,583 kJ/mol

The overall reaction is

Fe₂(SO₄)₃ → Fe₂O₃ + 3SO₃

[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

First, we need 1 mole of Fe₂O₃ as a product and this is provided by Equation (a). Next, we need 3 moles of SO₃ as a product and this is provided by Equation (b). Looking at the overall reaction, we need 1 moles of Fe₂(SO₄)₃ as reactant. So we reverse Equation (c) to get

(d) [tex]\begin{array}{cccccccc} \text{Fe}_2(\text{SO}_4)_3 & \rightarrow & 2\text{Fe} & + & 3\text{S} & + & 6\text{O}_2 & \Delta H = \text{2,583 kJ/mol} \end{array}[/tex]

Adding Equations (a), (b), and (d) together, we get

[tex]\begin{array}{llllllllll} 2\text{Fe} & + & \frac{3}{2}\text{O}_2 & \rightarrow & \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 & & & & & \Delta H = -\text{1,650 kJ/mol} \\ 3\text{S} & + & \frac{9}{2}\text{O}_2 & \rightarrow & 3\text{SO}_3 & & & & & \Delta H = -\text{792 kJ/mol} \\ & & \text{Fe}_2(\text{SO}_4)_3 & \rightarrow & 2\text{Fe} & + & 3\text{S} & + & 6\text{O}_2 & \Delta H = \text{2,583 kJ/mol} \\ \hline & & \text{Fe}_2(\text{SO}_4)_3 & \rightarrow & \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 & + & 3\text{SO}_3 & & & \Delta H = \text{141 kJ/mol} \end{array}[/tex]

Hence, the heat of reaction is

[tex]\boxed{\Delta H = \text{141 kJ/mol}}[/tex]

21. Calculate the molar mass of KAl(SO4)2·


Answer:

The molar mass of KAl(SO₄)₂ is 285.202 g/mol

Explanation:

The name of the compound KAl(SO₄)₂ is potassium aluminum sulfate.

The molar mass of a chemical compound can be computed by getting the sum of the standard atomic masses (in g/mol).

To solve for the molar mass of the this compound, we need first the individual molar masses of the elements present is the compound.

Here is the list of the involved elements with their molar mass:

Potassim (K) - 39.0983 g/molAluminum (Al) - 26.981539 g/molSulfur (S) - 32.065 g/molOxygen (O) - 15.999 g/mol

These information can be found in the periodic table.

Looking at the chemical formula of potassium aluminum sulfate, we can notice the subscripts in oxygen atom (O), and in the sulfate (SO₄). These subscripts indicates how many times the atom is to be multiplied.

Solving for the molar mass:

Potassim (K) - multiply by one since there is no subscript beside it

→ 39.0983 g/mol * 1 = 39.0983 g/mol

Aluminum (Al) - multiply by one since there is no subscript beside it

→ 26.981539 g/mol * 1 = 26.981539 g/mol

Sulfur (S) - multiply by 2 since there is subscript 2 outside the bracket

→ 32.065 g/mol * 2 = 64.13 g/mol

Oxygen (O) - multiply by 8 since it has a subscript 4 on its side and has another subscript 2 outside the bracket

→ 15.999 g/mol * 8 = 127.992 g/mol

Now we add all the underlined numbers:

39.0983 + 26.981539 + 64.13 + 127.992 = 258.202 g/mol

Therefore, the molar mass of KAl(SO₄)₂ is 285.202 g/mol.

25. The vapor pressure of pure water at 26°Cis 25.21 mmHg. What is the vapor pressure of a solution which contains 179.38 g of iron (III)sulfate[Fe2(SO4)3]in 450.0 g of water?The molar mass of iron (III)sulfateand water are 399.88g/mol and 18.0 g/mol, respectively


To solve this problem, we need to use Raoult's law, which states that the vapor pressure of a solution is proportional to the mole fraction of the solvent in the solution. The mole fraction of water in the solution can be calculated as follows:

Moles of water = mass of water / molar mass of water
Moles of water = 450.0 g / 18.0 g/mol
Moles of water = 25.0 mol

Moles of iron (III) sulfate = mass of iron (III) sulfate / molar mass of iron (III) sulfate
Moles of iron (III) sulfate = 179.38 g / 399.88 g/mol
Moles of iron (III) sulfate = 0.448 mol

Total moles of solute and solvent = moles of water + moles of iron (III) sulfate
Total moles of solute and solvent = 25.0 mol + 0.448 mol
Total moles of solute and solvent = 25.448 mol

The mole fraction of water in the solution is:

Mole fraction of water = moles of water / total moles of solute and solvent
Mole fraction of water = 25.0 mol / 25.448 mol
Mole fraction of water = 0.981

According to Raoult's law, the vapor pressure of the solution is:

Vapor pressure of solution = mole fraction of water x vapor pressure of pure water
Vapor pressure of solution = 0.981 x 25.21 mmHg
Vapor pressure of solution = 24.72 mmHg

Therefore, the vapor pressure of the solution is 24.72 mmHg.
