4. examples of aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems


(i) Aquatic Eco-system:

On the basis of salt content, aquatic eco-system can be divided into fresh water eco-system and marine eco-system.

The fresh water eco-system are usually named according to the size and nature of the aquatic body. Hence, the fresh water eco-system may be pond eco-system, lake eco-system, river eco-system and spring eco-system.

(ii) Terrestrial eco-system:

On the basis of the habitat conditions, the terrestrial eco-system can be divided into four sub-eco-systems.

These are:
(a) Grassland eco-system.
(b) Forest eco-system.
(c) Desert eco-system.
(d) Artificial eco-system or man-made eco-system.

7. 3. Types of Ecosystem Terrestrial Aquatic​


Answer:

1) Ecosystem refers to all of the non-living and living elements of a natural environment, including but not limited to water, sunlight, rock, sand, vegetation, microorganisms, bugs and wildlife.

2) Functions of ecosystem are exchange of energy and nutrients in the food chain. Functions of ecosystem are: It regulates the essential ecological processes, supports life systems and renders stability, it is responsible for the cycling of nutrients between biotic and abiotic components, and it maintains a balance among the various trophic levels in the ecosystem.

3) The types of ecosystem are: Terrestrial ecosystem, forest ecosystem, grassland ecosystem, desert ecosystem, tundra ecosystem, freshwater ecosystem, marine ecosystem, estuary ecosystem and mangrove ecosystem.

4) Structure of the ecosystem:

Abiotic: Soil, water, light, organic nutrients and weather

Biotic: Producers and Consumers

16. how important are feeding in an aquatic ecosystem


Answer:

Primary producers — including bacteria, phytoplankton, and algae — form the lowest trophic level, the base of the aquatic food web. Primary producers synthesize their own energy without needing to eat. Many photosynthesize, using the sun’s energy to build carbohydrates. However, some primary producers can create energy without sunlight using chemosynthesis to metabolize chemicals released from hydrothermal vents, methane seeps, and other geological features.
