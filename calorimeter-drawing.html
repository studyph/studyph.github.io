Afterwards the sample was released, the calorimeter’s water final temperature is 26.35 ° C. What mass of the water was in the calorimeter?​


Answer:

Solving this equation, you find that the mass of the water in the calorimeter is approximately 10.9 g.

Explanation:

To solve this problem, you need to use the equation for calculating heat transfer:

Heat transferred = mass * specific heat capacity * temperature change

In this case, the heat transferred is the heat gained by the water in the calorimeter. The mass of the water is the unknown quantity that you need to solve for. The specific heat capacity of water is 4.184 J/g°C, and the temperature change is the difference between the final temperature of the water (26.35 °C) and its initial temperature (23.0 °C).

Plugging these values into the equation, you get:

Heat transferred = mass * 4.184 J/g°C * (26.35 °C - 23.0 °C)

You also know that the heat transferred is equal to the heat lost by the vanadium sample. The heat lost by the sample can be calculated using the same equation:

Heat lost by the sample = mass of sample * specific heat capacity of sample * temperature change of sample

The specific heat capacity of vanadium is 0.489 J/g°C, and the temperature change of the sample is the difference between its initial temperature (78 °C) and the final temperature of the water (26.35 °C). Plugging these values into the equation, you get:

Heat lost by the sample = 35.9 g * 0.489 J/g°C * (78 °C - 26.35 °C)

Principle of calorimetry - law

The principle of calorimetry (or principle of mixtures) states that for an insulated system, heat energy lost by the hot body is equal to the heat energy gained by the cold body. Note: Heat transfer occurs until both the bodies attain the same temperature(t).


12. Explain the process of measuring the calorific value of a gaseous fuel using a bomb calorimeter. Discuss the role of each component of the calorimeter and the safety precautions that should be taken during the experiment.


Answer:

(answered by Knowledge to help and guide the questioners throughout)

A bomb calorimeter is an instrument that measures the calorific value (heat energy) of a gaseous fuel. It consists of a sealed metal chamber, known as a bomb, in which a sample of the fuel can be burned under controlled conditions. The heat released by the combustion reaction is collected by a body of water sitting inside the calorimeter, and the increase in temperature of the water is measured and used to calculate the energy released by the fuel.

The bomb calorimeter is made up of four main parts: the bomb, the oxygen inlet pump, the water container, and the thermometer. The bomb is an insulated steel container that can withstand high pressures, and is used to securely contain the fuel and control the combustion reaction. The oxygen inlet pump is used to create an oxygen-rich environment inside the bomb, which is necessary for complete combustion of the fuel. The water container is used to collect the heat released by the combustion reaction, and the thermometer is used to measure the increase in temperature of the water.

When measuring the calorific value of a gaseous fuel using a bomb calorimeter, there are several important safety precautions that must be taken. First, make sure the bomb is securely closed before starting the experiment. The contents of the bomb are highly flammable, and must not be exposed to open flames or sparks. Second, never leave the bomb unattended when it is in use, as it may overpressure and become dangerous. Third, use proper ventilation when the bomb is in use and keep combustible materials away from the calorimeter. Finally, always handle the calorimeter equipment carefully to avoid causing any unnecessary damage.

By following these safety guidelines and properly operating the bomb calorimeter, it is possible to accurately measure the calorific value of a gaseous fuel. In doing so, researchers can get valuable insight into the potential energy of the fuel and its effectiveness as a fuel source.

20. If 4.168 kJ of heat is added to a calorimeter containing 75.40 g of water, the temperature of the water and the calorimeter increases from 24.58 °C to 35.82 °C. Calculate the heat capacity of the calorimeter (in J/ °C). The specific heat of water is 4.184 J/g ·°C.


Explanation:

To calculate the heat capacity of the calorimeter, we need to use the formula:

Q = (m_water * c_water * ΔT_water) + (C_cal * ΔT_cal)

where Q is the heat added to the calorimeter, m_water is the mass of water in the calorimeter, c_water is the specific heat of water, ΔT_water is the change in temperature of the water, C_cal is the heat capacity of the calorimeter, and ΔT_cal is the change in temperature of the calorimeter.

We can first calculate the heat absorbed by the water in the calorimeter using the formula:

Q_water = m_water * c_water * ΔT_water

where Q_water is the heat absorbed by the water in the calorimeter. We can substitute the given values and solve for Q_water:

Q_water = (75.40 g) * (4.184 J/g·°C) * (35.82 °C - 24.58 °C)

Q_water = 4782.12 J

Next, we can calculate the heat absorbed by the calorimeter using the formula:

Q_cal = Q - Q_water

where Q_cal is the heat absorbed by the calorimeter. We can substitute the given values and the calculated value of Q_water to solve for Q_cal:

Q_cal = (4168 J) - (4782.12 J)

Q_cal = -614.12 J

The negative sign indicates that the calorimeter lost heat to its surroundings.

Finally, we can solve for the heat capacity of the calorimeter using the formula:

C_cal = Q_cal / ΔT_cal

We can substitute the given values to find C_cal:

C_cal = (-614.12 J) / (35.82 °C - 24.58 °C)

C_cal = (-614.12 J) / (11.24 °C)

C_cal = 54.7 J/°C

Therefore, the heat capacity of the calorimeter is 54.7 J/°C

26. A 0.750-kilogram calorimeter, with unknown specific heat, has an initial temperature of 25.0°c. Five hundred grams of water at 55.0°C is poured into the calorimeter. If the temperature of the water and calorimeter, upon reaching its thermal equilibrium, is 35.0°C, what is the specific heat of the calorimeter?(Use Cwater = 1.00 kcal/kgcº)​


Answer:

The specific heat of the calorimeter is 1.33 kcal/kg· °C

Explanation:

In this problem, the water and calorimeter have reached thermal equilibrium, meaning, they have reached equal heat. So, to find the specific heat of the calorimeter, we need to equate the heat of the water and calorimeter as equal.

Q(calorimeter) = Q(water)

Formula to use

Q = mcΔT

where

Q     is the heat, the unit is in Joules (J) or cal or kcal

m     is the mass, the unit is in kg

ΔT    is the change in temperature, the unit is in °C

Given information

m(calorimeter) = 0.75 kg

T₁ (cal) = 25 °C     (initial temperature of the calorimeter)

T₂ (cal) = 35 °C     (final temperature of the calorimeter)

m(water) = 500 g = 0.5 kg

T₁ (water) = 55 °C

T₂ (water) = 35 °C

c (cal) = ?

Solving the problem

Let us solve the specific heat of the calorimeter using the given equation above.

Q(calorimeter) = Q(water)

Then, substitute the given data

(0.75 kg)c(cal)(35°C - 25°C) = (0.5 kg)(1.00 kcal/kg·°C)(35°C - 55 °C)

c(cal)(7.5 kg· °C) = -10 kcal

Simplify the equation

c(calorimeter) = - 10 kcal / 7.5 kg· °C

c(calorimeter) = - 1.33 kcal/kg· °C

Take note that the negative sign indicates that the temperature of the water is greater than the calorimeter and the water has larger specific heat that the calorimeter.

Therefore, the specific heat of the calorimeter is 1.33 kcal/kg· °C
