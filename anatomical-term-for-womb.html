12. why do you need to know the anatomical terms of movements?


Answer:

Motion, the process of movement, is described using specific anatomical terms. Motion includes movement of organs, joints, limbs, and specific sections of the body. The terminology used describes this motion according to its direction relative to the anatomical position of the joints. Anatomists use a unified set of terms to describe most of the movements, although other, more specialized terms are necessary for describing the uniqueness of the movements such as those of the hands, feet, and eyes.

In general, motion is classified according to the anatomical plane it occurs in. Flexion and extension are examples of angular motions, in which two axes of a joint are brought closer together or moved further apart. Rotational motion may occur at other joints, for example the shoulder, and are described as internal or external. Other terms, such as elevation and depression, describe movement above or below the horizontal plane. Many anatomical terms derive from Latin terms with the same meaning.

28. briefly explain why is knowledge of the etymology and anatomical and physiological terms useful?​


ANSWER:

Because they were descriptive when first named by people who understood Latin & Greek.

They used pretty much the same convention as Linnaeus subsequently used to derive his binomial classification of species: A proper noun as the generic name, followed by an adjective or possessive as the descriptor, with further descriptors suffixed as necessary.

Hence Musculus biceps brachii.

“Musculus" is the diminutive of Latin Mus, for mouse, because the bulging bellies of muscles were reminiscent of little mice under the skin.

“Biceps" is Latin for two-headed, because the muscle has two proximal (Latin, proximus = nearest) attachments, to the scapula (diminutive of Graeco-Latin scaptus, spade, from the bone' s resemblance to a trowel). This descriptor distinguishes the muscle from its 3-headed antagonist the Triceps.

“Brachii", Latin for “of the arm" distinguishes it from its counterpart in the thigh, the Biceps femoris.
