9. are emotivism plausible for standards of morality​


Answer:

That depends on how you choose to define and use those terms.

Morality cannot be truly objective (or not knowably so, which amounts to the same thing), but the mistake is to think that subjective is an amorphous bloc of personal preferences. The statement that Paris is the capital of France is similarly not a purely objective statement, since concepts like Paris, France and capitals are human constructs. But it’s not simply a statement of my personal preferences, and if I say that Paris is the capital of Australia I’m simply wrong.

So while it is subjective, Paris is the capital of France does not “float free” of all objective considerations, and the same can be said of morality. It’s rooted in human nature as a social animal, and in the objective specifics of particular societies. Any personal morality that was totally disconnected from these would not be recognisable as a morality.

Keep in mind, a character doesn’t have to be nice, or moral, or a pillar of the community. Decent people with no flaws or vices don’t usually make for the most interesting reading. But nor can a character be all bad, with no redeeming traits. In other words, a character has to possess one essential element: complexity.

I don’t mean to suggest that a character should be hard to read (in fact, you don’t want them to be), or super mysterious, or generally murky and unclear. You want the reader to know what makes your person tick, what gets them up in the morning and what wakes them up at night. Your reader needs to know what your character wants, what he’s afraid of losing and willing to fight for.
