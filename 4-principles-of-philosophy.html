1. what is principle in philosophy ​


Answer:

In philosophy, a principle is a fundamental and general proposition or rule that is considered to be the basis for reasoning or action. Principles are often used to guide ethical or moral decision-making and can be derived from a range of sources such as religious or cultural beliefs, logical deduction, or scientific inquiry.

Principles can be understood in different ways depending on the philosophical tradition and context in which they are used. In some cases, principles are seen as a priori truths that are self-evident and do not require empirical verification. In other cases, principles are seen as hypotheses that are subject to testing and refinement through observation and experimentation.

Examples of principles in philosophy include the principle of non-contradiction, which states that something cannot both be and not be at the same time and in the same respect; the principle of utility, which holds that actions should be evaluated based on their ability to promote the greatest happiness for the greatest number of people; and the principle of autonomy, which recognizes the individual's right to self-determination and freedom from external coercion.

Answer:

Difference of Principle and Philosophy

There is more than one way to analyze the question. The first is to point out that “principle” usually refers to just one idea, whereas a philosophy is generally a whole body of inter-related ideas. So it’s one idea vs. many. But that answer is trivial.

Another way to analyze the question is talk about “principle” in its more general sense. As in…

“Living according to principle” [which might be more than one idea] VS.

“Living by a philosophy.”

This to me is a much more interesting distinction, and there is an answer….

In this comparison, both ways of living confer a kind of consistency and loyalty to abstract ideas, all of which tends to typify the human race as unique.

But there is a big difference. Living according to principle may turn out to be no more than simply living by the ideas one was raised with. These ideas may be good or bad. They are typically defended by saying, “That’s how I was raised.” You might be raised to do random acts of kindness, which is good, but you also might be raised to believe that people should be segregated by race, or that one race is better than another.

Living by a philosophy almost always implies that — right or wrong — you’ve put some thought into the first principles that drive your behavior and that you can at least make some argument, or give some reason, for why you believe as you do. It’s implied you can make some kind of intellectual defense, even if it’s half-baked.

Remember that philosophy is a Greek word meaning “love of wisdom.” Note carefuly the etymology; not just being wise, but rather the love of wisdom… which implies that even if you end up with false ideas (as most philosophers probably do) you’ve dedicated your mind, as Plato and Socrates did before you, to relentlessly searching for Truth throughout your life. And your current philosophy is the very best you’ve been able to come up with so far.

To a true philosopher, “I was raised that way” can never, ever, ever be a valid defense of any idea. Since ancient Greek times, philsophers have debated what IS a valid basis for first principles, and sometimes ended as total sketpics; but most have searched stubbornly for first principles that are in harmony with Reason rather than oppopsed to it.

So — you can have a liberal political philosophy or conservative political philosophy… but as long as you can defend it without saying, “that’s just how I was raised,” you are still worthy of being called a philosopher.

26. what is principle reason in philosophy


Principles of Philosophy (Latin: Principia philosophiae) is a book by René Descartes. In essence it is a synthesis of the Discourse on Method and Meditations on First Philosophy It was written in Latin, published in 1644 and dedicated to Elisabeth of Bohemia, with whom Descartes had a long-standing friendship. A French version (Les Principes de la Philosophie) followed in 1647. It set forth the principles of nature—the Laws of Physics—as Descartes viewed them. Most notably, it set forth the principle that in the absence of external forces, an object's motion will be uniform and in a straight line. Newton borrowed this principle from Descartes and included it in his own Principia; to this day, it is still generally referred to as Newton's First Law of Motion. The book was primarily intended to replace the Aristotelian curriculum then used in French and British universities. The work provides a systematic statement of his metaphysics and natural philosophy, and represents the first truly comprehensive, mechanistic account of the universe.
