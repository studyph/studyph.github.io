1. When a rocket blasts off, is it the fiery exhaust gases "pushing against" the launch pad that cause the rocket to lift off?​


Answer:

No, it is not the fiery exhaust gases pushing against the launch pad that cause the rocket to lift off. Rather, it is the force exerted by the exhaust gases in the opposite direction to the rocket, as described by Newton's Third Law of Motion: for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. As the rocket's engines burn fuel and release high-pressure gas out of nozzles at the bottom of the rocket, the gas exerts a force in the opposite direction to the rocket, propelling it upward. This force is known as thrust, and it overcomes the force of gravity, allowing the rocket to lift off. The launch pad is designed to withstand the immense heat and force of the rocket's engines and prevent damage to the surrounding area.

27. Topic: Force, Motion, and Energy Question 1: A rocket has a mass of 1,400 kg. It is launched with an initial thrust of 30,000 N. The air resistance at launch is negligible. Calculate the weight of the rocket in newtons. Question 2: Calculate the magnitude of the acceleration of the rocket, at the launch.


Question 1.

To find the weight of the rocket, we will just simply multiply its mass by g, which is 9.8 m/s^2.

1400 kg * 9.8 m/s^2 = 13720 kg * m/s^2

The weight of the rocket is 13720 N

Question 2.

To find the acceleration of the rocket upon launch, we will be using the formula F = ma.

F = ma

a = F / m

a = 30000 kg*m/s^2 / 1400 kg

a = 21.43 m/s^2

The acceleration of the rocket upon launch is 21.43 m/s^2
