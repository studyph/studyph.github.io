6. A. Chlorophyll

CHLOROPHYLL

Chlorophyll is not a mineral. It is a pigment that is found in plants, algae, and some bacteria and is responsible for the green color of plants. Chlorophyll is a complex molecule that is essential for photosynthesis, the process by which plants convert sunlight into energy. Although chlorophyll is not a mineral, it is essential for the health of plants and for the health of many ecosystems.

7. C. Natural resources

NATURAL RESOURCES

Many natural resources can be renewed, but not all of them can. Renewable natural resources are those that can be replenished over time through natural processes, such as the growth of plants or the reproduction of animals. Examples of renewable natural resources include water, timber, and crops. On the other hand, non-renewable natural resources are those that cannot be easily replenished and will eventually run out, such as oil, coal, and minerals.

The reason why some natural resources can be renewed is that they are replenished through natural processes, such as the growth of plants or the reproduction of animals. For example, trees can be cut down for timber, but they can also be replanted, allowing for a sustainable supply of wood. Similarly, crops can be harvested, but they can also be replanted, allowing for a continuous supply of food. Renewable natural resources are important because they can provide a sustainable source of resources for human use, without depleting the Earth's natural reserves.

8. C. Fossil fuels

FOSSIL FUELS

Natural resources such as coal, oil, and natural gas are also known as fossil fuels. Fossil fuels are formed over millions of years through the process of organic decay, in which the remains of plants and animals are gradually converted into energy-rich substances. These substances can be extracted and burned to produce energy, which is why they are known as fossil fuels. Fossil fuels are non-renewable natural resources, which means that they are not easily replenished and will eventually run out. Therefore, it is important to use fossil fuels responsibly and to develop alternative sources of energy to reduce our dependence on them.

9. A. Air and Water

AIR AND WATER

Air and water are essential for living, as they are necessary for the survival and well-being of all living organisms. All living things need air to breathe, and water to drink, in order to sustain their lives. In addition to providing the basic necessities of life, air and water also play important roles in maintaining the health and balance of ecosystems. For example, air is necessary for the process of photosynthesis, which is how plants convert sunlight into energy. Water is also necessary for many chemical reactions and processes in the body, such as digestion and metabolism. Overall, air and water are essential for life on Earth, and their importance cannot be overstated.

10. A. Cucumber

CUCUMBER

An example of a living natural resource is a cucumber. Natural resources are materials or substances that are found in nature and can be used by humans. Some natural resources, such as gold, iron, and stone, are non-living, meaning they do not have the ability to grow, reproduce, or adapt to their environment. Other natural resources, such as plants and animals, are living, meaning they have the ability to grow, reproduce, and adapt to their environment. The cucumber is an example of a living natural resource, as it is a plant that can grow, reproduce, and adapt to its environment.
