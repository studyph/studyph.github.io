11. Zn + CuSO4 = ZnSO4 + Cu If 94.04 grams of cuso4 are reacted with 0.0333 moles of Zn. How many grams of cu will be produced.


2,131 gr  of Cu will be produced

Further explanation

This is a single displacement reaction where Zn can reduce Cu

To find the mass of Cu produced, we first determine the number of moles. The number of Cu moles is based on the limiting reactant mole.

Because here we know the mass of CuSO₄ and Zn mole, we first determine the CuSO₄ mole and compare which one has the smallest ratio (the ratio between mole and reaction coefficient)

molar mass of CuSO₄ = 160 g / mol

mole CuSO₄ = mass: molar mass

mole of CuSO₄ = 94.04: 160

mole of CuSO₄ = 0.588

Because of the comparison of the Zn and CuSO₄ reaction coefficients in the equation above 1: 1, the smallest ratio (mole: reaction coefficient) is owned by Zn (0.0333 versus 0.588), so Zn functions as a limiting reactant

Then mole Cu = mole Zn = 0.0333

mass of Cu = mol x Ar Cu

mass of Cu = 0.0333 x 64 = 2.131 gr

24. how many moles of CuSO4 are required to react with 13.5g of Al?​


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Balance the chemical equation.

2Al + 3CuSO₄ → Al₂(SO₄)₃ + 3Cu

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of CuSO₄ required to react with Al using dimensional analysis.

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 2 moles of Al is stoichiometrically equivalent to 3 moles of CuSO₄.

The molar mass of Al is 26.98 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{CuSO}_4 & = \text{13.5 g Al} \times \frac{\text{1 mol Al}}{\text{26.98 g Al}} \times \frac{\text{3 mol} \: \text{CuSO}_4}{\text{2 mol Al}} \\ & = \text{0.750556 mol} \\ & \approx \boxed{\text{0.751 mol}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 0.751 mol of CuSO₄ is required to react with 13.5 g of Al.

27. 100 g of CuSO4·5H2O to ions


Answer:

CuSO4·5H2O is copper (II) sulfate pentahydrate. When 100 g of CuSO4·5H2O is dissolved in water, it will dissociate into its constituent ions as follows:

CuSO4·5H2O → Cu2+ + SO4^2- + 5H2O

This means that one mole of copper (II) sulfate pentahydrate will yield one mole of copper ions (Cu2+), one mole of sulfate ions (SO4^2-), and five moles of water molecules (5H2O). To determine the number of moles of copper (II) sulfate pentahydrate in 100 g, we need to know its molar mass. The molar mass of CuSO4·5H2O is:

(1 x 63.55 g/mol for Cu) + (1 x 32.06 g/mol for S) + (4 x 16.00 g/mol for O) + (5 x 18.02 g/mol for H2O) = 249.68 g/mol

Therefore, 100 g of CuSO4·5H2O is equal to 100 g / 249.68 g/mol = 0.4002 moles. This means that when 0.4002 moles of CuSO4·5H2O is dissolved in water, it will yield 0.4002 moles of Cu2+ ions, 0.4002 moles of SO4^2- ions, and 2.001 moles of water molecules.
