Plato was a student of Socrates who used the character of Socrates in his works to develop his philosophy. One of his most famous ideas is the idea of Forms. He says that things in this world are imperfect versions of the ideal forms. There is an ideal form of a horse in the realm of the forms, etc. He emphasized the One - the form of goodness (the True, the Good, the Beautiful) (see the allegory of the cave). One of his most famous works is the Republic which is his view of an ideal society.

Aristotle was a student of Plato who disagreed with him in terms of the forms. Aristotle believed that the forms existed in real things rather than another realm. All material things are a composite of form and matter (hylomorphism). The form informs the matter. Matter itself cannot exist on its own but must exist with a form (by matter what is meant is the principle of potentiality in an object, not the modern definition). Aristotle dealt a lot with change. How can things remain the same despite them changing? Aristotle says that things are composites of potentiality and actuality. Aristotle also wrote a lot about biology, virtue (the "golden mean"), politics, physics, morality, etc. Along with Plato, the two laid the cornerstones of Western philosophy and opened up later schools of thought like Scholasticism and Neo-Platonism.

22. explain the 100 words of philosophy aristotle in the understanding self?


Explanation:

Aristotle is a towering figure in ancient Greek philosophy, who made important contributions to logic, criticism, rhetoric, physics, biology, psychology, mathematics, metaphysics, ethics, and politics. He was a student of Plato for twenty years but is famous for rejecting Plato’s theory of forms. He was more empirically minded than Plato and Plato’s teacher, Socrates.

A prolific writer, lecturer, and polymath, Aristotle radically transformed most of the topics he investigated. In his lifetime, he wrote dialogues and as many as 200 treatises, of which only 31 survive. These works are in the form of lecture notes and draft manuscripts never intended for general readership. Nevertheless, they are the earliest complete philosophical treatises we still possess.

As the father of western logic, Aristotle was the first to develop a formal system for reasoning. He observed that the deductive validity of any argument can be determined by its structure rather than its content, for example, in the syllogism: All men are mortal; Socrates is a man; therefore, Socrates is mortal. Even if the content of the argument were changed from being about Socrates to being about someone else, because of its structure, as long as the premises are true, then the conclusion must also be true. Aristotelian logic dominated until the rise of modern propositional logic and predicate logic 2000 years later.
