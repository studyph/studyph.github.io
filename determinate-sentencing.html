If the sentence already makes proper use of the underlined determiner, write C in front of the number. 10. This documents in my hands will determine the outcome of this election.


Answer:

The answer is these. The use of the determiner “this” is incorrect because it does not match the noun, “documents,” a plural noun that requires a plural determiner. The determiner “these” is more appropriate as it is used to modify plural nouns like this:

These documents in my hands…

Demonstratives

Demonstratives are determiners that demonstrate the distance or location of an object, person, or event from the speaker. This can refer to both physical and abstract proximity. The demonstratives this and these are both used to something close to the speaker. Here are examples:

This dress is for my 18th birthday.  These apples are tasty.  

We use this for singular nouns like “dress” or uncountable nouns like “sugar,” whereas we use these for plural countable nouns like the following:

These girls These people These sheets of paper These files

If the sentence already makes proper use of the underlined determiner, write C in front of the number. 1. A history of the Philippines was at first written by Americans.


Answer:

The answer is C. The use of the determiner “a” is correct because the word “history” starts with the consonant “h” that sounds like “he” instead of a vowel sound. Use the determiner “a” if a word starts with a vowel (a, e, i, o, u) or a vowel sound (an artist, an urban city).

Explanation:

When to Use “A” and “An”  

Most of the time, words that begin with an h also have an h-sound. These words can start with a “ha,” “he,” “ho,” “hi”, or “hu” sounds, and they require the determiner “a.” Here are examples:

A house /ha/ A hip  A history A hotel

However, there are cases when a word starting with h has a vowel sound instead of a consonant. These words require the determiner “an.” Here are examples:

An hour  An honest An heir An honorable

Here, the h is silent. In the case of the sentence above, “history” has a consonant sound and requires the determiner “a.”

Answer:

The answer is C. The use of the determiner “his” is correct because this pronoun properly represents the subject Mario, a man’s name. The pronoun “his” is an example of a possessive determiner placed before nouns to indicate who or what possesses/owns something. Mario owns the wallet, so we use the male pronoun ‘his.’

Explanation:

What Are Determiners?

Determiners are used in two primary ways: to refer and to quantify. To refer means that the determiner shows who or what we are talking about. In this case, the determiner “his” is used to refer to who owns the wallet, Mario.

The pronoun “his” is also used to avoid repeating Mario’s name and create more diversity in the sentence. Here are examples:

The police arrested the suspect. They inspected his bag searching for the stolen phone. Anthony wants his friends to be cool like him.

If the sentence already makes proper use of the underlined determiner, write C in front of the number. 5. Due to a President‘s motorcade, we were stuck in traffic for two hours.


Answer:

The answer is the. The use of the determiner a is incorrect because the capital letter in “President” suggests that it is referring to one specific person. The determiner a is used for an unspecific noun, but “President” here is a specific noun that requires the as determiner.

Due to the President‘s motorcade…

Explanation:

We use a when the noun is not specific. Here’s an example:

A woman knocked on our door at midnight.

This identifies the gender of the person but not her identity. This woman could be anyone. Now, compare this with this sentence:

The woman who invented the self-wringing mop will be honored today.

Here, the determiner the is used because it’s referring to one specific woman. Even though her name is not given, she is identified as the one who invented a special mop. It’s not just any woman.

The same applies to the sentence above, it's not just any president of any country. It is the president of that particular country where the speaker/writer comes from.

28. Determine whether the sentences are using the underlined determiners correctly. If the sentence already makes proper use of the underlined determiner, write C in front of the number. 9. People should keep his noses in their own business.


Answer:

The answer is their. The use of the determiner “his” is incorrect because the subject of the sentence, “people,” is a plural noun and requires a plural determiner. The determiner “their” is an example of a possessive determiner that tells us that “noses” belong to “people.”  

   People should keep their noses... Explanation: Possessive Determiner

A possessive determiner tells us who or what possesses or owns something. Here are examples:

My Your His Her Its Our Their

Here’s the sentence again:

People should keep his noses in their own business. (Incorrect) People should keep their noses in their own business. (Correct)

The possessive determiner “his” is used for a singular noun like “a man” or “Anthony.” Here’s an example:

Anthony was told not to keep his nose in other people’s business.

As such, it does not go well with the plural noun “people,” which is the noun that “possesses” those noses.
