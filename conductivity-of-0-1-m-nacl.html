1. What is the mass of nacl in a 1 l bottle of 2.00 m nacl?


73 g

Explanation:

The molecular weight of NaCl is 58.44 g/mol

So, one mole of NaCl weighs 58.44 g.

A 2.5 M solution is 2.5 moles per liter (Molarity is just the number of moles per liter).

Therefore, 0.5 L would contain 1.25 mol. Hence, you would need 1.25 × 58.44 g = 73 g.

6. How many liters of 0.075 M NaCl solution can you prepare from 450 mL of 9.0 M NaCl?


[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & M_{\text{i}} = 9.0 \: M \\ & M_{\text{f}} = 0.075 \: M \\ & V_{\text{i}} = \text{450 mL = 0.450 L} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Final Step: Calculate the final volume of the solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} V_{\text{f}} & = \frac{M_{\text{i}}V_{\text{i}}}{M_{\text{f}}} \\ & = \frac{(9.0 \: M)(\text{0.450 L})}{0.075 \: M} \\ & = \boxed{\text{54 L}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 54 L of 0.075 M NaCl solution can be prepared from 450 mL of 9.0 M NaCl.

9. How many grams of NaCl is required to prepare 200 mL of a solution of 1 M NaCl? ( Molar mass of NaCl=58.44 g/mol). a. 11.688 g b. 11.69 g c. 11.6 g d. 12 g


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & M = 1 \: M = \text{1 mol/L} \\ & V_{\text{solution}} = \text{200 mL = 0.2 L} \\ & MM_{\text{solute}} = \text{58.44 g/mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solute}} & = (M)(V_{\text{solution}}) \\ & = \text{(1 mol/L)(0.2 L)} \\ & = \text{0.2 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mass of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} mass_{\text{solute}} & = (n_{\text{solute}})(MM_{\text{solute}}) \\ & = \text{(0.2 mol)(58.44 g/mol)} \\ & = \text{11.688 g} \\ & \approx \boxed{\text{12 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the answer is d. 12 g (rounded to two significant figures).

11. What mass (in grams) of solid NaCl is needed to prepare 5L of 0.65 M NaCl solution? Input the numerical value only. Exclude the unit.


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Multiply the molarity by the volume of solution to get the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of solute} & = \text{molarity} \times \text{volume of solution} \\ & = 0.65 \: M \times \text{5 L} \\ & = \text{0.65 mol/L} \times \text{5 L} \\ & = \text{3.25 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the mass of solute (NaCl).

The molar mass of NaCl is 58.44 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of solute} & = \text{moles of solute} \times \text{molar mass of solute} \\ & = \text{3.25 mol} \times \text{58.44 g/mol} \\ & = \boxed{\text{190 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the mass of solid NaCl needed to prepare 5 L of 0.65 M NaCl solution is 190 g.

13. 1. What are [Na+], [Cl-] and [K+] in a solution containing 0.10 M each of NaCl and KCI?​


Answer:

In a solution containing 0.10 M NaCl and 0.10 M KCl, the ions present are Na+, Cl-, and K+.

Since NaCl and KCl dissociate completely in water, the concentrations of their ions are equal to their initial concentrations:

[Na+] = 0.10 M

[Cl-] = 0.10 M

[K+] = 0.10 M

Therefore, the concentrations of Na+, Cl-, and K+ in the solution are all 0.10 M

16. 3. A solution of NaCl in 250 grams of water boils at 102. 04 0 C (NaCl=58 g/mole) a. What is the concentration of the solution? b. How many grams of NaCl are present in the solution? ​


Given:

mass of solvent = 250 g = 0.250 kg

molar mass of solute = 58 g/mol

[tex]\text{BP}_{solution} = \text{102.04°C}[/tex]

solute: NaCl

solvent: water

Required:

a) molality

b) mass of solute

Solution: (a)

Step 1: Calculate the boiling point elevation.

Note: The boiling point of water is 100°C.

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = \text{BP}_{solution} - \text{BP}_{solvent}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = \text{102.04°C} - \text{100°C}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = \text{2.04°C}[/tex]

Step 2: Determine the van't Hoff factor.

Since NaCl is an electrolyte, we will assume that NaCl is dissociated completely into one sodium ion and one chloride ion. Therefore, there are two ions in a formula unit of NaCl. This means that,

[tex]i = 2[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molality of solution.

Note: The value of Kb for water is 0.512°C/molal.

[tex]\text{molality} = \frac{\Delta T_{\text{b}}}{iK_{\text{b}}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molality} = \frac{\text{2.04°C}}{(2)(\text{0.512°C/molal})}[/tex]

molality = 1.9922 molal

[tex]\boxed{\text{molality = 1.99 molal}}[/tex]

Solution: (b)

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

Note: 1 molal = 1 mol/kg

moles of solute = molality × mass of solvent

moles of solute = 1.9922 mol/kg × 0.250 kg

moles of solute = 0.49805 mol

Step 2: Calculate the mass of solute.

mass of solute = moles of solute × molar mass of solute

mass of solute = 0.49805 mol × 58 g/mol

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass of solute = 28.9 g}}[/tex]

17. Explain why solid Nacl doesn't conduct electricity


Answer:

Solid sodium chloride does not conduct electricity, because there are no electrons which are free to move. When it melts, sodium chloride undergoes electrolysis, which involves conduction of electricity because of the movement and discharge of the ions.

21. Typical blood serum is about 0.14 M NaCl. What volume of blood contains 1.0 mg NaCl?​


Explanation:

molar mass NaCI = 58.44 g/mol

0.14 M NaCl means 0.14 moles of NaCl in 1 Liter of blood. Let's convert this to mg NaCl per ml of blood.

0.14 mol/L x 58.44 g/mol x 1000 mg/g x 1 L/1000 ml = 8.2 mg/ml

So, there would be 8.2 mg of NaCl in 1 ml of blood. To get 1.0 mg of NaCl we would need

8.2 mg/ml (x ml) = 1.0 mg

x = 0.12 mls of blood needed
