9. Students sign up to be subjects in a psychology experiment. When they arrive, they are told that interviews are running late and are taken to a waiting room. The experimenters then stage a theft of a valuable object left in the waiting room. Some subjects are alone with the thief, and others are in pairs—these are the treatments being compared and the researcher's question is whether the subject report the theft or not. The students had agreed to take part in an unspecified study, and the true nature of the experiment was explained to them afterward. Does this meet the requirement of informed consent?​


Answer:

The experiment does not meet the requirement of informed consent because the students were not fully informed about the nature of the study and were not given the opportunity to decline participation in the theft scenario. Additionally, the experiment involved deception, which raises ethical concerns.

Explanation:

No, this does not meet the requirement of informed consent. Informed consent is a fundamental ethical principle in research that requires that individuals be fully informed about the nature and purpose of the study, as well as any potential risks or benefits, before they agree to participate.

In this scenario, the students were not fully informed about the nature and purpose of the study, as they were told that they were participating in an unspecified study. Additionally, they were not informed about the true nature of the experiment until after it was over.

Furthermore, the experiment involved deception, as the students were not told that a theft was going to be staged in the waiting room. Deception can be ethically justifiable in some cases, but it requires that participants be debriefed afterward and that any potential harm be minimized.

The lack of full disclosure and the use of deception in this experiment means that the students did not provide informed consent. This raises ethical concerns about the study and the treatment of the participants.
