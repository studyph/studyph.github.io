The main difference between consumer and organizational buyers is the purpose of their purchases. Consumer buyers are individuals or households that buy goods and services for personal use, while organizational buyers are businesses, governments, and institutions that buy goods and services for their operations or resale. Consumer buyers tend to make purchase decisions based on personal preferences, emotions, and perceptions, while organizational buyers focus on criteria such as price, quality, reliability, and supplier relationships. For example, a consumer buyer may purchase a new phone based on its brand reputation or features, while an organizational buyer may purchase new computers based on their compatibility with existing software and hardware systems.

The Blackwell, Miniard, and Engel model identifies five roles of who buys in the consumer and organizational buying processes: initiator, influencer, decider, buyer, and user. For example, in the consumer buying process for a new car, the initiator could be the person who first expresses the need or desire for a new car, the influencer could be a family member or friend who provides advice or recommendations, the decider could be the person who makes the final decision on the car model and features, the buyer could be the person who negotiates the price and purchase terms, and the user could be the person who actually drives and uses the car. In the organizational buying process for new software, the initiator could be an IT manager who identifies the need for better software, the influencer could be a consultant or a vendor who recommends a specific solution, the decider could be a procurement or finance manager who approves the purchase, the buyer could be a purchasing agent who negotiates the contract and terms, and the user could be the employees who use the software in their work.

Several factors can affect the consumer decision-making process, including personal factors (e.g., age, income, lifestyle, personality), psychological factors (e.g., motivation, perception, learning, beliefs, attitudes), social factors (e.g., family, culture, reference groups, social class), and situational factors (e.g., time, place, occasion, urgency). For example, a consumer may decide to purchase a luxury watch based on their self-image and social status, their perception of the watch's quality and brand reputation, their cultural values and traditions, and the occasion or purpose of the purchase (e.g., a gift for a special occasion).
