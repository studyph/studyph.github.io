1. A vacuum gauge mounted on a condenser reads 0.66 m hg. what is the absolute pressure in the condenser in kpa when the atmospheric pressure is 101.3 kpa


Pressure is the perpendicular force exerted on a surface per unit area. Furthermore, there are three kinds of pressure: atmospheric pressure, gauge pressure and absolute pressure.

Atmospheric pressure is the already existing pressure within our surroundings.

Gauge pressure is the change of pressure of the atmosphere around the sensor. Take into account that the gauge sensor will refer to the existing pressure around it as 0, so it will read 1 atom of pressure inside the gauge as 0 if the pressure outside is 1 atom as well. When the pressure became 3 atoms while the outside is 1 atom the sensor will show you a pressure of 2 atoms.

Absolute pressure is the pressure measured against a vacuum, so it is the sum of the preexisting pressure in the atmosphere and the change in the pressure measured by the gauge sensor.


Understanding these concepts will us the following formula:

Absolute Pressure = Atmospheric Pressure + Gauge Pressure

Gauge Pressure =  Absolute Pressure - Atmospheric Pressure 


Going back, the problem has the following given:

Gauge Pressure = 0.66 mmHg

Atmospheric Pressure = 101.3 kpa

Absolute Pressure = x kpa


This means that the unknown value is the absolute pressure inside the condenser.


Looking at the given we notice that they have different units. Since the unknown value has a unit of kpa we will convert the other unit (mmHg) as kpa.


Conversion (mmHg to kpa):

1 mmHg = 0.133322 kpa


Computation for Gauge Pressure:

[tex]Gauge Pressure = 0.66 mmHg * \frac{0.133322 kpa}{1 mmHg} [/tex] 

[tex]Gauge Pressure = 0.66 * \frac{0.133322 kpa}{1} [/tex]

[tex]Gauge Pressure = 0.087992 kpa[/tex]


Therefore, the new given for the problem are:

Gauge Pressure = 0.087992 kpa

Atmospheric Pressure = 101.3 kpa

Absolute Pressure = x kpa


With all given having the same unit we can now compute for the unknown value, i.e. absolute pressure.


Computation: 

[tex]Absolute Pressure = Gauge Pressure + Atmospheric Pressure[/tex]

[tex]Absolute Pressure = 0.087992 kpa + 101.3 kpa[/tex]
[tex]Absolute Pressure = 101.387992 kpa[/tex]

This means that the absolute pressure inside the condenser is 101.387992 kpa or approximately 101.3880 kpa! That should be the answer.

[tex]∆T[/tex]

[tex]\frac{V_{1}}{T_{1}} = \frac{V_{2}}{T_{2}}[/tex]

[tex]∆T = -\text{97.72 K}[/tex]

8. A boiler installed where the atmospheric pressure is 752 mm hg has a pressure reading of 12 kg/cm^2. what is the absolute pressure in kpa?


The absolute pressure : 1277.056 KPa

Further explanation

Boiler is a device used to produce steam which is then used for various needs for example for electricity generation

Inside the boiler experiences pressure due to the heating (from burner) of the water inside

Absolute pressure is the total pressure resulting from the sum of the pressures in the boiler with atmospheric (air) pressure. Pressure shown on  the boiler (pressure gauge) is the pressure inside the boiler

Can be formulated:

P gauge = P absolute  - P atmosphere

P Absolute  = P gauge +  P  atmosphere

P atmospheric standard is valued at 1 atm = 1.01 × 10⁵ Pa.

A boiler installed where the atmospheric pressure is 752 mm Hg has a pressure reading of 12 kg/cm².

So :

P atm = 752 mmHg = 752 x 0,133322 KPa =100.258 KPa

Pressure reading = P gauge =  12 kg/cm² = 12 x 98,0665 KPa = 1176.798 KPa

Then

P absolute = P gauge + P atm

21. Air enters a nozzle steadily at 280 kpa and 77°c with a velocity of 50 m/s and exits at 85 kpa, 320 m/s. the heat losses from the nozzle to the surroundings at 20°c are estimated to be 3.2 kj/kg


Answer:

The question are determine

(a) the exit temperature = 24°C

(b) the total entropy generated for this process = 188.04J/Kkg

Explanation:

How to solve it?

(a) Supposing ideal gas behavior, the specific enthalpy of air is given by

    h = cp∙T

Specific heat capacity of dry air at 40° is [1]:

cp = 1.005 kJ/kgK

Solve the final temperature is

T₂ = T₁ + (q - ([tex]\frac{1}{2}[/tex]) ∙ (v₂² - v₁² ))/cp

= 77°C + (3200J/kg - ([tex]\frac{1}{2}[/tex]) ∙ ((320m/s)² - (50m/s)² )) / 1005J/kgK

= 77°C - 53K

= 24°C

(b) The whole entropy change is the sum of the surrounding and entropy change of the gas.

∆s_sur = -q / T_sur

= 3200J/kg / (20 + 273)K

= 10.92J/K

The entropy change of an ideal gas that is under change of state from

T₁,V₁ to T₂,V₂ is to ∆S = n∙Cv∙ln([tex]\frac{T₂}{T₁}[/tex]) + n∙R∙ln([tex]\frac{V₂}{V₁}[/tex])

In terms of specific properties

∆S = m∙cp∙ln([tex]\frac{T₂}{T₁}[/tex]) - m∙R'∙ln([tex]\frac{p₂}{p₁}[/tex]) or in other words,

∆s = cp∙ln([tex]\frac{T₂}{T₁}[/tex]) - R'∙ln([tex]\frac{p₂}{p₁}[/tex])

R' is the specific gas constant of air, it is:

R' = [tex]\frac{R}{M}[/tex] = 287J/kgK

The specific entropy change of the air flowing through the nozzle is:

∆s air = 1005kJ/kgK ∙ ln((24 +273)/(77+273)) - 287J/kgK∙ln([tex]\frac{85}{280}[/tex])

= 177.12J/kgK

Total entrophy is = 10.92J/K + 177.12J/kgK = 188.04J/Kkg
