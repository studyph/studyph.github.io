21. Define the following Geometric terminologies 1.If-then Statement 2.Hypothesis 3.Conclusion 4.Truth Value 5.Converse Statement 6.Counterexample 7.Biconditional 8.Deductive Reasoning 9.Inductive Reasoning


Answer:If-then Statement: An If-then statement is a mathematical statement that consists of two parts: the hypothesis and the conclusion. The hypothesis is a statement that provides a condition, and the conclusion is a statement that follows if the condition is met. For example, "If a number is even, then it is divisible by 2."

Hypothesis: The hypothesis is the first part of an If-then statement that provides a condition or assumption. It is the part that comes after the word "if." For example, in the statement "If a triangle has three sides of equal length, then it is equilateral," the hypothesis is "a triangle has three sides of equal length."

Conclusion: The conclusion is the second part of an If-then statement that follows the condition provided by the hypothesis. It is the part that comes after the word "then." For example, in the statement "If a triangle has three sides of equal length, then it is equilateral," the conclusion is "it is equilateral."

Truth Value: The truth value of a mathematical statement is a value that indicates whether the statement is true or false. For example, the statement "5 + 7 = 12" has a truth value of true, while the statement "2 + 2 = 5" has a truth value of false.

Converse Statement: The converse statement of an If-then statement is obtained by interchanging the hypothesis and the conclusion. For example, the converse of "If a triangle has three sides of equal length, then it is equilateral" is "If a triangle is equilateral, then it has three sides of equal length."

Counterexample: A counterexample is an example that shows that a statement is false. It is a specific case that does not satisfy the condition provided by the statement. For example, the statement "All prime numbers are odd" is false, and 2 is a counterexample to this statement, as it is a prime number but is not odd.

Biconditional: A biconditional statement is a mathematical statement that consists of two parts connected by the phrase "if and only if." It asserts that the two parts are equivalent and true at the same time. For example, "A triangle is equilateral if and only if it has three sides of equal length."

Deductive Reasoning: Deductive reasoning is a type of reasoning that involves starting with general premises and using logical steps to arrive at a specific conclusion. It is a top-down approach to reasoning that is used in mathematical proofs, where the conclusion is derived from previously established axioms or theorems.

Inductive Reasoning: Inductive reasoning is a type of reasoning that involves making generalizations based on specific observations or patterns. It is a bottom-up approach to reasoning that is used in scientific investigations and data analysis, where specific observations or experiments are used to make generalizations about larger populations or phenomena.

29. Explain the meaning of the following terminology. Why did they affect in India? Terminologies -Rowlatt Act - Amritsar Massacre -Satyagraha -Salt March Effect ( explain each terminologies )


Answer:

1. Rowlatt Act: The Rowlatt Act was a law passed by the British in India in 1919 that authorized the government to imprison anyone without trial or conviction for up to two years. It also allowed the British to ban public meetings and censor the press. This act was highly controversial, and it led to widespread protests and demonstrations across India.

2. Amritsar Massacre: The Amritsar Massacre was a tragic event that occurred on April 13, 1919, in the city of Amritsar, Punjab. British troops opened fire on a peaceful gathering of thousands of unarmed Indians who were protesting against the Rowlatt Act. The massacre left hundreds of people dead and wounded. This event had a significant impact on the Indian independence movement, as it increased the demand for self-rule and inspired many Indians to join the struggle for independence.

3. Satyagraha: Satyagraha is a philosophy of nonviolent resistance developed by Mahatma Gandhi. The term means "insistence on truth" and Gandhi believed in using nonviolent means to resist injustice and oppression. Satyagraha played a significant role in the Indian independence movement as it helped to mobilize large numbers of people, and it inspired other nonviolent movements around the world.

4. Salt March: The Salt March was a nonviolent protest organized by Mahatma Gandhi in 1930. The march was a response to the British monopoly on salt, which prevented Indians from making their own salt. Gandhi and a group of followers walked more than 240 miles from Sabarmati Ashram to the Arabian Sea to collect salt. The march received extensive media coverage and inspired Indians to join the independence movement. This event marked a significant turning point in the Indian independence struggle as it demonstrated the power of nonviolent resistance.

20 TERMINOLOGIES AND DEFINITION

a. Earthquake - a natural disaster caused by the shaking of the Earth's surface due to the movement of tectonic plates.

b. fault - a crack in the Earth's crust where two tectonic plates meet and move relative to each other.

c. typhoon - a tropical cyclone that forms in the western Pacific Ocean.

d. comet - a small, icy celestial body that releases gas and dust as it approaches the sun.

e. asteroid - a small, rocky celestial body that orbits the sun.

f. meteor - a small, rocky or metallic object that enters the Earth's atmosphere and burns up.

f. Richter scale - a logarithmic scale used to measure the magnitude of earthquakes.

g. seismic waves - waves of energy that travel through the Earth's crust and cause earthquakes.

h. plate tectonics - the scientific theory that explains the movement of the Earth's crustal plates and their interactions.

i. epicenter - the point on the Earth's surface directly above the focus of an earthquake.

j. aftershock - a smaller earthquake that follows the main shock of a larger earthquake.

k. fault line - the line that separates two tectonic plates that are moving relative to each other.

l. subduction zone - a region where one tectonic plate is forced beneath another and sinks into the mantle.

m. volcanic eruption - the release of ash, gas, and magma from a volcano.

n. tsunamis - large ocean waves caused by earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or meteor impacts.

o. landslide - the movement of rock, soil, or debris down a slope due to gravity.

p. ground rupture - a crack in the Earth's surface caused by movement along a fault.

q. intensity - a measure of the shaking produced by an earthquake at a specific location.

r. magnitude - a measure of the size of an earthquake, usually expressed as the Richter magnitude scale.

FIVE QUESTIONS

a. Why do earthquakes happen?

Earthquakes happen due to the movement of tectonic plates and the release of stored energy in the Earth's crust.

b. How does plate tectonics contribute to earthquakes?

Plate tectonics contributes to earthquakes because it causes the movement of tectonic plates, which can create stress and strain in the Earth's crust that eventually leads to earthquakes.

c. Why are earthquakes more common in some areas than others?

Earthquakes are more common in some areas than others because they are caused by the movement of tectonic plates, and these plates are more active in some regions than others.

d. How are tsunamis generated by earthquakes?

Tsunamis are generated by earthquakes when the movement of tectonic plates causes the seafloor to rise or fall suddenly, creating large ocean waves.

e. How can we predict earthquakes?

Earthquakes can be predicted to some extent by monitoring the Earth's crust for changes in the level of stress and strain, and by studying the pattern of past earthquakes in a region.
