3. What is cause and effect of typhoon Yolanda​


Explanation:

Typhoon Yolanda, also known as Typhoon Haiyan, was one of the strongest tropical cyclones ever recorded. It struck the Philippines in November 2013 and caused widespread devastation, resulting in thousands of deaths and billions of dollars in damage.

The causes of Typhoon Yolanda are typical of a tropical cyclone. Warm ocean temperatures and low atmospheric pressure create a region of low pressure that can develop into a tropical storm. As the storm grows in intensity, it can become a typhoon. In the case of Yolanda, the typhoon was also affected by a weather phenomenon known as El Niño, which contributed to the storm's strength and intensity.

The effects of Typhoon Yolanda were catastrophic. The storm caused widespread destruction, particularly in the city of Tacloban, where thousands of people were killed or went missing. The storm surge that accompanied the typhoon, which was estimated to be up to six meters high in some areas, destroyed homes and other buildings, leaving many homeless. The typhoon also caused significant damage to infrastructure, including roads, bridges, and airports.

The aftermath of the typhoon was marked by a massive humanitarian response, with aid agencies and governments from around the world sending assistance to the affected areas. However, recovery efforts were hampered by the scale of the destruction, as well as the limited capacity of local authorities to respond to such a large-scale disaster.

EFFECT: damaged homes, agricultural and fishing businesses, and loss of life.

Answer:

Typhoon Yolanda, also known as Haiyan, was one of the deadliest typhoons to ever hit the Philippines. It made landfall on November 8, 2013, causing widespread devastation and loss of life. The disaster had many causes, and its effects were felt for years to come.

One of the main causes of Typhoon Yolanda was the unusually warm waters of the Pacific Ocean, which provided the energy needed to fuel the storm. As the typhoon approached the Philippines, it gained strength and became a category 5 super typhoon, with winds of up to 315 km/h. This extreme weather event caused massive destruction to infrastructure, homes, and businesses, and led to the loss of thousands of lives.

The effects of Typhoon Yolanda were catastrophic, with many areas left completely devastated. The storm surge, which was caused by the typhoon's strong winds and low pressure, reached up to six meters in some areas, causing widespread flooding and damage to homes and buildings. The typhoon also knocked out power and communication lines, making it difficult for people to call for help or receive assistance.

The aftermath of Typhoon Yolanda also had long-term effects on the Philippines. Many people lost their homes, businesses, and livelihoods, leading to a prolonged period of economic hardship. The disaster also highlighted the need for better disaster preparedness and response in the country, leading to improvements in infrastructure, emergency services, and community resilience.
