a. From the balanced equation, 1 mole of Fe₂O₃ reacts to produce 2 moles of Fe. So, the number of moles of Fe produced = (15 g Fe₂O₃) / (159.69 g/mol Fe₂O₃) x (2 mol Fe / 1 mol Fe₂O₃) = 0.188 moles

The molar mass of Fe is 55.85 g/mol. Therefore, the mass of Fe produced = 0.188 moles x 55.85 g/mol = 10.52 g

b. The balanced equation shows that 3 moles of CO react with 1 mole of Fe₂O₃ to produce 2 moles of Fe. Therefore, the number of moles of Fe produced from 8.4 g of CO = (8.4 g CO) / (28.01 g/mol CO) x (1 mol Fe / 3 mol CO) x (2 mol Fe / 1 mol Fe₂O₃) = 0.0257 moles

The mass of Fe produced from 8.4 g of CO = 0.0257 moles x 55.85 g/mol = 1.44 g

a. The balanced equation shows that 1 mole of Si reacts with 1 mole of C to produce 1 mole of SiC. So, the mole ratio of Si to C is 1:1.

b. From the balanced equation, 1 mole of Si reacts to produce 1 mole of SiC. So if 2 moles of Si are consumed, 2 moles of SiC are produced.

c. The molar mass of SiC is 40.10 g/mol. Therefore, the mass of SiC produced = (0.500 g Si) / (28.09 g/mol Si) x (1 mol SiC / 1 mol Si) x (40.10 g/mol SiC) = 0.715 g

3.

a. From the balanced equation, 1 mole of Si reacts with 2 moles of Cl₂ to produce 1 mole of SiCl₄.

The number of moles of Cl₂ used = 35 g / 70.9 g/mol = 0.494 moles

The number of moles of SiCl₄ produced = 0.494 moles / 2 = 0.247 moles

b. To find the limiting reactant, we need to calculate the number of moles of Si for 56 g of Si. The molar mass of Si is 28.09 g/mol, so the number of moles of Si used = 56 g / 28.09 g/mol = 1.99 moles

To react with 1.99 moles of Si, we need (1.99 moles Si) x (2 moles Cl₂ / 1 mole Si) = 3.98 moles of Cl₂. Since we only have 0.494 moles of Cl₂, it is the limiting reactant.

c. The number of moles of excess Si is (3.98 moles - 0.494 moles) / 1 = 3.49 moles.

4.

a. The balanced equation shows that 4 moles of HF react with 1 mole of Si to produce 1 mole of SiF₄ and 2 moles of H₂. So, the mole ratio of the reactants is 4:1.

b. The mole ratio of HF to SiF₄ is 4:1.

c. If 4 moles of Si were consumed in the reaction, 1 mole of SiF₄ was produced. Therefore, the number of moles of HF used = 4 moles Si x (4 moles HF / 1 mole Si) = 16 moles.

d. The molar mass of SiF₄ is 104.08 g/mol. Therefore, the mass of SiF₄ produced = (40 g HF) / (20.01 g/mol HF) x (1 mol SiF₄ / 4 mol HF) x (104.08 g/mol SiF₄) = 104 g

To determine the limiting reagent, we need to calculate the number of moles of each reactant and compare their mole ratio to the balanced chemical equation.

The molar mass of CS₂ is 76.14 g/mol. Therefore, the number of moles of CS₂ = 38 g / 76.14 g/mol = 0.499 moles.

The molar mass of CaO is 56.08 g/mol. Therefore, the number of moles of CaO = 40 g / 56.08 g/mol = 0.713 moles.

The mole ratio of CS₂ to CaO is 1:2 in the balanced equation. Therefore, there are not enough moles of CaO to react with all the moles of CS₂. CaO is the limiting reagent.

The excess reagent is CS₂. The number of moles of excess CS₂ = (0.499 moles CS₂) - (0.713 moles CaO x 1 mole CS₂ / 2 moles CaO) = 0.143 moles CS₂. The excess mass of CS₂ = 0.143 moles x 76.14 g/mol = 10.9 g.

9. 1. Silicon dioxide (quartz) is usually unreactive but reacts with hydrogen fluoride according to the equation below. SiO2(s)+4HF(g) SiF4(g) + 2H2O(l)Assume that 6.0 mol HF is added to 4.5 mol SiO2a. What is the limiting reactant?b. What is the excess reactant?c. What is the maximum amount of SiFi4, in grams, that can be produced?


a. SOLUTION:

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of SiF₄ formed by each reactant.

• Using HF

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 4 moles of HF is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mole of SiF₄

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{SiF}_4 & = \text{6.0 mol HF} \times \frac{\text{1 mol SiF}_4}{\text{4 mol HF}} \\ & = \text{1.5 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• Using SiO₂

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mole of SiO₂ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mole of SiF₄.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{SiF}_4 & = \text{4.5 mol} \: \text{SiO}_2 \times \frac{\text{1 mol SiF}_4}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{SiO}_2} \\ & = \text{4.5 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Determine the limiting reactant.

Since HF produced less amount of SiF₄ than that of SiO₂,

[tex]\boxed{\text{HF is the limiting reactant.}}[/tex]

-----------------------------------------------------

b. SOLUTION:

Since SiO₂ produced greater amount of SiF₄ than that of HF,

[tex]\boxed{\text{SiO}_2 \: \text{is the excess reactant.}}[/tex]

-----------------------------------------------------

c. SOLUTION:

The maximum mass of a product that can be formed is dictated by the limiting reactant. In this case, we will start at the number of moles of SiF₄ formed from the limiting reactant (HF) which is equal to 1.5 mol.

The molar mass of SiF₄ is 104.077 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of} \: \text{SiF}_4 & = \text{1.5 mol} \: \text{SiF}_4 \times \frac{\text{104.077 g} \: \text{SiF}_4}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{SiF}_4} \\ & = \text{156.116 g} \\ & \approx \boxed{\text{160 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the amount of SiF₄ that can be formed is 160 g.

19. 1. Silicon dioxide (quartz) is usually unreactive but reacts with hydrogen fluoride according to the equation below. SiO2(s)+4HF(g) SiF4(g) + 2H2O(l)Assume that 6.0 mol HF is added to 4.5 mol SiO2a. What is the limiting reactant?b. What is the excess reactant?c. What is the maximum amount of SiFi4, in grams, that can be produced?


Answer:

Limiting Reactant: 6.0 mol HF

Excess Reactant: 4.5 mol SiO2

Maximum amount of SiF4: 156 g

Explanation:

Ratio in the reaction is 1 mol SiO2 is to 4 mol HF. Since HF is greater in proportion, let's find how much of the SiO2 it needs.

Given 6 mol HF,

6 mol HF x (1 mol SiO2/4 mol HF) = 1.5 mol SiO2

We only need 1.5 mol SiO2, but we have 4.5 mol SiO2 in the given. Thus, SiO2 is the excess reactant.

To solve for the maximum amount of SiF4 in grams, we need to use the given limiting reactant and convert it to grams using its molar mass.

For molar mass, use periodic table:

Molar mass of SiF4 = 28 + (19)4 = 104 g/mol SiF4

Use to solve for the amount:

6 mol HF x (1 mol SiF4/4 molHF) x (104g SiF4/1 mol SiF4) = 156 g SiF4
