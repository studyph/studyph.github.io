6. What is the reason for the difference between the allocated cost computed using fifo-process costing and average-process costing? *


There are a few reasons for the difference between the allocated cost computed using FIFO-process costing (https://brainly.ph/question/16934814) and average-process costing (https://brainly.ph/question/16619586). The main reason is that different methods are used to compute the cost of inventory under each system. Under FIFO-process costing, the inventory cost is based on the order in which units are produced. The first units produced are assumed to be sold first, and the cost of these units is assigned to sales. On the other hand, average-process costing uses a weighted average method to compute the inventory cost. This means that all units in inventory are assigned a portion of the total cost of production, regardless of when they were produced. As a result, inventory cost is higher under average-process costing, and this higher cost is allocated to sales.

Another difference between the two methods is that FIFO-process costing requires more detailed record-keeping than average-process costing. In particular, FIFO-process costing requires companies to keep track of the cost of each batch of units produced. This information is then used to compute the inventory cost and allocate it to sales. On the other hand, average-process costing only requires companies to track the total cost of production for some time. As a result, FIFO-process costing is generally more expensive and time-consuming than average-process costing.

Finally, the difference between the two methods can also be attributed to how companies account for changes in the costs of materials and labor. Under FIFO-process costing, companies only consider the cost of materials and labor used to produce units sold during a period. This means that any changes in the cost of materials and labor that occur after the production of these units is complete are not reflected in the inventory cost. On the other hand, average-process costing uses a weighted average method to account for changes in the cost of materials and labor. This means that all changes in the cost of materials and labor are reflected in the inventory cost, regardless of when they occur. As a result, the cost of inventory is higher under average-process costing.

In summary, the main reasons for the difference between the allocated cost computed using FIFO-process costing and average-process costing are the different methods used to compute the cost of inventory and how companies account for changes in the cost of materials and labor.

11. What are the theories of cost allocation?


Answer:

•Insulating allocation scheme: The allocation base is chosen so that the costs allocated to one division do not depend on the operating performance of another

division. ▸ Example: Floor area or a fixed pre-determined rate.

•Noninsulating allocation scheme: The allocation base is chosen so that the costs allocated to one division does depend on the operating performance of another division.

> Example: Share of sales or costs of each division.

•Both schemes motivate mangers to reduce waste of common resources.
