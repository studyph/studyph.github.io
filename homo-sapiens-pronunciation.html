7. 1. Identify and differentiate the characteristics of the following hominids: Homo erectus, Homo sapiens and Homo sapiens sapiens. Explain the changes they have in biological, cultural and socio-political processes. ​


Answer:

Homo erectus, Homo sapiens, and Homo sapiens are three important hominid species that played a significant role in human evolution. Here are the characteristics that differentiate them:

Homo erectus:

Lived approximately 1.8 million to 200,000 years agoHad a larger brain and a more modern body plan than earlier hominidsWalked upright on two legs (bipedalism)Used stone tools and may have been the first hominid to control fireWas widespread, with populations found throughout Africa and AsiaMay have been the first hominid to leave Africa and colonize other parts of the world

Homo sapiens:

Lived approximately 300,000 to 12,000 years agoHad a larger brain than Homo erectus and more advanced cultural and social practicesWalked upright on two legs (bipedalism)Had a more sophisticated tool-making ability, including the production of more complex stone tools and the use of bone and antlerExhibited evidence of language, art, and symbolismFormed social groups and engaged in cooperative activities such as hunting and gatheringWas able to adapt to a wide range of environments, including extreme cold and arid regions

Homo sapiens sapiens (modern humans):

Emerged approximately 300,000 years ago and are the only surviving species of the Homo genusHave a larger brain and more advanced cultural and social practices than earlier Homo sapiensWalk upright on two legs (bipedalism)Have highly sophisticated tool-making abilities, including the ability to make complex tools and use agriculture to produce foodExhibit language, art, symbolism, and abstract thinkingForm complex social structures, including family units, tribes, and nationsHave developed sophisticated cultural practices, including religion, music, and literature

The changes these hominids have undergone in biological, cultural, and socio-political processes are significant. Biological changes include an increase in brain size and the evolution of bipedalism. Cultural changes include the development of language, art, and symbolism, as well as the ability to produce complex tools and adapt to a wide range of environments. Socio-political changes include the formation of social groups, the development of complex social structures, and the emergence of nation-states.

Overall, these hominid species represent significant milestones in human evolution, with each contributing to the development of the modern human species.

9. explain the process of evolution from Homo habilis up to Homo sapiens sapiens ​


Answer:

Human evolution, the process by which human beings developed on Earth from now-extinct primates. Viewed zoologically, we humans are Homo sapiens, a culture-bearing upright-walking species that lives on the ground and very likely first evolved in Africa about 315,000 years ago. We are now the only living members of what many zoologists refer to as the human tribe, Hominini, but there is abundant fossil evidence to indicate that we were preceded for millions of years by other hominins, such as Ardipithecus, Australopithecus, and other species of Homo, and that our species also lived for a time contemporaneously with at least one other member of our genus, H. neanderthalensis (the Neanderthals). In addition, we and our predecessors have always shared Earth with other apelike primates, from the modern-day gorilla to the long-extinct Dryopithecus. That we and the extinct hominins are somehow related and that we and the apes, both living and extinct, are also somehow related is accepted by anthropologists and biologists everywhere. Yet the exact nature of our evolutionary relationships has been the subject of debate and investigation since the great British naturalist Charles Darwin published his monumental books On the Origin of Species (1859) and The Descent of Man (1871). Darwin never claimed, as some of his Victorian contemporaries insisted he had, that “man was descended from the apes,” and modern scientists would view such a statement as a useless simplification—just as they would dismiss any popular notions that a certain extinct species is the “missing link” between humans and the apes. There is theoretically, however, a common ancestor that existed millions of years ago. This ancestral species does not constitute a “missing link” along a lineage but rather a node for divergence into separate lineages. This ancient primate has not been identified and may never be known with certainty, because fossil relationships are unclear even within the human lineage, which is more recent. In fact, the human “family tree” may be better described as a “family bush,” within which it is impossible to connect a full chronological series of species, leading to Homo sapiens, that experts can agree upon.

16. Characteristics of:Homo habilisHomo erectusHomo sapiens​


Homo habilis, one of the earliest members of the genus Homo, has a slightly larger braincase and smaller face and teeth than in Australopithecus or older hominin species. But it still retains some ape-like features, including long arms and a moderately- prognathic face.

Homo erectus had a brain size approaching that of modern humans. Averaging just under 1,000 cubic centimeters, this brain actually reached the lower limit of modern human brain size. Erectus was also the first human species to have a wide, fleshy nose. The face was flat and the skull had prominent ridges over the brow.

Homo sapiens ,Age is about 120,000 years old (but debated). This skull is transitional between Homo heidelbergensis and early modern Homo sapiens. It has a number of primitive features but also has some modern characteristics such as a reduced brow ridge and smaller facial features.
