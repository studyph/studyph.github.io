1.What are the types of organizational structures ? briefly define each

=Hierarchical org structure.

- A hierarchical organizational structure contains a direct chain of command from the top of the organization to the bottom. Senior management makes all critical decisions, which are then passed down through subsidiary levels of management.

Functional org structure.

- A functional organization is a common type of organizational structure in which the organization is divided into smaller groups based on specialized functional areas, such as IT, finance, or marketing.

Horizontal or flat org structure.

- A flat organization (also known as horizontal organization) has an organizational structure with few or no levels of middle management between staff and executives.Tall and flat organizations differ based on how many levels of management are present in the organization and how much control managers are endowed with.

Divisional org structures (market-based, product-based, geographic)

-The divisional structure is a type of organizational structure that groups each organizational function into a division. ... Each division contains all the necessary resources and functions within it to support that product line or geography.

Matrix org structure.

- A matrix organization is defined as one in which there is dual or multiple managerial accountability and responsibility. In a matrix there are usually two chains of command, one along functional lines and the other along project, product, or client lines.

Team-based org structure.

- Team-based organizations emphasize the value and importance of all employees regardless of their specific roles or the simplicity and complexity of the tasks that they perform. In this organizational structure, employees have more autonomy and more authority to make decisions, without needing managerial approval.

Network org structure

- The network structure is a newer type of organizational structure viewed as less hierarchical (i.e., more “flat”), more decentralized, and more flexible than other structures. In a network structure, managers coordinate and control relationships that are both internal and external to the firm.

13. How do traditional organizational designs compare with newer organizational structures?


Answer:

Explanation:

Traditional organizational designs are typically hierarchical in nature, with clear lines of authority and a top-down decision-making process. They are characterized by a clear division of labor, formal rules and procedures, and a focus on efficiency and control.

Newer organizational structures, on the other hand, tend to be more flexible, decentralized, and adaptive. They often emphasize collaboration, innovation, and a more horizontal decision-making process. They may also incorporate more self-organizing teams, cross-functional groups, and a focus on employee empowerment and engagement.

While traditional designs may provide a stable and predictable environment, they can also be slow to adapt to changing market conditions and customer needs. Newer structures, on the other hand, may be better suited to rapidly changing environments and uncertain markets, but may be less effective in situations that require a high degree of coordination and control.

Ultimately, the choice of organizational design depends on a variety of factors, including the organization's size, goals, culture, and external environment. A successful organization may incorporate elements of both traditional and newer structures, adapting as needed to meet the evolving needs of its stakeholders.

Answer:

These structures often rely on command and control systems to keep employees aligned with organizational goals. These structures often rely on lean principles, self-managed teams, and agile project management practices. They focus on employee-driven collaboration and problem-solving and are designed to be more responsive to market trends and customer needs.

16. Similarities of traditional organizational design to modern organizational design​.


Answer:

There are also some differences between traditional and modern organizational design:

1. Flexibility: Modern organizational design tends to be more flexible than traditional design, with a greater emphasis on adaptability and the ability to respond to change. Traditional organizational design tends to be more rigid and hierarchical, with a clear division between management and workers.

2. Employee involvement: Modern organizational design often places a greater emphasis on employee involvement and empowerment, with the idea that employees should have a say in decision-making and be able to contribute their own ideas and creativity. Traditional organizational design tends to be more hierarchical, with decision-making power concentrated at the top of the organization.

3. Technology: Modern organizational design tends to make more use of technology, such as computers and communication systems, to facilitate communication and collaboration among employees. Traditional organizational design tends to rely more on face-to-face communication and less on technology.

4.Globalization: Modern organizational design often takes into account the increasing globalization of business, with a focus on creating networks and partnerships with organizations around the world. Traditional organizational design tends to be more local in focus, with less emphasis on global connections.

What Abilities Does This Discipline Give Managers?

By studying the principles of organizational behavior, managers become adept at the following five key skills:

Identify and promote positive behaviors: "Prosocial" behaviors within an organization are those which benefit other individuals and the company as a whole. Leaders at every level of the organization need to be able to identify, promote and reward these behaviors -- and conversely, to discourage behaviors that lead to mistrust and other poor interpersonal dynamics between people who must work together.

Create a positive workplace culture: Individual "prosocial" behaviors do not necessarily occur naturally. New hires do not come into organizations with a mental makeup optimized for the success of their companies. They must first be incentivized, in part through rewards, recognition, perks and bonuses. The right incentives are the building blocks of a supportive and selfless workplace culture.

Motivate employees to exhibit "prosocial" behaviors: This discipline offers a set of motivational tools for managers to use, which takes into account individual differences between employees. Effectively, this skill is applied psychology and sociology for managers.

Identify the causes of "antisocial" behaviors: Toxic behaviors that can infect a department and spread throughout an organization may originate with individuals; they may come from the top down; or they may even be the result of external or internal influences. A manager with expertise in organizational behavior will be able to find the root causes of negative behaviors and develop plans to solve the identified problems.

Assess likely employee response before initiating organizational change: Predictive capabilities are among the most important for managers, and become even more important as leaders work their way up in the organizational structure. In order to determine the right strategies and implement them successfully, leaders at every level must be able to accurately anticipate how employees will react, and work to develop contingencies. The study of organizational behavior enables this predictive capability.
