5. . Plans and Expectation of the Internship/Training Program in agriculture


The internship/training program in agriculture offers valuable learning opportunities for students or recent graduates who want to acquire hands-on experience in the agricultural industry. The program typically includes a mix of classroom instruction, fieldwork, and practical training, covering topics such as crop management, soil science, irrigation, natural resource conservation, and animal health.

During the program, interns/trainees can expect to work closely with experienced professionals who can provide mentorship and guidance. They can also expect to engage in diverse agricultural activities that develop their technical skills, problem-solving abilities, and critical thinking. Additionally, the internship/training program in agriculture can facilitate networking opportunities and provide exposure to new technologies and techniques in the field.

Overall, the internship/training program in agriculture provides a solid foundation for students or recent graduates to begin their careers in agriculture or to further their education in the field.

19. Draw a plan crops of organic agriculture and practices ane assumtion​


Answer:

Organic agriculture is a farming system that relies on practices that promote ecological balance, conserve biodiversity, and avoid the use of synthetic fertilizers, pesticides, and genetically modified organisms (GMOs). Here is a plan for an organic agriculture farm:

Crop Rotation: Crop rotation is the practice of planting different crops in a particular field in a sequence to avoid depleting the soil of nutrients. The crops should be rotated in such a way that the nitrogen-fixing plants such as legumes are alternated with heavy feeders such as corn, and brassicas.

Soil Management: Soil health is essential in organic agriculture. Soil can be managed by adding compost, manure, and cover crops that add organic matter and essential nutrients to the soil. Minimizing soil disturbance and practicing reduced tillage will preserve the soil structure and reduce soil erosion.

Companion Planting: Companion planting involves growing two or more crops together, which can benefit each other. For example, growing beans or peas with corn will provide nitrogen for corn while corn provides support for beans.

Organic Pest Control: Organic pest control involves using natural predators, crop rotation, and crop diversification to control pests and diseases. This includes the use of beneficial insects like ladybugs and practicing crop rotation to reduce the buildup of pests and diseases in the soil.

Integrated Livestock Management: Incorporating livestock into the farming system provides multiple benefits such as providing natural fertilizer, reducing weed and pest pressure, and providing an additional source of income. Integration of livestock should be done in a way that minimizes the impact on the environment.

Assumptions:

1. The farm is located in an area with a suitable climate and soil for organic agriculture.

2. The farmer has access to organic seeds, compost, and manure for soil fertility management.

3. The farmer is willing to invest time and effort into implementing organic practices.

4. The farmer has a market for organic produce.

5. The farmer has the necessary knowledge and skills to implement organic farming practices.

Information Sheet 1.1

APPLY APPROPRIATE SAFETY MEASURES WHILE WORKING IN FARM

INTRODUCTION:

Many hazards are present in the farm. If the farmers are not aware of these hazards these may cause injury to their body or may cause diseases and even death. Farmer should always apply appropriate safety measures while working in the farm. In this lesson the students with the guidance and supervision of their teacher should identify farm works that involve the use of chemicals and hazardous tools and equipment; determine the uses of Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) and determine farm emergency procedures regarding safety working environment.

HAZARD, RISK AND EXPOSURE IN THE FARM

Agricultural crop production deal with a lot of activities to be done in the different workplace. While performing these activities we expose ourselves to a lot of risk. Workplace hazard is a major cause of accident, injury, or harm to a worker who performs such task. These hazards should be the major concern of all who are involved in a certain job or work.

It is important to distinguish hazard, risk and exposure when undertaking risk management.

Hazard is the potential for harm, or adverse effect on an employee‘s health. Anything which may cause injury or ill health to anyone at or near a workplace is a hazard.

Risk is the likelihood that a hazard will cause injury or ill health to anyone at or near a workplace. The level of risk increases with the severity of the hazard and the duration and frequency of exposure.

Exposure occurs when a person comes into contact with a hazard.

Classes of Hazard

Hazards are classified into five different types. They are:

Physical - includes floors, stairs, work platforms, steps, ladders, fire, falling objects, slippery surfaces, manual handling (lifting, pushing, pulling), excessively loud and prolonged noise, vibration, heat and cold, radiation, poor lighting, ventilation, air quality

Mechanical and/or electrical - includes electricity, machinery, equipment, pressure vessels, dangerous goods, fork lifts, cranes, hoists

Chemical - includes chemical substances such as acids or poisons and those that could lead to fire or explosion, like pesticides, herbicides, cleaning agents, dusts and fumes from various processes such as welding

Biological - includes bacteria, viruses, mold, mildew, insects, vermin, animals

Psychosocial environment - includes workplace stressors arising from a variety of sources.
