Computing for Percent by Volume

Problem # 1

   Given:

        volume of solute (isopropanol) = 25 mL

        volume of solvent (water) = 45 mL

    Asked: % by volume

    Formula: % volume = [tex]\frac{volume of solute}{volume of solution}[/tex] x 100%

    Solution:

         % volume = [tex]\frac{volume of solute}{volume of solution}[/tex] x 100%

         % volume = [tex]\frac{volume of solute}{volume of solute + solvent}[/tex] x 100%

         % volume = [tex]\frac{25 mL}{25 mL + 45 mL}[/tex]  x 100%

        % volume = [tex]\frac{25 mL}{70 mL}[/tex] x 100%

        % volume = 0.3571 x 100%

        % volume = 35. 71 %

Problem # 2

   Given:

        volume of solute (acetic acid) = 160 mL

        volume of solution = 650 mL

    Asked: % by volume

    Formula: % volume = [tex]\frac{volume of solute}{volume of solution}[/tex] x 100%

    Solution:

        % volume = [tex]\frac{volume of solute}{volume of solution}[/tex] x 100%

        % volume = [tex]\frac{160 mL}{650 mL}[/tex] x 100%

        % volume = 0.2462 x 100%

        % volume = 24.62 %

Explanation:

The percent by volume is one way to describe the concentration of the solution. It is the ratio of the volume of solute in relation to the volume of the solution. This can be computed using the formula,

        % volume = [tex]\frac{volume of solute}{volume of solution}[/tex] x 100%.

Solute is the substance dissolved by the solvent. The combination of this solute and solvent forms a solution. Thus, in case the volume of solution is not given, we can calculate the volume of solution by adding the volume of solute and volume of solvent.

Also, it is important to note that the greater the percent by volume, the higher the concentration or the more concentrated the solution is.

22. When 3.5 moles of baking soda (NaHCO3) is mixed with a 2.8 moles of acetic acid HC2H3O2), how many grams of sodium acetate (NaC2H3O2) will form? Theoretical yield


Answer:

230 g

Explanation:

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

NaHCO3 + HC2H3O2 → NaC2H3O2 + H2CO3

Step 2: Compute the mass of NaC2H3O2 produced from 3.5 moles of NaHCO3.

mass NaC2H3O2 = 3.5 mol NaHCO3 × (1 mol NaC2H3O2 / 1 mol NaHCO3) × (82.03 g NaC2H3O2 / 1 mol NaC2H3O2)

mass NaC2H3O2 = 290 g

Step 3: Compute the mass of NaC2H3O2 produced from 2.8 moles of HC2H3O2.

mass NaC2H3O2 = 2.8 mol HC2H3O2 × (1 mol NaC2H3O2 / 1 mol HC2H3O2) × (82.03 g NaC2H3O2 / 1 mol NaC2H3O2)

mass NaC2H3O2 = 230 g

Step 4: Determine the limiting reagent.

Since HC2H3O2 produces less amount of NaC2H3O2 than NaHCO3, HC2H3O2 is the limiting reagent.

Step 5: Determine the mass of NaC2H3O2 produced.

Since HC2H3O2 is the limiting reagent, the mass of NaC2H3O2 produced is 230g.

23. Determine how much vinegar (HC2H3O2) in gal is required to react with an iron nail to produce 32.5g of hydrogen. The density of HC2H3O2 is 1.053 g/ml.


✏️STOICHIOMETRY

[tex]\sf{\large{\red{SOLUTION:}}}[/tex]

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

6HC₂H₃O₂ + 2Fe → 2Fe(C₂H₃O₂)₃ + 3H₂

Step 2: Calculate the molar masses of H₂ and HC₂H₃O₂.

• For H₂

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{molar mass of} \: \text{H}_2 & = \text{2(1.008 g)} \\ & = \text{2.016 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For HC₂H₃O₂

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{molar mass of} \: \text{HC}_2\text{H}_3\text{O}_2 & = \text{4(1.008 g) + 2(12.01 g) + 2(16.00 g)} \\ & = \text{60.05 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mass of HC₂H₃O₂ required to produce 32.5 g of hydrogen.

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 6 moles of HC₂H₃O₂ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 3 moles of H₂.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of} \: \text{HC}_2\text{H}_3\text{O}_2 & = 32.5 \: \cancel{\text{g} \: \text{H}_2} \times \frac{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{H}_2}}{2.016 \: \cancel{\text{g} \: \text{H}_2}} \times \frac{6 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{HC}_2\text{H}_3\text{O}_4}}{3 \: \cancel{\text{mol}\: \text{H}_2}} \times \frac{\text{60.05 g} \: \text{HC}_2\text{H}_3\text{O}_2}{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{HC}_2\text{H}_3\text{O}_2}} \\ & = \text{1936.136 g} \: \text{HC}_2\text{H}_3\text{O}_2 \end{aligned}[/tex]

Final Step: Calculate the volume of vinegar.

Note that 1 gallon = 3.7854 liter.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{volume of} \: \text{HC}_2\text{H}_3\text{O}_2 & = \frac{\text{mass of} \: \text{HC}_2\text{H}_3\text{O}_2}{\text{density of} \: \text{HC}_2\text{H}_3\text{O}_2} \\ & = \frac{\text{1936.136 g}}{\text{1.053 g/mL}} \\ & = \text{1838.686 mL} \\ & = 1838.686 \: \cancel{\text{mL}} \times \frac{1 \: \cancel{\text{L}}}{1000 \: \cancel{\text{mL}}} \times \frac{\text{1 gal}}{3.7854 \: \cancel{\text{L}}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{0.486 gal}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 0.486 gallon of vinegar is required.

29. What is the molarity of acetic acid solution if 50 ml of 17 M acetic acid (HC2H3O2) in 1.2 L?


Given:

[tex]M_{1} = \text{17 M}[/tex]

[tex]V_{1} = \text{50 mL}[/tex]

[tex]V_{2} = \text{1.2 L = 1200 mL}[/tex]

Unknown:

[tex]M_{2}[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]M_{1}V_{1} = M_{2}V_{2}[/tex]

[tex]M_{2} = M_{1} × \frac{V_{1}}{V_{2}}[/tex]
