2. what is the theme of the golden hour by james baldwin?​


Answer:

You have heard of Homer, and of the two wonderful poems, the Iliad and the Odyssey, which bear his name. No one knows whether these poems were composed by Homer, or whether they are the work of many different poets. And, in fact, it matters very little about their authorship. Everybody agrees that they are the grandest poems ever sung or written or read in this world; and yet, how few persons, comparatively, have read them, or know any thing about them except at second-hand! Homer commences his story, not at the beginning, but "in the midst of things;" hence, when one starts out to read the Iliad without having made some special preparation beforehand, he finds it hard to understand, and is tempted, in despair, to stop at the end of the first book. Many people are, therefore, content to admire the great masterpiece of poetry and story-telling simply because others admire it, and not because they have any personal acquaintance with it.

26. upon entering high school, Rodriguez saw himself as being not a good reader or bookish despite having read so many authors in the likes of Dante, Descartes, Engels and James Baldwin. why did he say this? Isn't this a harsh assessment of himself Why or why not?​


Answer:

Rodriguez saw himself as not a good reader or bookish despite having read many authors in high school because he realized that his academic achievement in reading and writing did not necessarily translate to his social and cultural identity as a Mexican-American student. He felt a cultural divide between his home life and the education he received in school. His family background emphasized practical knowledge and skills, while his school education emphasized abstract thinking and academic achievements.

Rodriguez's self-assessment can be seen as both harsh and honest. On the one hand, he had read numerous authors and was quite well-read for his age, which suggests he had some skills in reading and comprehension. On the other hand, he recognized that his reading was not grounded in a broader appreciation for literature, but rather a means of achieving academic success. By acknowledging his shortcomings, Rodriguez was able to critically examine his own identity and experiences, which ultimately led to his personal growth and success
