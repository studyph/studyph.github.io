10. DEFINITION OF TERMS:Back of trouser -Belt loops- Cuff- Fly- Front of trouser- Placket- Pocket- Waist band- FIVE WAYS TO TRANSFER PATTERN MARKINGS TO FABRIC:​


Back of trouser - The part of a pair of trousers that covers the back of the legs and buttocks.

Belt loops - Strips of fabric sewn onto the waistband of trousers or skirts, through which a belt can be threaded.

Cuff - The folded or turned-up end of a sleeve or pant leg that is sewn in place to create a finished edge.

Fly - The opening at the front of a pair of trousers or pants that allows them to be put on and taken off.

Front of trouser - The part of a pair of trousers that covers the front of the legs.

Placket - An opening or slit in a garment, usually in the front or back, that allows it to be put on and taken off easily.

Pocket - A small pouch or compartment sewn onto a garment, typically used for carrying small items.

Waistband - The strip of fabric that encircles the waist and supports the garment.

Five ways to transfer pattern markings to fabric are:

Tracing - Lay the pattern on top of the fabric and use a tracing wheel and paper to transfer the markings to the fabric.

Chalk or pencil markings - Use tailor's chalk or a fabric pencil to trace the pattern markings directly onto the fabric.

Thread tracing - Baste along the pattern markings using a contrasting thread, then remove the pattern and use the thread as a guide for cutting and sewing.

Tailor's tacks - Use a needle and contrasting thread to mark the pattern onto the fabric by sewing a series of small stitches.

Marking pens or pencils - Use special fabric marking pens or pencils to trace the pattern markings directly onto the fabric

17. GIVER DESCRIPTION OF THE FOLLOWING FINGERPRINT PATTERN; LOOP, WHORL, ARCH, TENTED​


Answer:

Loops - prints that recurve back on themselves to form a loop shape. Divided into radial loops (pointing toward the radius bone, or thumb) and ulnar loops (pointing toward the ulna bone, or pinky), loops account for approximately 60 percent of pattern types.

Whorls - form circular or spiral patterns, like tiny whirlpools. There are four groups of whorls: plain (concentric circles), central pocket loop (a loop with a whorl at the end), double loop (two loops that create an S-like pattern) and accidental loop (irregular shaped). Whorls make up about 35 percent of pattern types.

Arches - create a wave-like pattern and include plain arches and tented arches. Tented arches rise to a sharper point than plain arches. Arches make up about five percent of all pattern types.

Tented Arch – Similar to the plain arch, the tented arch also has raised ridges flowing in the same fashion. The distinct difference comes in the pitch of the raised ridge. The tented arch has a sharper edge compared to the plain arch, which forms a tent-like shape.
