Answer:

Personification - "Thou foster-child of silence and slow time, Sylvan historian" - giving human characteristics to the inanimate object, the urn.

Alliteration - "A flowery tale more sweetly than our rhyme" - repeating the same consonant sound at the beginning of the words.

Metaphor - "Heard melodies are sweet, but those unheard are sweeter" - comparing unheard melodies to something sweeter than heard melodies.

Anaphora - "What men or gods are these? What maidens loth? What mad pursuit? What struggle to escape? What pipes and timbrels? What wild ecstasy?" - repetition of the same phrase at the beginning of each line.

Imagery - "Of deities or mortals, or of both, in Tempe or the dales of Arcady" - painting a visual image of the urn and its possible origins.

Hyperbole - "More happy love! more happy, happy love! For ever warm and still to be enjoy'd" - exaggerating the happiness of love.

Symbolism - "Beauty is truth, truth beauty, that is all ye know on earth, and all ye need to know" - using the urn as a symbol of beauty and truth.
