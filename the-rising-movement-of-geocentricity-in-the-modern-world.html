7. definition of geocentrism & explanation of geocentrismFOR REPORTING thank u ​


Answer:

Geocentrism is a cosmological model that places the Earth at the center of the universe, with all other celestial bodies including the sun, planets, and stars orbiting around it. This model was widely accepted in ancient times and remained the dominant view of the universe until the 16th century.

Geocentrism was based on the observation that the Earth appeared to be still and the celestial bodies appeared to move around it. This led to the belief that the Earth was the fixed center of the universe and that all other bodies in the heavens were in motion around it. This belief was reinforced by the works of ancient Greek philosophers such as Aristotle and Ptolemy, who developed complex models of the universe that incorporated epicycles, or small circular motions within larger circular orbits, to explain the observed motions of the planets.

However, with the advent of modern astronomy and the work of scientists such as Nicolaus Copernicus, Johannes Kepler, and Galileo Galilei, it became increasingly clear that the geocentric model was inadequate to explain the observed motions of the planets and stars. The heliocentric model, which places the sun at the center of the solar system, ultimately replaced the geocentric model as the accepted scientific view of the universe. Nonetheless, geocentrism remains a significant historical and cultural phenomenon, representing an important milestone in the development of human understanding of the universe.

Geocentrism is the belief that the Earth is the center of the universe and that all celestial bodies, including the Sun, Moon, planets, and stars, revolve around it. In ancient times, this was the dominant cosmological model and was supported by various philosophical and religious traditions.

The concept of geocentrism is based on the observation that the Earth appears to be stationary while the Sun, Moon, planets, and stars move across the sky. This apparent motion was explained by the geocentric model as being caused by the celestial bodies moving in perfect circular orbits around the Earth.

However, the geocentric model was challenged by the heliocentric model proposed by Copernicus in the 16th century, which placed the Sun at the center of the solar system with the planets orbiting around it. The development of modern astronomy and the discovery of new evidence through telescopes and other instruments further discredited geocentrism.

Today, geocentrism is no longer considered a valid scientific model and has been replaced by the heliocentric model, which is widely accepted in the scientific community.
