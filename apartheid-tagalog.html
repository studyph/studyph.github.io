6. how much do you know apartheid​


Answer:

Apartheid (Afrikaans: “apartness”) is the name of the policy that governed relations between the white minority and the nonwhite majority of South Africa during the 20th century. Although racial segregation had long been in practice there, the apartheid name was first used about 1948 to describe the racial segregation policies embraced by the white minority government. Apartheid dictated where South Africans, on the basis of their race, could live and work, the type of education they could receive, and whether they could vote. Events in the early 1990s marked the end of legislated apartheid, but the social and economic effects remained deeply entrenched.

15. what is your opinion about apartheid in South Africa


Answer:

Apartheid was a system of institutionalized racial segregation and discrimination that was enforced in South Africa from 1948 until the early 1990s. The apartheid laws were designed to maintain white domination and control over the country's black majority population.

The apartheid system created separate living areas, schools, and hospitals for different racial groups. It also imposed restrictions on the movement and employment of non-white people. The laws were enforced through violence, imprisonment, and torture.

The international community widely condemned apartheid, and many countries imposed economic sanctions on South Africa in protest. The anti-apartheid movement gained momentum in the 1980s, and Nelson Mandela, a prominent anti-apartheid activist, was released from prison in 1990.

After negotiations between the government and the African National Congress (ANC), South Africa held its first multiracial democratic elections in 1994, and the ANC won a majority. The new government dismantled the apartheid laws and established a new constitution that guaranteed equal rights for all citizens.

In conclusion, apartheid was a discriminatory and oppressive system that violated human rights and caused immense suffering for millions of people in South Africa. Its legacy continues to affect the country today, but progress has been made towards reconciliation and creating a more equitable society.

26. speech about apartheid in Africa​


Good evening, ladies and gentlemen. Today, I would like to talk to you about apartheid in Africa.

For those who may not be familiar with the term, apartheid was a system of racial segregation that was implemented in South Africa from 1948 to 1994. Under this system, the country's white minority government created a set of laws that effectively discriminated against the black majority population, denying them basic rights and freedoms.

Apartheid was a deeply oppressive and inhumane system that affected every aspect of life in South Africa. Black people were forced to live in separate areas from whites, and were not allowed to vote or hold political office. They were also denied access to many public services, including education and healthcare.

The impact of apartheid on the people of South Africa was devastating. Many black South Africans were forced to leave their homes and were forced into poverty. Many were also imprisoned, tortured, and even killed for protesting against the government's policies.

The international community condemned apartheid and imposed economic sanctions on South Africa in an effort to end the system. Finally, in 1994, after decades of struggle and resistance, apartheid was abolished and Nelson Mandela was elected as the country's first black president.

While South Africa has made great strides towards achieving racial equality since the end of apartheid, the legacy of this system still affects the country today. Racial disparities in wealth and access to opportunities continue to exist, and many South Africans still face discrimination and inequality.

It is important to remember the atrocities of apartheid and to continue to work towards a world where everyone is treated equally, regardless of their race. Thank you.

28. How is she affected by apartheid​


Answer:Since the early twentieth century, African women actively opposed the pass laws restricting the movement of Africans. The women understood that these laws would tear African families apart, codifying where Africans could work and live and with whom. Even in the early 1900s, black women successfully prevented proposed legislation that would require them to carry passbooks. In 1953, however, their fear was close to becoming a reality, as the government announced that it would soon impose pass laws restricting the movement of black African women.

In the 1950s, the African National Congress turned to grassroots organizing to work against increasing racial restrictions. Women played a key role, encouraging the larger democratic movement to include women’s issues and fostering the leadership of women. The newly formed Federation of South African Women began organizing women of all races to fight together for equality. The federation started locally but spread throughout the country, organizing from street to street and within trade unions. These grassroots efforts led to many local demonstrations and culminated in the women’s march on Pretoria, the capital, in 1956. Yet their work did not stop the government from extending the pass laws to African women.

One of the leaders was Frances Baard, a longtime organizer with a reputation for perseverance. After the women’s march, the government charged her with treason but was unable to prove its case. A few years later, the government struck again, imprisoning her for five years. In this excerpt from her autobiography My Spirit Is Not Banned, she offers a firsthand account of the women’s march and the particular challenges black African women faced under apartheid.

Filipino:

Ang Apartheid ay isang sistemang pangpolitika sa Timog Aprika, na ginamit noong ika-20 daantaon pangunahing sa pagitan ng dekada ng 1940 at ng dekada ng 1980. Ang apartheid ay ang patakaran ng segregasyon o paghihiwalay ng mga puting tao at taong itim o negro sa Timog Aprika. Sa pagbubukod na ito, nabibigyan ng kapangyarihan at pabor ang mga taong may puting balat kaysa mayroong itim na kulay ng balat.[1] Sa sistema, ang mga tao ng Timog Aprika ay hinati sa kanilang mga lahi. Sa kasalukuyan, ang salitang apartheid ay paminsan-minsang ginagamit upang tumukoy sa mga katulad na sistema sa ibang mga bansa.
