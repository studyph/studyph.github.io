VOLCANOES

Major Locations of Volcanoes

The Pacific Ring of Fire is an immensely huge area located in the basin of the Pacific Ocean where many earthquakes and volcanic eruptions happen every year. In a large 40,000 km not actually a ring but a horseshoe shape zone, it is associated with a nearly continuous series of oceanic trenches, volcanic arcs, and volcanic belts and plate movements.

The Ring of Fire is home to 75% of the world's volcanoes and 90% of its earthquakes. It has 452 Volcanoes in total. These Volcanoes are located in countries such as Philippines, Indonesia, Japan and Singapore.

Magma

- A molten rock material within the earth from which igneous rock results by cooling.

Lava

- A molten rock that spewed out from a volcano or from a fissure in the surface or a rock that has cooled and hardened overtime.

Viscosity

- This is the property of resistance to flow in a fluid or semifluid

Two factors that affects the Magma's Viscosity

• Temperature

• Silica Content

Pahohoe and Aa lava flows

Aa forms when lava flows rapidly. Under these circumstances, there is rapid heat loss and a resulting increase in viscosity.

Pahoehoe forms when lava flows more slowly. Under these circumstances, a well-developed skin can form which inhibits heat loss. When a tear in the skin does form, it is readily healed.

Explosive Volcanoes are usually the ones that produces volcanic tremors when erupting, huge deposits of smoke and lava with pyroclastic materials, and are really violent.

Quite Volcanoes refers to volcanoes that are asleep or the ones that erupts quitely, emitting smokes and ashes, and not really violent.

Harmful effects of Explosive Volcanoes

• The Sudden explosion after eruption

• Great amount of ashes

• Dangerous pyroclastic materials

• Volcanic Quake

• Small Tsunamis

• Huge Deposit of lava flows

• Abrupt cooling of planet's temperature

5 different types of Volcanoes

• Shield

• Strato- volcano or composite

• Cinder

• Spatter

• Complex

Batholith - great mass of intruded igneous rock that for the most part stopped in its rise a considerable distance below the surface.
