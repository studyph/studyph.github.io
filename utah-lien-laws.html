8. What is difference of dead sea and the great salt lake of Utah?


The Dead Sea – bordering Israel, the West Bank and Jordan – is a salt lake whose banks are more than 400m below sea level, the lowest point on dry land. Its famously hypersaline water makes floating easy, and its mineral-rich black mud is used for therapeutic and cosmetic treatments at area resorts. The surrounding desert offers many oases and historic sites.

While the Great Salt Lake, located in the northern part of the U.S. state of Utah, is the largest salt water lake in the Western Hemisphere, and the eighth-largest terminal lake in the world.


According to a resident of Utah, Adam, "Other answers will tell you that the Dead Sea is saltier. This is technically true; the salinity of the Great Salt Lake varies anywhere from 5% to 27%, depending on where you measure and how much rain we've been getting. The northern end is the saltiest, while the closer you get to the Jordan river (yes, we named our river after theirs), the fresher it gets. The Dead Sea, on the other hand, is roughly 33% salt, which is higher, but there's a bit more to the story than that.

These two bodies of water differ in a few interesting ways. Firstly, our lake is bigger--55,000 square kilometers to the Dead Sea's 41,000. The Dead Sea, however, is much deeper (140 ft vs 16 ft), and has about ten times as much water overall. This is because the Dead Sea is deep in a steep valley far below sea level, surrounded by mountains. The Great Salt Lake is over a kilometer above sea level and surrounded by a vast, flat plain. This gives us another unique feature--as you travel west of the lake, there are large pockets of salt flats, a particularly barren form of desert that is mostly useful for setting land speed records, making potash, and gathering salt by the truckload.

 The Great Salt Lake's water contains more than twice as much dissolved sodium as the Dead Sea."

Answer:

Starch

is the most important source of carbohydrates in the human diet and accounts for more than 50% of our carbohydrate intake. It occurs in plants in the form of granules, and these are particularly abundant in seeds (especially the cereal grains) and tubers, where they serve as a storage form of carbohydrates.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

              ADDITIONAL INFORMATION:

MAIN FUNCTION OF A STARCH:

The main function of starch is as way to store energy for plants. Starch is a source of sugar in an animal's diet. Animals break down starch using amylase, an enzyme found in saliva and the pancreas that breaks down starch to get energy.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TYPES OF STARCHES:

1. Tapioca

2. Wheat

3. Cornstarch

4. Arrowroot

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

STARCH IS A USEFUL ENERGY STORE:

Starch is a good storage of carbohydrates because it is an intermediate compared to ATP and lipids in terms of energy. In plants, starch storage folds to allow more space inside cells. It is also insoluble in water, making it so that it can stay inside the plant without dissolving into the system.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

USE OF A STARCH:

You see, plants need energy to grow and grow and grow. They use energy from sunlight to make a simple sugar, glucose.It is used in the food industry either as food products or additives for thickening, preservation and quality enhancer in baked foods, confectioneries, pastas, soups and sauces, and mayonnaises.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

STARCH PRODUCED BY PLANTS:

The simplest form of starch is the linear polymer amylose; amylopectin is the branched form. Wheat starch granules stained with iodine. Starch is manufactured in the green leaves of plants from excess glucose produced during photosynthesis and serves the plant as a reserve food supply.
