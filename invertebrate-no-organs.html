17. a tube like invertebrate that converts organic waste into rich humus making soil fertile​


A tube like invertebrate that converts organic waste into rich humus making soil fertile​ is an earthworm. It is used to transform organic waste into dark brown, nutrient-rich humus, which is an excellent source of plant manure.

An earthworm is a member of the phylum Annelida and a terrestrial invertebrate. They feature a tube-within-a-tube body layout, exterior and internal segmentation that corresponds, and typically have setae on every segment. Wherever soil, water, and temperature allow, they can be found.

Commonly found in soil, earthworms consume a range of organic materials. Plant material, live protozoa, rotifers, nematodes, bacteria, fungi, and other microbes are all considered to be organic matter. The digestive system of an earthworm spans the entire length of its body.

By use of its skin, an earthworm breathes. It has a straightforward, closed circulatory system as well as a twofold transport system composed of coelomic fluid that circulates within the fluid-filled coelom.
