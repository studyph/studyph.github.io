7. give the 3 examples of types of lever. explain.​


Answer:

1.First-class lever: In this type of lever, the fulcrum is located between the effort and load. Examples of first-class levers are seesaws, scissors, and crowbars. Seesaws work by having the fulcrum in the middle, where the effort is applied on one side and the load on the other. When the effort is greater than the load, the seesaw moves down.

2.Second-class lever: In this type of lever, the load is located between the fulcrum and the effort. Examples of second-class levers are wheelbarrows, nutcrackers, and bottle openers. Wheelbarrows have the wheel as the fulcrum, the load as the material being transported, and the effort as the force applied to the handles. This type of lever is efficient because it can move heavy loads with less effort.

3.Third-class lever: In this type of lever, the effort is located between the fulcrum and the load. Examples of third-class levers are fishing rods, brooms, and shovels. The fulcrum is at one end of the lever, the load is at the other end, and the effort is applied in the middle. Third-class levers require more effort than first-class and second-class levers because the load is closer to the fulcrum.

First, second and third class levers in the body

Levers in our body are formed from bones, joints and muscles.

A lever consists of:

a rigid structure (bone)

a force acting upon it (muscle) to produce a turning movement (angular motion)

a fulcrum which is a fixed point (joint)

a load or resistance that is placed on the rigid structure (weight of body part being moved and anything that it is carrying)

Lever in the human body, consisting of a load represented by a weight, effort acting on the lever, and a fulcrum at a joint or fixed point.

A typical lever

There are three types of lever.

1. First class lever – the fulcrum is in the middle of the effort and the load.

The fulcrum is in the middle of the effort and the load, as exemplified in the neck when nodding the head.

First class lever

This type of lever is found in the neck when raising your head to head a football. The neck muscles provide the effort, the neck is the fulcrum, and the weight of the head is the load.

2. Second class lever – the load is in the middle between the fulcrum and the effort.

The load is in the middle of the fulcrum and the effort, as exemplified in the ankle when standing on tiptoe.

Second class lever

This type of lever is found in the ankle area. When standing on tiptoe, the ball of the foot acts as the fulcrum, the weight of the body acts as the load and the effort comes from the contraction of the gastrocnemius muscle. This second class lever is used when taking off for a jump or pushing against the blocks in a sprint start.

3. Third class lever – the effort is in the middle between the fulcrum and the load.

The effort is in the middle of the fulcrum and the load, as exemplified during a biceps curl.

Third class lever

During a biceps curl, the fulcrum is the elbow joint, the effort comes from the biceps contracting and the resistance is the weight of the forearm and any weight that it may be holding.

Mechanical advantages of levers

When a lever's effort arm is longer than its load arm, it is said to have high mechanical advantage. Levers with high mechanical advantage can move large loads with a relatively small amount of effort. Second class levers always have high mechanical advantage. First class levers can have high mechanical advantage, if the fulcrum is close to the load.

24. The 3 types of levers. Give examples and explain them.​


Answer:

There are three classes levers, according to the position of the fulcrum. Class 1 has the fulcrum placed between the effort and load. Class 2 has the load between the effort and the fulcrum. Class 3 has the effort between the load and the fulcrum.

THE 3 TYPES OF LEVERS

Lever is composed of straight or bend rigid bar that is free to turn on the fixed movable part known as fulcrum.

PARTS OF A LEVER

1.) Resistance (R) or load. A force that is being lift by the effort.

2.) Effort (E). A force that lifts the resistance.

3.) Fulcrum (F). The point on which a lever rests or is supported and on which pivot points. Movement of the machine.

4.) Resistant arm. The distance of the resistance to the fulcrum.

5.) Effort arm. The distance of effort to the effort.

CLASSES OF LEVER

The three classes of levers vary in the placement of the effort, resistance or load, and the fulcrum along the bar.

1.) FIRST-CLASS LEVER (RFE of EFR)

The fulcrum is between the effort and the load or resistance as in a seesaw.

SeesawscissorsPliers

2.) SECOND-CLASS LEVER (FRE or ERF)

The fulcrum lies at one end, the effort is applied to the other end, and the load is in the middle as in a wheelbarrow.

Staplerwheelbarrownutcracker

3.) THIRD-CLASS LEVER (REF or FER)

The fulcrum is at one end but the load is at the other end and the effort is applied in the middle as in a human forearm.

SpoonTongForearm
