7. in terms of architectural designs, what are the similarities of chinese, japanese, and korean architecture?​


East Asia has a vast variety of environments, and it would be a disservice to not discuss them briefly to understand their impact on architecture. While China inarguable possesses the widest range of diverse environments, what is often considered quintessential Chinese civilization was based in the Central Plains, or Zhongyuan region, which is defined by large alluvial plains that allowed for large scale agricultural. Though the region is also bordered by significant mountain ranges, most of the nations human population has resided in these plains, which in turn had the strongest influence on their architectural development. For this reason, earth and brickwork are significant in China’s architectural history.

Korea, on the other hand, is a very mountainous region, providing a high availability of timber and very rich granite deposits. Though large cities were often based in plains or river valleys (true enough for any civilization), stone and woodworks were more definitive for their national architecture.

Japan is somewhat similar to Korea in its environment, being very mountainous with larger settlements being based in plains and valleys. Unfortunately, being so close to a tectonic boundary makes Japan considerably less geologically stable than Korea or China. Therefore, Japan’s architecture is defined primarily on woodworks which were more suited to standing up to frequent earthquake activity.

30. compare and contrast japanese, Chinese, and Korean houses using the elements of arts and principle designs. point out the strong points of each house and it's weaknesses. ​


Answer:

Japanese, Chinese, and Korean houses all have distinct architectural styles that reflect their respective cultures and histories.

Japanese houses are known for their simplicity and functionality. The traditional Japanese house, known as a "minka," is built mainly of natural materials such as wood, paper, and thatch. It features a sloping, tiled roof and a raised floor to protect against flooding. The interior is typically divided into a series of small rooms, with sliding doors that can be opened or closed to control the flow of light and air. Elements of art such as wood carving, and principle of design like asymmetrical balance, are commonly used in traditional Japanese houses.

One of the strong points of Japanese houses is their ability to blend in with nature. They often have traditional gardens that feature rock formations and miniature landscapes, which can provide a sense of tranquility and serenity. On the other hand, one of the weaknesses of Japanese houses is that they may not be as energy-efficient as modern houses, as they are not well insulated and can be drafty in the winter.

Chinese houses, in contrast, tend to be more ornate and grandiose. Traditional Chinese houses are often built of brick and tile, and feature sweeping eaves, elaborate decorative carvings, and painted murals. They also have a central courtyard, which serves as the focal point of the house and is used for entertaining and relaxation. The Chinese aesthetic is often characterized by balance, symmetry, and an emphasis on positive and negative space.

One of the strong points of Chinese houses is the attention to detail and craftsmanship in the decorative elements. They have a grandeur and elegance that reflect the cultural values of the time. On the other hand, a weakness of traditional Chinese houses is that they are not always designed with privacy in mind and may not have as much separation between living spaces as other architectural styles.

Korean houses are known for their simplicity and functionality, like Japanese houses. The traditional Korean house, known as a "hanok," is built mainly of natural materials such as wood, bamboo, and thatch. It features a sloping, tiled roof and a raised floor, as well as a central courtyard. The interior is typically divided into a series of small rooms, with sliding doors that can be opened or closed to control the flow of light and air. In terms of elements of art and principle of design, Korean traditional houses has a strong emphasis in the use of natural materials, and the use of balance, symmetry, and natural color palettes.

One of the strong points of Korean houses is that they are designed to be energy-efficient and take advantage of natural cooling and heating. They also often have traditional gardens that can provide a sense of tranquility and serenity. On the other hand, one of the weaknesses of Korean houses is that they may not be as spacious as modern houses and may not have as much separation between living spaces.

It's important to note that these generalizations are simplifications of a complex reality. All three countries have a great variety of architectural styles and the cultures are very dynamic, therefore these comparisons are meant to give a general idea of the main characteristics of these three traditional houses.
