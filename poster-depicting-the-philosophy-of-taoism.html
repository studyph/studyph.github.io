1. what is the moral philosophy of taoism


The moral philosophy of Taoism

Taoism or Daoism is a philosophy from China that focuses on studying natural life. Taoism emphasizes the harmonious relationship between humans and nature.

Taoism was founded by a Chinese thinker named "Lao Tzi" who also wrote the holy book of Taoism, the "Tao Te Ching".

Experts in China distinguish between Taoism as a philosophy (Tao Chia) and Taoism as a religion (Tao Chiao).

Here are some of the moral philosophies of Taoism:

1. Respect and protect life.

Taoism teaches us to be more compassionate by respecting all aspects of life, starting with ourselves and then with other things, be it humans, nature, or animals. We are forbidden to abuse our power by killing weak animals and plants carelessly. We are also forbidden to harm others in order to achieve our goals. Taoism believes that our lives are connected to other lives, and respecting all forms of life can balance our yin and yang energies.

2. Love and help one another.

Taoism also teaches us to care for others such as giving alms to the homeless and helping injured animals

3. Don't judge others easily.

Taoism teaches that all creatures are important, therefore we must respect them by not easily criticizing and trying to better understand and appreciate them, both those we love and those we hate.

4. Be honest with yourself and others.

In life, we ​​may often try to always please others and then make a decision or accept something that we really don't like, which is not honest with ourselves. This is forbidden because it can cause discomfort. We should also always tell the truth even if it will make us not have many friends.

The essence of Taoism is to teach us to connect with our true selves and find peace, accept the truth of ourselves and others, and help others.

4. Ethics is a branch of philosophy which deals with correct judgement. which scenario depicts this?​


Answer:

The field of ethics (or moral philosophy) involves systematizing, defending, and recommending concepts of right and wrong behavior. Philosophers today usually divide ethical theories into three general subject areas: metaethics, normative ethics, and applied ethics. Metaethics investigates where our ethical principles come from, and what they mean. Are they merely social inventions? Do they involve more than expressions of our individual emotions? Metaethical answers to these questions focus on the issues of universal truths, the will of God, the role of reason in ethical judgments, and the meaning of ethical terms themselves. Normative ethics takes on a more practical task, which is to arrive at moral standards that regulate right and wrong conduct. This may involve articulating the good habits that we should acquire, the duties that we should follow, or the consequences of our behavior on others. Finally, applied ethics involves examining specific controversial issues, such as abortion, infanticide, animal rights, environmental concerns, homosexuality, capital punishment, or nuclear war.

13. What aspect of Taoism often misunderstood as a fatalistic and superstitious philosophy and religion?​


Answer:

1.Daoism could be called “superstitious” by atheists (including marxist mainland Chinese), as all religions are. However, while it is a religion to some, Daoism can also be practiced as a non-religious philosophy. So (assuming the critique were valid) “superstitious” applies to it less well than to traditions that are strictly religious, such as Christianity and Islam.

2.The core of Daoism is non-religious, so “superstitious” would seem to be an aspersion by atheists who view any spiritual practice as superstition. This reflects a very shallow understanding of spiritual practice.

3.To the Western mind, nature is something to be forcibly re-engineered, remoulded, and paved over. No aligning, harmonizing, or patience required. To the Western mind, aligning with nature gracefully might seem resigned, helpless, or “fatalistic”. But that is a gross misperception by an inferior mind (at least in this one respect).

4.From the Daoist perspective, the Western tendency to forcibly re-engineer, remould, and pave over nature (as mainland China now also does, having abandoned Daoism) is destructive, wasteful, clumsy, coarse, and careless.

5.Whether or not you embrace Daoism, gracefully aligning with, harmonizing with, and waiting on nature is the superior way, and there’s nothing “fatalistic” about that. On the other hand, the Western way, which is wantonly destroying nature, is fatal.
