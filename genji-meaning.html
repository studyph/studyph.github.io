The Tale of Genji, thought by many to be the first novel in the history of world literature, was written by a woman, Murasaki Shikibu, in the eleventh century. Lady Murasaki lived during the Heian Period (794-1185), an era remarkable for the poetry, diaries, and fiction produced by court ladies. Their sensitivity to nature and the art of love set the tone for the art and literature of their time. While the men wrote in an awkward, scholarly form of Chinese, the women developed a Japanese script more suitable to the Japanese language. In succeeding periods of warfare, popular tales of the bravery of the samurai warriors would all but obliterate court ladies' writings. Even in such tales, however, the warrior would stop in the midst of battle to recite a poem, play his flute, and sigh over the falling of a blossom.

30. what is the rising action, falling action of the tale of genji?​


"The Tale of Genji" is a classic work of Japanese literature that was written by Murasaki Shikibu in the 11th century. As a long and complex narrative, it contains multiple rising and falling actions throughout its many chapters. However, I can provide a general overview of the rising and falling actions of the tale:

The rising action of "The Tale of Genji" is generally considered to be the first 17 chapters, which focus on the life of Genji, the son of an emperor, and his romantic relationships with various women. During this section of the book, Genji experiences many successes and failures in love, and his relationships with the women in his life become increasingly complex and intertwined.

The falling action of the tale is generally considered to begin around Chapter 18, when Genji's son Kaoru becomes the focus of the narrative. The later chapters of the book explore Kaoru's relationships and experiences, as well as the lives of other characters who are connected to Genji's family. The falling action of the tale is marked by a sense of melancholy and nostalgia, as the characters reflect on the passing of time and the impermanence of life.

Overall, it is important to note that "The Tale of Genji" is a complex work with many different narrative threads and themes. The rising and falling actions of the tale are not necessarily straightforward or easily defined, and different readers may interpret the structure of the book in different ways.
