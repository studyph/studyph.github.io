5. A solution of 5.36 g of a molecular compound dissolved in 76.8 g of benzene has a boiling point of 81.48° C. The boiling point of pure benzene is 80.10° C and the boiling point elevation constant for benzene is 2.53°C/m. What is the molar mass of the solute? A. 128 g/molB. 69.7 g/molC. 32.2 g/molD. 6.00 g/molwith explanation please ​


Given:

mass of solute = 5.36 g

mass of solvent = 76.8 g = 0.0768 kg

[tex]K_{\text{b}} = 2.53^{\circ}\text{C/m}[/tex]

[tex]T_{\text{b}} = 81.48^{\circ}\text{C}[/tex]

[tex]T^{\circ}_{\text{b}} = 80.10^{\circ}\text{C}[/tex]

solute: a molecular compound

solvent: benzene

Required:

molar mass of solute

Strategy:

In this problem, we need to find the molar mass of the solute. Since the given solute is a molecular compound, the formulas we are going to use to find the molar mass of solute are as follows:

[tex]\boxed{\Delta T_{\text{b}} = T_{\text{b}} - T^{\circ}_{\text{b}}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\Delta T_{\text{b}} = K_{\text{b}}m}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{m = \dfrac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent}}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{molar mass of solute} = \dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{moles of solute}}}[/tex]

where:

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = \text{boiling-point elevation}[/tex]

[tex]T_{\text{b}} = \text{boiling point of the solution}[/tex]

[tex]T^{\circ}_{\text{b}} = \text{boiling point of the pure solvent}[/tex]

[tex]K_{\text{b}} = \text{molal boiling-point elevation constant}[/tex]

[tex]m = \text{molality}[/tex]

Note: The molal boiling-point elevation constant (Kb) depends on the type of solvent used.

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the boiling point elevation.

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = T_{\text{b}} - T^{\circ}_{\text{b}}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = 81.48^{\circ}\text{C} - 80.10^{\circ}\text{C}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = 1.38^{\circ}\text{C}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molality of the solution.

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = K_{\text{b}}m[/tex]

[tex]m = \dfrac{\Delta T_{\text{b}}}{K_{\text{b}}}[/tex]

[tex]m = \dfrac{1.38^{\circ}\text{C}}{2.53^{\circ}\text{C/m}}[/tex]

[tex]m = \text{0.54545 m}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

Note: 1 m = 1 mol/kg

[tex]m = \dfrac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent}}[/tex]

moles of solute = [tex](m)[/tex](mass of solvent)

moles of solute = (0.54545 mol/kg)(0.0768 kg)

moles of solute = 0.041891 mol

Step 4: Calculate the molar mass of solute.

[tex]\text{molar mass of solute} = \dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{moles of solute}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molar mass of solute} = \dfrac{\text{5.36 g}}{\text{0.041891 mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{molar mass of solute = 128 g/mol}}[/tex]

Answer:

A. 128 g/mol

6. When 0.279 g of a molecular compound, benzoic acid, was dissolved in 43.0 g of benzene, the freezing point of the solution was lowered to5.15 °C. What is the molecular weight of the benzoic acid? (Note: Ks førbenzene = 5.12°C/m; Freezing point of benzene= 5,5°C)​


✒️FREEZING-POINT DEPRESSION

[tex]\tt{\huge{\blue{Solution:}}}[/tex]

The problem asks to solve for the molecular weight of the solute. First, we need to identify the quantities to be used. These are

m = molality

mass = mass of solute

n = number of moles of solute

MW = molecular weight of solute

[tex]\text{kg}_{solv} =[/tex] kilograms of solvent

[tex]K_{\text{f}} =[/tex] molal freezing-point depression constant

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{f}} =[/tex] freezing-point depression

[tex]T{{\circ}\atop{\text{f}}} =[/tex] freezing point of the pure solvent

[tex]T_{\text{f}} =[/tex] freezing point of the solution

Second, we need to identify the given values. These are

mass = 0.359 g

[tex]\text{kg}_{solv} =[/tex] 43.0 g = 0.0430 kg

[tex]K_{\text{f}} =[/tex] 5.12 °C/m

[tex]T{{\circ}\atop{\text{f}}} =[/tex] 5.5°C

[tex]T_{\text{f}} =[/tex] 5.15°C

Third, we need to find the freezing-point depression of the solution. The freezing-point depression is

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{f}} = T{{\circ}\atop{\text{f}}} - T_{\text{f}}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{f}} = 5.5^{\circ}\text{C} - 5.15^{\circ}\text{C}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{f}} = 0.35^{\circ}\text{C}[/tex]

Fourth, we need to find the molality of the solution. The molality is

[tex]m = \dfrac{\Delta T_{\text{f}}}{K_{\text{f}}}[/tex]

[tex]m = \dfrac{0.35^{\circ}\text{C}}{5.12^{\circ}\text{C/m}}[/tex]

m = 0.068359 m

Fifth, we need to find the number of moles of solute. The number of moles of solute is

[tex]n = m(\text{kg}_{solv})[/tex]

[tex]n = (0.068359 \: m)(\text{0.0430 kg})[/tex]

n = 0.0029395 mol

Finally, we can now solve for the molecular weight of solute. Therefore, the molecular weight of solute is

[tex]\text{MW} = \dfrac{\text{mass}}{n}[/tex]

[tex]\text{MW} = \dfrac{\text{0.359 g}}{\text{0.0029395 mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{MW = 122 g/mol}}[/tex]

Multiply the whole equation by 2 to eliminate the fraction.

(C₆H₆ + ¹⁵/₂O₂ --> 6CO₂ + 3H₂O)2

Balanced equation:

2C₆H₆ + 15O₂ --> 12CO₂ + 6H₂O

Products side:

12 carbon atoms

12 hydrogen atoms

30 oxygen atoms

CO₂ = 12.011 + 2(15.999) = 44.009 g/mole

18. When 5.16 g of an unknown molecular compound, was dissolved in 40.0 g of benzene, the freezing point of the solution was lowered to 5.23 °C. What is the molar mass of unknown compound?​


Answer:

To find the molar mass of the unknown compound, we can use the equation:

ΔT = Kf × m

where ΔT is the change in freezing point, Kf is the freezing point depression constant of benzene (5.12 °C/m), and m is the molality of the solution.

First, we need to calculate the molality of the solution:

molality (m) = moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg

The mass of benzene in kg is 40.0 g / 1000 g = 0.0400 kg

To find the moles of solute, we can use the formula:

moles = mass / molar mass

The mass of the unknown compound is 5.16 g.

Therefore, moles of solute = 5.16 g / molar mass

Putting this all together, we have:

m = moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg

m = (5.16 g / molar mass) / 0.0400 kg

m = 129 / molar mass

Now we can use the freezing point depression equation to solve for the molar mass of the unknown compound:

ΔT = Kf × m

5.23 °C = 5.12 °C/m × (129 / molar mass)

molar mass = (5.12 °C/m × 129) / 5.23 °C

molar mass = 126.4 g/mol

Therefore, the molar mass of the unknown compound is approximately 126.4 g/mol.

24. The freezing point of a solution containing 65gm of a compound in 100 gm Benzene is 3.8 °C. What is the molecular weight of the compound if the freezing point of benzen is 5.5°C and Kf is 4.9°C/molal​


The freezing point depression (ΔTf) of a solution is given by:

ΔTf = Kf x molality

where Kf is the freezing point depression constant of the solvent and molality is the concentration of solute in moles per kilogram of solvent.

In this case, the molality of the solution can be calculated as follows:

mass of benzene = 100 g

mass of solute = 65 g

mass of solvent = 100 g

molality = moles of solute / kg of solvent

The molecular weight of the solute can be calculated using the following formula:

molecular weight = mass of solute / moles of solute

To find the number of moles of solute, we can use the freezing point depression equation:

ΔTf = Kf x molality

molality = ΔTf / Kf

molality = (5.5 - 3.8) / 4.9 = 0.367

moles of solute = (mass of solute / molecular weight of solute)

molality = moles of solute / kg of solvent

0.367 = moles of solute / 0.1 kg

moles of solute = 0.0367

Substituting this value in the molecular weight equation gives:

molecular weight = mass of solute / moles of solute

molecular weight = 65 g / 0.0367 mol

molecular weight = 1771 g/mol

Therefore, the molecular weight of the compound is 1771 g/mol.

Answer:

To solve for the molecular weight of the compound, we can use the formula:

ΔT = Kf·m·i

where:

ΔT = change in freezing point = 5.5°C - 3.8°C = 1.7°C

Kf = freezing point depression constant = 4.9°C/molal

m = molality of the solution = moles of solute / mass of solvent (in kg)

i = van't Hoff factor (for non-electrolytes, i = 1)

First, let's calculate the molality of the solution:

mass of compound = 65 g

mass of benzene = 100 g = 0.1 kg

moles of compound = mass / molar mass

molar mass of compound = (mass of compound) / (moles of compound) = 65 g / (moles of compound)

molality = (moles of compound) / (mass of benzene in kg) = (moles of compound) / 0.1 kg

Substituting the given values:

1.7°C = 4.9°C/molal · (moles of compound / 0.1 kg) · 1

moles of compound = (1.7°C / (4.9°C/molal · 0.1 kg)) = 0.346 moles

molar mass of compound = (mass of compound) / (moles of compound) = 65 g / 0.346 moles = 187.86 g/mol

Therefore, the molecular weight of the compound is approximately 187.86 g/mol.
