1. what is phagocytosis?​


Answer:

Phagocytosis is the process by which a cell uses its plasma membrane to engulf a large particle, giving rise to an internal compartment called the phagosome. It is one type of endocytosis. 


2. phagocytosis vs. pinocytosis​


Explanation:

Phagocytosis engulfs whole particles, which are later broken down by enzymes, such as cathepsins, and absorbed into the cells. Pinocytosis, on the other hand, is when the cell engulfs already-dissolved or broken-down food.


3. Outline the major phases of phagocytosis.


Answer:

Phagocytosis has five stages:

1. Phagocyte interaction with an antigen and consequent endocytosis or engulfment

2. Phagosome formation

3. Fusion of lysosome forming phagolysosome

4. Digestion

5. Release

Explanation:

Phagocytosis is an immune mechanism of the body's innate immune system. This process is carried out by white blood cells, particulary neutrophils and monocytes/macrophages. These cells act as phagocytes.

Phagocytosis may either be direct or indirect. Direct phagocytosis involves direct recognition of the phagocyte with the antigen via the primitive pattern receptor or the toll-like receptor. Indirect phagocytosis, on the other hand, requires opsonins, such as antibodies and complement proteins, that will bind to the antigen. Opsonins help enhance the phagocytosis.

After recognition, endocytosis or engulfment of the antigen occurs. Once inside the phagocyte, the antigen is enclosed in a vesicle forming the phagosome. The lysosome will fuse with the phagosome, forming the phagolysosome. This fusion initiates the digestion process of the antigen.

Digestion may be through the:

1. release of granular contents such as lysosomal enzymes; or

2. oxidative burst by production of oxygen-reactive species that renders toxicity to the antigen.

After digestion, antigen remnants that are not digested will be released by exocytosis.

4. Does a bacterial cell wall protects the cell from phagocytosis?


Answer:

Some pathogenic bacteria are inherently able to resist the bactericidal components of host tissues, usually as a function of some structural property. For example, the poly-D-glutamate capsule of Bacillus anthracis protects the organisms against action of cationic proteins (defensins) in sera or in phagocytes.

13. Which transport mechanism can bring whole cells into a cell? Exocytosis Pinocytosis Phagocytosis Facilitated diffusion


Answer:

Endocytosis

Explanation:

Endocytosis is a type of active transport that moves particles, such as large molecules, parts of cells, and even whole cells, into a cell. There are different variations of endocytosis, but all share a common characteristic: The plasma membrane of the cell invaginates, forming a pocket around the target particle.

Answer:

Endocytosis is a cellular process in which substances are brought into the cell. The material to be internalized is surrounded by an area of cell membrane, which then buds off inside the cell to form a vesicle containing the ingested material. Endocytosis includes pinocytosis and phagocytosis.
