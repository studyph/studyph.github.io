4. how to separate the precipitate from the liquid in precipitation reaction?


The insoluble solid, called the precipitate, initially forms a suspension, meaning that it is well dispersed in solution. The precipitate typically agglomerates, and then is separated from the liquid by sedimentation, centrifugation, or filtration.

5. To determine which one of the products in a precipitation reaction is the precipitate, which of the following should you follow?


Precipitation occurs when a cation and anion in an aqueous solution reacts to form an insoluble ionic solid compound which we called precipitates. In a chemical reaction, we can determine the precipitate in a precipitation reaction by using the solubility rules because not all aqueous solution forms a precipitate.

Precipitates mostly occurs in a single replacement or double replacement reaction. Specifically, double replacement reaction is also called precipitation reaction.  Here is an example of Precipitation reaction:  

2NaOH(aq)+MgCl2(aq)→2NaCl(aq)+Mg(OH)2(s)

The solubility rules will help scientist predict if a precipitate will form in a chemical reaction in an aqueous solution.  

Here is the solubility rules that will help you predict the formation of precipitate in a precipitation reaction. This rules should be follow from top to bottom:

1. Salts formed with group 1 cations and NH4+ cations are soluble. There are some exceptions for certain Li+ salts.

2. Acetates (C2H3O2−), nitrates (NO3−), and perchlorates (ClO4−) are soluble.

3. Bromides, chlorides, and iodides are soluble.

4. Sulfates are soluble with the exception of sulfates formed with Ca2+, Sr2+, and Ba2+.

5. Salts containing silver, lead, and mercury (I) are insoluble.

6. Carbonates , phosphates, sulfides, oxides, and hydroxides (OH−OH−) are insoluble. Sulfides formed with group 2 cations and hydroxides formed with calcium, strontium, and barium are exceptions.
