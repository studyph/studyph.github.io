9. CH4+2H2O→ 4H2 (g) + .CO2(g) IF 60.0 g of CH4 reacted with 6.3g of water, what is the limiting reactant?


Answer:

The limiting reactant of a reaction is the substance present in lesser amounts. In this case, it is H 2O :))

Explanation:

This is obtained by, first, computing the number of moles of CH4 and H2O from the given mass.

To convert from mass to moles, one only needs to multiply the given mass to the molar mass of the compound.

60 g CH4 • (1 mol CH4/16.05 g CH4) ≈ 3.78 mol CH4

6.3 g H20 • (1 mol H2O/18.01 g H20) ≈ 0.35 mol H20

Second, compute for the mole ratio to confirm how much of each substance would be consumed theoretically in the reaction.

A 3.78 mol CH4 would use up around 7.56 mol of H2O, while a 0.35 mol of H20 would use up approximately 0.18 mol of CH4.

It is presented in this computation:

3.78 mol CH4 • (2 mol H2O/1 mol CH4) = 7.56 mol H2O

0.35 mol H2O • (1 mol CH4/ 2 mol H2O) = 0.18 mol CH4

So, if all 3.78 mole of CH4 is used, at some point there would be no more moles of H20 left for the reaction to proceed (because we are only given 0.35 moles of the said substance). The lesser amount of H20 would inhibit further reaction. Hence, it is the limiting reactant.

mole ratio = 1 mol CH₄ : 2 mol O₂

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of O₂ needed using the mole ratio.

[tex]\text{moles of O₂ = 2.25 mol CH₄} × \frac{\text{2 mol O₂}}{\text{1 mol CH₄}}[/tex]

For CO₂

Step 1: Determine the mole ratio needed.

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of CO₂ produced using the mole ratio.

[tex]\text{moles of CO₂ = 2.25 mol CH₄} × \frac{\text{1 mol CO₂}}{\text{1 mol CH₄}}[/tex]

mole ratio = 1 mol CH₄ : 2 mol H₂O

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of H₂O produced using the mole ratio.

16. Determine ΔH for the reaction 2 CH4 → 2 H2 + C2H4 given these data:CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O ΔH = −891 kJC2H4 + 3O2 → 2 CO2 + 2 H2O ΔH = −1,411 kJ2H2 + O2 → 2 H2O ΔH = −571 kJ​


[tex]\tt{\huge{\blue{Explanation:}}}[/tex]

Our goal here is to calculate the enthalpy change for the decomposition of CH₄ into C₂H₄ and H₂. The reaction does not occur directly, however, so we must use an indirect route using the information given by Equations (a), (b), and (c). The equations are:

(a) CH₄ + 2 O₂ → C₂H₄ + 2 H₂O ∆H = –891 kJ

(b) C₂H₄ + 3O₂ → 2 CO₂ + 2 H₂O ∆H = –1,411 kJ

(c) 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O ∆H = -571 kJ

The overall reaction is

2 CH₄ → 2 H₂ + C₂H₄

[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

Looking at the overall reaction, we need 2 moles of CH₄ as a reactant so we multiply Equation (a) by 2 to get

(d) [tex]\begin{array}{cccccccl} 2\text{CH}_4 & + & 4\text{O}_2 & \rightarrow & 2\text{CO}_2 & + & 4\text{H}_2\text{O} & \Delta H = 2(-\text{891 kJ}) \\ & & & & & & & \Delta H = -\text{1,782 kJ} \\ \end{array}[/tex]

Looking at the overall reaction, we need 1 mole of C₂H₄ as a product. So we reverse Equation (b) to get

(e) [tex]\begin{array}{cccccccl} 2\text{CO}_2 & + & 2\text{H}_{2}\text{O} & \rightarrow & \text{C}_2\text{H}_4 & + & 3\text{O}_2 & \Delta H = -(-\text{1,411 kJ}) \\ & & & & & & & \Delta H = \text{1,411 kJ} \\ \end{array}[/tex]

Looking at the overall reaction, we need 1 mole of H₂ as a product. So we reverse Equation (c) to get

(f) [tex]\begin{array}{cccccl} 2\text{H}_2\text{O} & \rightarrow & 2\text{H}_2 & + & \text{O}_2 & \Delta H = -(-\text{571 kJ}) \\ & & & & & \Delta H = \text{571 kJ} \\ \end{array}[/tex]

Adding Equations (d), (e), and (f) together, we get

[tex]\begin{array}{cccccccl} 2\text{CH}_4 & + & 4\text{O}_2 & \rightarrow & 2\text{CO}_2 & + & 4\text{H}_2\text{O} & \Delta H = -\text{1,782 kJ} \\ 2\text{CO}_2 & + & 2\text{H}_{2}\text{O} & \rightarrow & \text{C}_2\text{H}_4 & + & 3\text{O}_2 & \Delta H = \: \: \: \: \text{1,411 kJ} \\ & & 2\text{H}_2\text{O} & \rightarrow & 2\text{H}_2 & + & \text{O}_2 & \Delta H = \quad \: \: \: \text{571 kJ} \\ \hline & & 2\text{CH}_4 & \rightarrow & 2\text{H}_2 & + & \text{O}_2 & \Delta H = \quad \: \: \: \text{200 kJ} \end{array}[/tex]

Therefore, the heat of reaction is

[tex]\boxed{\Delta H = \text{200 kJ}}[/tex]

a) Synthesis

b) Double Displacement

d) Synthesis

f) Decomposition

Further explanation  

There are several chemical reactions  

1. Combination / Synthesis  

The combination from two-element

A + B ⇒ AB

2. Decomposition  

One compound breaks down into 2 components

AB ⇒ A + B

3. Single displacement

One element replaces another element in a compound  

A+BC  ⇒ AC + B

4. Double-Replacement reactions  

Two elements in two compounds replace each other to form new products  

AB + CD ⇒ AD + CB

5. Combustion  

Reaction with oxygen which produces CO₂ and water  

CxHy + O₂ ⇒ CO₂ + H₂O

(a) N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) → 2NH₃(g)

2 reactants N₂ and H₂ combine to form NH₃ : Syntesis

(b) Mg(OH)₂(s) + 2HCl(l) → MgCl₂(s) + 2H₂O(l)

Cation Mg⁺ and anion Cl⁻ switch places : Double-Replacement reactions  

(c) CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)

Oxygen combines with hydrocarbon to form water and carbondioxide : Combustion

(d) H₂(g) + 1/2O₂(g) → H₂O(l)

2 reactants H₂ and O₂ combine to form H₂O : Syntesis

(e) HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)

Cation Na⁺ and anion Cl⁻ switch places : Double-Replacement reactions  

(f) CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g)

A single reactant CaCO₃ breaks down into CaO + CO₂ : Decomposition

27. Methane and oxygen react in the presence of a catalyst to form formaldehyde. In a parallel reaction, methane is oxidized to CO2 & H2O. Consider the following stoichiometric reactions CH4(g)+O2(g)==>HCHO(g)+H2O(g) CH4(g)+2O2(g)==>CO2(g)+2H2O(g) a)Calculate the standard heat of reaction for both reaction b)If mole of CO2 in the effluent gases is 0.15 and there is no remaining O2, determine the composition of effluent gases and the amount of heat remove from the reactor


Answer: a) To calculate the standard heat of reaction

Explanation:

(ΔH°) for a reaction, we use the equation: ΔH° = ΣΔHf° (products) - ΣΔHf° (reactants).

For the first reaction:

CH4(g) + O2(g) --> HCHO(g) + H2O(g)

ΔHf° for CH4 = -74.8 kJ/mol

ΔHf° for O2 = 0 kJ/mol

ΔHf° for HCHO = -170.7 kJ/mol

ΔHf° for H2O = -285.8 kJ/mol

ΔH° = (-170.7 kJ/mol + -285.8 kJ/mol) - (-74.8 kJ/mol + 0 kJ/mol) = -456.5 kJ/mol

For the second reaction:

CH4(g) + 2O2(g) --> CO2(g) + 2H2O(g)

ΔHf° for CH4 = -74.8 kJ/mol

ΔHf° for O2 = 0 kJ/mol

ΔHf° for CO2 = -393.5 kJ/mol

ΔHf° for H2O = -285.8 kJ/mol

ΔH° = (-393.5 kJ/mol + -285.8 kJ/mol2) - (-74.8 kJ/mol + 0 kJ/mol2) = -890.2 kJ/mol

b) To determine the composition of the effluent gases and the amount of heat removed from the reactor, we need to know the moles of CO2 and the moles of H2O that were produced in the reaction. The moles of CO2 produced in the reaction is given as 0.15. Therefore, we can use the stoichiometry of the second reaction to find the moles of H2O produced:

CH4(g) + 2O2(g) --> CO2(g) + 2H2O(g)

0.15 mol CO2 = (1 mol CO2 / 1 mol CH4) * n (CH4)

n(CH4) = 0.15 mol CO2 / (1 mol CO2 / 1 mol CH4) = 0.15 mol CH4

From the stoichiometry of the reaction, we can see that 2 moles of H2O are produced for every mole of CH4. Therefore, the moles of H2O produced in the reaction are:

n(H2O) = 2 moles H2O / 1 mole CH4 * 0.15 mol CH4 = 0.3 mol H2O

The effluent gases are composed of 0.15 moles of CO2 and 0.3 moles of H2O. To find the amount of heat removed from the reactor, we can use the standard heat of reaction for the second reaction and multiply it by the moles of CH4 that reacted:

Q = ΔH° * n(CH4) = -890.2 kJ/mol * 0.15 mol CH4 = -133.53 kJ

So, the effluent gases are composed of 0.15 moles of CO2 and 0.3 moles of H2O and 133.53 kJ of heat were removed from the reactor.
