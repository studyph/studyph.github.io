8. 8. The behavior of the participants is observed and is critical to the analysis of results.​


Answer:

Participant Observation

Explanation:

Participant observation is in some ways both the most natural and the most challenging of qualitative data collection methods. It connects the researcher to the most basic of human experiences, discovering through immersion and participation the hows and whys of human behavior in a particular context. Such discovery is natural in that all of us have done this repeatedly throughout our lives, learning what it means to be members of our own families, our ethnic and national cultures, our workgroups, and our personal circles and associations. The challenge of harnessing this innate capability for participant observation is that when we are participant observers in a more formal sense, we must, at least a little, systematize and organize an inherently fluid process. This means not only being a player in a particular social milieu but also fulfilling the role of the researcher—taking notes; recording voices, sounds, and images; and asking questions that are designed to uncover the meaning behind the behaviors. Additionally, in many cases, we are trying to discover and analyze aspects of social scenes that use rules and norms that the participants may experience without explicitly talking about, that operate on automatic or subconscious levels, or are even officially off-limits for discussion or taboo. The result of this discovery and systemization is that we not only make ourselves into acceptable participants in some venue but also generate data that can meaningfully add to our collective understanding of human experience.

11. when is the best time to observe the behavior of a real gas​


Answer:

At high pressures, most real gases exhibit larger PV/nRT values than predicted by the ideal gas law, whereas at low pressures, most real gases exhibit PV/nRT values close to those predicted by the ideal gas law. Gases most closely approximate ideal gas behavior at high temperatures and low pressures.

18. Understanding the James - Lange Theory on Emotions. What is the correct sequence? A. Physiological changes, feel emotion, interpretation of emotion, observable behavior. B. Physiological changes, interpretation of physiological change, feel emotion, observable behavior. C. Feel emotion, physiological changes, interpretation of physiological change, observable behavior. D. Brain interpretation, physiological change, observable way. E. Interpretation of stimuli, brain interpretation, physiological changes, observable behavior. Discuss your answer, and then submit.


Answer:

B. Physiological changes, interpretation of physiological change, feel emotion, observable behavior.

Explanation:

The James-Lange Theory of Emotions states that events around us cause physiological changes or arousal. We then interpret these physiological changes or arousal and based on our interpretation, we feel the emotions associated with the interpretation. We then act in response to the felt emotion. These actions are the observable behaviors following the emotion. To illustrate the James-Lange Theory of Emotions, consider the following:

You see a man in a dark alley (stimulus)Your heart then suddenly beat faster and you feel sweaty (physiologic response)Your brain tried to interpret these emotions (interpretation)You feel fear based on the interpretation made by the brain (emotion)You turned around and ran back home (behavior)

23. it means looking to wards to observable and external behavior​


OBSERVABLE AND EXTERNAL BEHAVIOR

Observable behaviors are those that you can observe someone else doing. This includes activities such as walking, talking, sitting, singing, embracing, eating, sleeping, and solving math problems. Mental and emotional actions and states that cannot be directly witnessed are considered unobservable behaviors.

Problem behaviors that are focused toward the outside world are known as externalizing behaviors. Physical aggressiveness, disobedience of regulations, cheating, theft, and property destruction are among them.

It is possible to observe behavior. What we see or hear, such as a pupil sitting, standing, speaking, whispering, yelling, or writing, is what we call it. What matters is how a student displays his or her feelings, not what the kid is feeling. A student might express his displeasure by making a face, yelling, crossing his arms, and turning away from the teacher, for example. These acts are more descriptive than simply mentioning that the pupil appears to be anxious.

Behavior can be measured. This implies that the teacher has the ability to define and characterize the behavior. When the behavior occurs, the teacher can immediately recognize it, including when it begins, finishes, and how frequently it occurs. For instance, "constantly interrupting the teacher" is not measurable because it is not specific.
