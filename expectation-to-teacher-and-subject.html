8. If teachers teach 1 subject? do the teachers expect to study all the subjects?


Answer:

Because at the start of their educational journey no-one knows, including the pupil herself, which they will enjoy or excel at So it is only fair to offer them as many as possible.

No-one is expected to, or capable of, studying all the subjects. Nor are they given the chance. Consider languages. Very few schools offer more than two foreign languages, out of dozens of modern languages that are examinable. Only one school in Lincolnshire teaches Theatre arts, Music is elective (and probably chargeable) if offered at all, and subjects like Cookery (“Home economics”, ”Nutrition”, “Catering”) have vanished from the curriculum. Theology, Latin, Mechanics, Politics, Civics, Law, Rhetoric, Philosophy, have all vanished from the school environment within my lifetime (No one school offered all of them to every participant, of course).

No-one at a school is learning everything about a subject, just an introductory smattering. Your teachers will be graduates in their specialties, understanding far more than they are required to impart to you.

The subjects offered at Secondary school are foundational. They are basic fundamental units with very wide applicability - Arithmetic is required in most other disciplines, for example, from score keeping in sports, through material usage in art, all the way up to calculating mechanical forces in physics, or reagent quantities in chemistry.

Some are culturally essential. For example, English Language (or the equivalent national tongue) is taught to ensure that pupils have a reasonable chance of fitting into a world where they are expected to know the difference between must and should in the Highway Code, and what the difference is between an estimate and a quotation is from a contractor. How to signify time, discriminate sarcasm from insult, identify fact from speculation.

Nor do we expect school children to plod on regardless with all topics throughout their school career. At some stage around 14 years old they will “drop” art, or geography, or history, or literature, to concentrate on their individual skills. At age 16 they will further refine their choices down to three or four that will lead to a preferred career. They will pick a university course that looks like a single topic (it isn’t, of course). It is a series of selections and specialisations as time goes on

The “subjects” taught at school are coarse approximations to human knowledge. Take modern genetics, for example. Even at undergraduate level it is across Biology, Chemistry, Statistics, and quite a lot of Physics. It requires smatterings of theology, law, culture, and even history to inform the Ethics of the work. The undergraduate needs to have been exposed to all of that, even if they were not examined in it.

Education is not a catechism, to be learned in rote; it is a buffet at which you can gorge, or pick, or sample, or evade, as suits you. It is not something to be endured, but something to be enjoyed, something full of surprises, unexpected stimulii, thrills (and disappointments), and self-discovery.

10. What is your expectation to your subject teacher?


Answer:

As a student, I expect my Subject Teacher to be an expert or knowledgeable on their teaching. At the same time, an excellent mix of witty and laid-back, yet not a softie. Students can get out of hand at times, and even if the student is me, I want them to reprimand a class or individual as needed. Aside from that, I believe that a superb teacher is kind, approachable, eager, and concerned. Who is approachable to everyone on campus, not just students. A teacher whom students know they can approach with any difficulties or worries, or even tell a joke.

When it comes to teaching, I expect my subject teacher to be knowledgeable and skilled in the subject and provide many real-life examples or scenarios in which the information can apply. Who can also create a way for us students to engage in the learning process. In short, he knows how to be resourceful in a creative way. Like giving ice breakers, it can be a game or activity before or in the middle of the class to get a feel for the subject content and expectations and for us to have fun while learning. A subject teacher should also know how to recognize their students for the progress that they have made, even if it is tiny. We also want to be praised and recognized for the things that we achieve or do because it motivates us to study more and be engaged on the subject.

Finally, I expect my subject teacher to enjoy what he/she does because it reflects how you teach, and students can tell that.

Subjects are subjects that must be taught (learned) for elementary school or high school. The benefits and functions of subjects, if the benefits of what is learned by students can be clearly identified and understood, will foster student motivation. The benefits of certain subjects are actually not only for now but can be useful for the future, or benefits not only when at school but can be useful when already working or in everyday life in society.
