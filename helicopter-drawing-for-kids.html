5. If the mass of a helicopter is 4,500 kg and the net force on it is 18,000 N, what is the helicopter acceleration


[tex]\tt{\huge{\blue{Explanation:}}}[/tex]

The force acting on an object is given by

[tex]\boxed{F = ma}[/tex]

where:

F = force

m = mass

a = acceleration

[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

Solving for a

F = ma

[tex]\dfrac{F}{m} = \dfrac{ma}{m}[/tex]

[tex]a = \dfrac{F}{m}[/tex]

[tex]a = \dfrac{\text{18,000 N}}{\text{4,500 kg}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{a = \text{4 m/}\text{s}^{2}}[/tex]

Therefore, the acceleration of the helicopter is 4 m/s².

14. A helicopter is flying at a height of 1.5km. If it has 50 x 10 6 Joules of potential energy, what is the mass of the helicopter?


The mass of the helicopter : 3401.36 kg

Further explanation

Potential energy (PE) is the energy that an object has because of the location of that object. Usually, it is related to the force of gravity

 [tex]\rm PE=m.g.h[/tex]

g = acceleration due to gravity (m/s²)

h = height (m)

m = mass (kg)

The higher the object from the surface of the earth and the greater the mass, the greater the potential energy of the object. Earth's gravity also affects the potential energy of objects

Height of helicopter : h = 1.5 km = 1500 m

Potential energy :PE = 50.10⁶ J

The mass of helicopter :

[tex]\rm mass=\dfrac{PE}{g.h}\\\\mass=\dfrac{50.10^6}{9.8\times 1500}\\\\mass=3401.36~kg[/tex]

30. A helicopter leaves Bristol at 10:00 It flies forts at 80nm/1. It lands for 30 minutes and then flies a further 65 kilometres in 30 minutes. The helicopter then immediately returns to its base in Bristol, flying at 100km/h. Draw a distance-time graph to show the journey.Need help plss​


Answer:

Explanation:First, let's break down the journey into three parts:

Bristol to the first landing spot

First landing spot to the second landing spot

Second landing spot back to Bristol

For the first part of the journey, the helicopter flies forwards at a speed of 80 knots per hour (nm/h). Since we don't know the distance from Bristol to the first landing spot, we'll label this as "Distance 1" on the graph. The time at which the helicopter reaches the first landing spot is 10:00 + the time it takes to travel "Distance 1" at a speed of 80 nm/h.

For the second part of the journey, the helicopter flies a further 65 kilometers (km) in 30 minutes. Since we know the distance and time, we can calculate the speed using the formula: Speed = Distance / Time. The speed for this part of the journey is 130 km/h. We'll label the distance from the first landing spot to the second landing spot as "Distance 2" on the graph. The time at which the helicopter reaches the second landing spot is the time it reached the first landing spot + 30 minutes + the time it takes to travel "Distance 2" at a speed of 130 km/h.

For the third part of the journey, the helicopter immediately returns to Bristol at a speed of 100 km/h. We'll label the distance from the second landing spot back to Bristol as "Distance 3" on the graph. The time at which the helicopter arrives back at Bristol is the time it reached the second landing spot + 30 minutes + the time it takes to travel "Distance 3" at a speed of 100 km/h.

On the distance-time graph, we can plot each part of the journey as a line segment with the corresponding distance and time values. The time values will be on the x-axis and the distance values will be on the y-axis. The three line segments will not be connected since there are breaks in between the flight segments due to the landings.
