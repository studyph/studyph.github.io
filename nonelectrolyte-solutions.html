Solution properties are the properties of a solution that don't depend on the type of solute but only on the concentration of the solute.  

The term is used in the Solution properties  

1. molal (m)

that is, the number of moles of solute in 1 kg of solvent  

 2. Boiling points  

Solutions from non-volatile substances have a higher boiling point and lower freezing points than the solvent  

ΔTb = Tb solution - Tb solvent

ΔTb = boiling point elevation  

  ΔTb = Kb. m  

Kb = molal boiling point increase  

m = molal solution

15 g of urea

100 g water

The boiling point

Tb water = 100 °C

mol urea(MW= 60,06 g/mol) :

= 15 g : 60.06 g/mol

= 0.25

The molality of solution :

= 0.25 : 0.1 kg water

= 2.5 m

The boiling point :

Tb solution - Tb solvent = Kb . m

Tb solution - 100 = 0.52 x 2.5

Tb solution = 101.3 °C

7. a water solution containing an unknown quantity of a nonelectrolyte solute is found to have a freezing point of -0.23°C. what is the molal concentration of the solution ​


Answer:

0.124 m

Explanation:

FPsoln → freezing point of the solution (in °C)

FPsolv → freezing point of the solvent (in °C)

∆Tf → freezing point depression (in °C)

kf → molal freezing point depression constant (in °C/m)

m → molality

Given: FPsoln = -0.23 °C

Required: m (molal concentration)

Equation: FPsoln = FPsolv - ∆Tf

∆Tf = m • kf

Solution:

For ∆Tf

∆Tf = FPsolv - FPsoln

∆Tf = 0 °C - (-0.23 °C)

∆Tf = 0.23 °C

For m

m = ∆Tf / kf

m = 0.23 °C / 1.86 °C/m

Answer: m = 0.124 m


8. A solution is prepared by dissolving 27.0 g of urea [(NH2)2CO], in 150.0 g of water. Calculate the boiling point of the solution. Urea is a nonelectrolyte.


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & mass_{\text{solute}} = \text{27.0 g} \\ & MM_{\text{solute}} = \text{60.056 g/mol} \\ & mass_{\text{solvent}} = \text{150.0 g = 0.1500 kg} \\ & K_{\text{b}} = 0.52^{\circ}\text{C/}m \\ & T^{\circ}_{\text{b}} = 100^{\circ}\text{C} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solute}} & = \frac{mass_{\text{solute}}}{MM_{\text{solute}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{27.0 g}}{\text{60.056 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{0.450 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molality of solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} m & = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{mass_{\text{solvent}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.450 mol}}{\text{0.1500 kg}} \\ & = \text{3.00 mol/kg} \\ & = 3.00 \: m \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the boiling-point elevation.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \Delta T_{\text{b}} & = K_{\text{b}}m \\ & = (0.52^{\circ}\text{C/}m)(3.00 \: m) \\ & = 1.56^{\circ}\text{C} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 5: Calculate the boiling point of the solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} T_{\text{b}} & = T^{\circ}_{\text{b}} + \Delta T_{\text{b}} \\ & = 100^{\circ}\text{C} + 1.56^{\circ}\text{C} \\ & = \boxed{101.56^{\circ}\text{C}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the boiling point of the solution is 101.56°C.

10. Introduction of electrolyte and nonelectrolyte


Answer:

An electrolyte is a compound that conducts an electric current when it is in an aqueous solution or melted. A nonelectrolyte is a compound that does not conduct an electric current in either aqueous solution or in the molten state.

12. It was found that 85.5 g of a certain nonelectrolyte dissolved in 1kg waterlowered the freezing point of water by 0.465°C. Find the molecular weightof the solute.​


Given:

[tex]\text{mass of solute = 85.5 g}[/tex]

[tex]\text{mass of solvent = 1 kg}[/tex]

[tex]∆T = \text{0.465°C}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]\text{molecular weight of solute}[/tex]

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the molality of solution.

[tex]∆T = k_{f}m[/tex]

[tex]m = \frac{∆T}{k_{f}}[/tex]

[tex]m = \frac{\text{0.465°C}}{\text{1.86°C/}m}[/tex]

[tex]m = 0.250 \: m[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]m = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent (in kg)}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = m × \text{mass of solvent (in kg)}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = 0.250 \: m × \text{ 1 kg}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute = 0.250 mol}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molecular weight of solute.

[tex]\text{molecular weight of solute} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{moles of solute}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molecular weight of solute} = \frac{\text{85.5 g}}{\text{0.250 mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{molecular weight of solute = 342 g/mol}}[/tex]

13. To make a solution, 40 g of a nonelectrolyte is dissolved into 300 g of water. The freezing point depression of the solution is measured to be -7°C. Calculate the molar mass of the solute.


Given:

[tex]\text{mass of solute = 40 g}[/tex]

[tex]\text{mass of solvent = 300 g = 0.300 kg}[/tex]

[tex]\text{FP}_{solution} = -\text{7°C}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]\text{molar mass of solute}[/tex]

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the freezing point depression.

[tex]\Delta T_{f} = \text{FP}_{solvent} - \text{FP}_{solution}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{f} = \text{0°C} - (-\text{7°C})[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{f} = \text{7°C}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molality of the solution.

[tex]\Delta T_{f} = k_{f}m[/tex]

[tex]m = \frac{\Delta T_{f}}{k_{f}}[/tex]

[tex]m = \frac{\text{7°C}}{(\text{1.86 °C/}m)}[/tex]

[tex]m = 3.7634 \: m[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = m × \text{mass of solvent}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = 3.7634 \: m × \text{0.300 kg}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute = 1.129 mol}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the molar mass of the solute.

[tex]\text{molar mass of solute} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{moles of solute}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molar mass of solute} = \frac{\text{40 g}}{\text{1.129 mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{molar mass of solute = 35.4 g/mol}}[/tex]

22. A solution of 35.7 g of a nonelectrolyte in 220.0 g of chloroform has a boiling point of 64.5 °C. What is themolar mass of this compound?​


Given:

mass of solute = 35.7 g

mass of solvent = 220.0 g = 0.2200 kg

[tex]\text{BP}_{solution} = \text{64.50°C}[/tex]

solute: non-electrolyte

solvent: chloroform

Required:

molar mass of solute

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the boiling point elevation.

Note: The boiling point of chloroform is 61.3°C.

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = \text{BP}_{solution} - \text{BP}_{solvent}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = \text{64.50°C} - \text{61.3°C}[/tex]

[tex]\Delta T_{\text{b}} = \text{3.20°C}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molality of solution.

Note: The value of Kb for chloroform is 3.63°C/molal.

[tex]\text{molality} = \frac{\Delta T_{\text{b}}}{K_{\text{b}}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molality} = \frac{\text{3.20°C}}{\text{3.63°C/molal}}[/tex]

molality = 0.881543 molal

Step 3: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

Note: 1 molal = 1 mol/kg

moles of solute = molality × mass of solvent

moles of solute = 0.881543 mol/kg × 0.2200 kg

moles of solute = 0.193939 mol

Step 4: Calculate the molar mass of solute.

[tex]\text{molar mass of solute} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{moles of solute}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molar mass of solute} = \frac{\text{35.7 g}}{\text{0.193939 mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{molar mass of solute = 184 g/mol}}[/tex]

24. What is the freezing point (°C) of asolution prepared by dissolving 50.0 g ofglycerin (C3H80, a nonelectrolyte) in 200.0 gof ethanol ?​


Solution:

Note that the solute is glycerin and the solvent is ethanol. The molal freezing point depression constant (kf) and the normal freezing point of ethanol are 1.99 °C/m and -117.3°C, respectively.

First, we calculate the number of moles of the solute. We write

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \text{50.0 g glycerin} × \frac{\text{1 mol glycerin}}{\text{92.094 g glycerin}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \text{0.54292 mol}[/tex]

Second, we calculate the molality of the solution. We write

[tex]m = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent (kg)}}[/tex]

[tex]m = \frac{\text{0.54292 mol}}{\text{0.2000 kg}}[/tex]

[tex]m = 2.7146 \: m[/tex]

Third, we calculate the freezing point depression. We write

[tex]∆T_{f} = mk_{f}[/tex]

[tex]∆T_{f} = (2.7146 \: m)(1.99 \: \frac{\text{°C}}{m})[/tex]

[tex]∆T_{f} = \text{5.40°C}[/tex]

Finally, we can calculate the freezing point of the solution. Therefore,

[tex]T_{f} = T°_{f} - ∆T_{f}[/tex]

[tex]T_{f} = -\text{117.3°C} - \text{5.40°C}[/tex]

[tex]\blue{T_{f} = -\text{122.7°C}}[/tex]

29. what is the vapor pressure at 25°C of a 20% aqueous solution of a nonelectrolyte with a molar mass of 121.3? assume 100g solution​


Answer:

P solution = 22.9 mmHg

Explanation:

Note: The vapor pressure of water at 25°C is 23.8 mmHg. Water is the solvent in this problem.

Assuming that the mass of solution is 100 g

mass solute = 20 g

mass solvent = 80 g

Calculating the number of moles of each component

n solute = 20 g × (1 mol / 121.3 g) = 0.1649 mol

n solvent = 80 g × (1 mol / 18.015 g) = 4.4407 mol

Calculating the total number of moles

n T = n solute + n solvent

n T = 0.1649 mol + 4.4407 mol

n T = 4.6056 mol

Calculating the mole fraction of the solvent (H2O)

X solvent = n solvent / n T

X solvent = 4.4407 mol / 4.6056 mol

X solvent = 0.9642

Calculating the vapor pressure of the solution

P solution = (X solvent)(P solvent)

P solution = (0.9642)(23.8 mmHg)

P solution = 22.9 mmHg
