14. According to the following reaction, how many grams of copper are required for the complete reaction of 24.5 grams of silver nitrate?[tex]\text{silver nitrate}(\text{aq}) + \text{copper(s)} \to \text{copper(II) nitrate(aq)} + \text{silver(s)}[/tex]


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

2AgNO₃ + Cu → Cu(NO₃)₂ + 2Ag

Step 2: Calculate the molar mass of AgNO₃.

The molar masses of Ag, N, and O are 107.9 g, 14.01 g, and 16.00 g, respectively.

[tex]\begin{aligned} MM_{\text{AgNO}_3} & = \text{107.9 g + 14.01 g + 3(16.00 g)} \\ & = \text{169.9 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mass of Cu reacted with AgNO₃.

The molar mass of Cu is 63.55 g.

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 2 moles of AgNO₃ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mole of Cu.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of Cu} & = \text{24.5 g} \: \text{AgNO}_3 \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{AgNO}_3}{\text{169.9 g} \: \text{AgNO}_3} \times \frac{\text{1 mol Cu}}{\text{2 mol} \: \text{AgNO}_3} \times \frac{\text{63.55 g Cu}}{\text{1 mol Cu}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{4.58 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 4.58 g of copper are required for the complete reaction of 24.5 grams of silver nitrate.

19. How many nitrogen atoms are in 10.2 g of copper ii nitrate hints?


Hello!

How many nitrogen atoms are in 10.2 g of copper ii nitrate hints?

data:

m1 (solute mass) = 10.2 g

Let's first find the molar mass of Copper II Nitrate:

MM - Molar Mass of Cu(NO3)2

Cu = 1*(63.5u) = 63.5 u

N = 2*(14u) = 28 u

O = 6*(16u) = 96 u

-----------------------------------

MM - Molar Mass of Cu(NO3)2 = 63.5 + 28 + 96 = 187.5 g/mol

* Let's find the number of moles, let's see:

[tex]n = \dfrac{m\:(g)}{MM\:(g/mol)}[/tex]

[tex]n = \dfrac{10.2\:\diagup\!\!\!\!\!g}{187.5\:\diagup\!\!\!\!\!g/mol}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{n \approx 0.054\:mol}[/tex]

* Knowing that by Avogadro's Law for each mole of a substance we have 6.022 * 10²³ molecules.

1 mol ------------------------ 6.022*10²³ molecules  

0.054 mol ------------------ y molecules  

[tex]\dfrac{1}{0.054} = \dfrac{6.022*10^{23}}{y}[/tex]

multiply the means by the extremes  

[tex]1*y = 0.054*6.022*10^{23}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{y = 3.25188*10^{22}\:molecules\:of\:Cu(NO_3)_2}[/tex]

* We know that an Copper II Nitrate molecule is formed by two Nitrogen atoms, so we have:

1 molecule of Cu(NO3)2 --------------------------------- 2 atoms of N

3.25188*10²² molecules of Cu(NO3)2 --------------- y atoms of N

[tex]\dfrac{1}{3.25188*10^{22}} = \dfrac{2}{y}[/tex]

multiply the means by the extremes  

[tex]1*y = 2*3.25188*10^{22}[/tex]

[tex]y = 6.50376*10^{22}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\boxed{y \approx 6.5*10^{22}\:Atoms\:of\:Nitrogen}}\end{array}}\qquad\checkmark[/tex]

Answer:  

Approximately 6.5*10²² atoms of nitrogen

21. Lead ii chloride + calcium nitrate formula


Lead ii chloride + calcium nitrate formula

Lead(II) nitrate is an inorganic compound with the chemical formula Pb(NO3). It commonly occurs as a colourless crystal or white powder and, unlike most other lead(II) salts, is soluble in water.

Known since the Middle Ages by the name plumb dulcis, the production of lead(II) nitrate from either metallic lead or lead oxide in nitric acid was small-scale, for direct use in making other lead compounds. In the nineteenth century lead(II) nitrate began to be produced commercially in Europe and the United States. Historically, the main use was as a raw material in the production of pigments for lead paints, but such paints have been superseded by less toxic paints based on titanium dioxide. Other industrial uses included heat stabilization in nylon and polyesters, and in coatings of photothermographic paper. Since around the year 2000, lead(II) nitrate has begun to be used in gold cyanidation.
