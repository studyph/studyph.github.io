4. How does the dream of every member of the younger family differ and agree with one another? In the story A Raisin in the Sun


A Raisin in the Sun is essentially about dreams, as the main characters struggle to deal with the oppressive circumstances that rule their lives. The title of the play references a conjecture that Langston Hughes famously posed in a poem he wrote about dreams that were forgotten or put off. He wonders whether those dreams shrivel up “like a raisin in the sun.” Every member of the Younger family has a separate, individual dream—Beneatha wants to become a doctor, for example, and Walter wants to have money so that he can afford things for his family. The Youngers struggle to attain these dreams throughout the play, and much of their happiness and depression is directly related to their attainment of, or failure to attain, these dreams. By the end of the play, they learn that the dream of a house is the most important dream because it unites the family.

THE NEED TO FIGHT RACIAL DISCRIMINATION

The character of Mr. Lindner makes the theme of racial discrimination prominent in the plot as an issue that the Youngers cannot avoid. The governing body of the Youngers’ new neighborhood, the Clybourne Park Improvement Association, sends Mr. Lindner to persuade them not to move into the all-white Clybourne Park neighborhood. Mr. Lindner and the people he represents can only see the color of the Younger family’s skin, and his offer to bribe the -Youngers to keep them from moving threatens to tear apart the Younger family and the values for which it stands. Ultimately, the Youngers respond to this discrimination with defiance and strength. The play powerfully demonstrates that the way to deal with discrimination is to stand up to it and reassert one’s dignity in the face of it rather than allow it to pass unchecked

7. story: a raisin in the sun question : how did the younger family deals with the vuca world?​nonsense report


Answer:

In the story "A Raisin in the Sun" by Lorraine Hansberry, the Younger family, which consists of Mama, Walter, Beneatha, and Ruth, struggle to deal with the unpredictable and uncertain nature of their world, often referred to as a "Vuca" world. The family lives in a small apartment in Chicago's South Side, and their everyday lives are characterized by poverty and racial discrimination.

Mama, the matriarch of the family, tries to hold the family together despite the challenges they face. She wants to use the life insurance money from her late husband to buy a house for the family and escape the cramped living conditions of their apartment. However, her son Walter wants to use the money to invest in a liquor store, which leads to conflicts and tension within the family.

Beneatha, Mama's daughter, is trying to find her own identity as an African-American woman in a white-dominated society. She rejects traditional gender roles and wants to become a doctor. Beneatha's quest for self-discovery and her rejection of traditional gender roles also causes conflicts within the family.

Ruth, Walter's wife, is trying to maintain a sense of stability for her family. She becomes pregnant and faces difficult decisions about her future and that of her unborn child. Ruth also deals with the uncertainty of her husband's business ventures and the family's financial situation.

All of these factors combined create a sense of uncertainty and unpredictability for the Younger family, making it challenging for them to navigate their daily lives and plan for the future. Throughout the play, the family struggles to come to terms with the vuca world they live in and make choices that will determine their future.

Character Analysis Walter Lee Younger ("Brother")

Essentially, this play is the story of Walter Lee Younger, sometimes called "Brother." Passionate, ambitious, and bursting with the energy of his dreams, Walter Lee is a desperate man, shackled by poverty and prejudice, and obsessed with a business idea that he thinks will solve all of his economic and social problems. He believes, for example, that through his business idea, he will suddenly accumulate all the money he will ever need. 29. what is the style of the story a raisin in the sun??what is the tone of the story a raisin in the sun??​


Answer:

The style of A Raisin in the Sun is direct and colloquial. Lorraine Hansberry wrote the play as a realist drama, meaning that she attempted to capture the everyday reality of her subjects, a working-class Black family living in South Side, Chicago, sometime in the late 1940s or 1950s.
