20. Wilhelm Roentgen discovered


Wilhelm Conrad Roentgen discovered modern technology's X-ray in his laboratory in Würzburg, Germany on November 8,1895.

Wilhelm Roentgen, a German professor of physics, was the first person to discover electromagnetic radiation in a wavelength range commonly known as X-rays today. Although, many people had observed the effects of X-ray beams before, Roentgen was the first one to study them systematically. To highlight the unknown nature of his discovery, he called them X-rays though they are still known as Roentgen-rays as well. For his remarkable achievement he was honored with the first he first Nobel Prize in Physics in 1901.

26. Imbentions of Marie Curie?


What are the inventions of Marie Curie?

Marie Curie is not only an inventor but rather a discoverer.

You will be surprised to know that the humble physicist performed all her research and experiments in a laboratory that was not as sophisticated as her male peers. The makeshift laboratory was previously a medical dissection room, and it did not have good ventilation. The world of Scientific discovery was dominated by males, but Marie Curie excelled and even surpassed her peers.

With her husband's invention, the electrometer, she studied the behavior of uranium. This is where she discovered that electricity was conducted through the air in the presence of uranium samples. This led to her hypothesis that the atoms emit radiation and thus are indivisible.

Due to this discovery, Marie Curie wanted to perform more studies on the element and gathered more uranium minerals for use. She observed that these minerals emitted a higher amount of radiation. She set out to discover and isolate the cause. This led to her discovery of new elements: Radium and Polonium.

Not one to rest, Curie decided to identify radioactive elements. She proved that Thorium was another radioactive element.

Marie Curie the Humanitarian

X-ray machines were invented by Wilhelm Rontgen but were not easily accessible. Marie Curie wanted to help out French troops fighting in World War I. She knew that time was of the essence when treating wounded soldiers. Even without the help of the government, she put her mind to the task of building portable X-ray machines.

With the help of the Union of Women of France, the Red Cross, and her wealthy connections, she was eventually able to set up 200 units of radiology units in field hospitals, 20 vehicles with mobile x-ray machines, and even train 150 female volunteers.
