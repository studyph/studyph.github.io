1. Explain and give an example for the stage of ANGER ​


Answer:

Anger is an emotion with various levels of intensity. It is a feeling that consists of annoyance, displeasure, aggravation, and hostility. When someone is angry, they might go from slightly annoyed to downright enraged. At the same time, there are levels of anger issues based on how intense the feeling of anger is, but the stages of anger deal with the steps of or leading up to an angry emotional reaction. These stages fixate on the length of time it takes for anger to unravel from the escalation of this emotion to its aftereffects.

Anger can affect someone inwardly and outwardly. As an emotion, it has the potential to raise blood pressure, cause headaches, or even increase one's body temperature. Outwardly, however, anger can cause someone to raise their voice, tremble, clench their jaw, sweat, or pace.

The four stages of anger are the buildup, the spark, the explosion, and the aftermath. Within these stages lies the possibility of deescalating an angry reaction before a situation gets out of hand. How a person in the anger stage will act is dependent upon if someone recognizes a person in the first stage of anger, for example. They might be able to aid the person from reaching a stage of anger, such as the explosion stage, that may cause a person or the ones around them immense emotional distress.

Explanation:

The BuildupThe first stage of anger is the buildup, which serves as the foundation of an angry reaction. Anger is built upon this foundation as the factors behind the buildup can explain its presence entirely. The buildup stage of anger may be rooted in internal thoughts about oneself, such as pessimism about the past or future, lowly thoughts about oneself regarding self-worth or capability, or an overall negative attitude about themselves and others or their environment. These internal thoughts can translate into a physical response, such as physical signs of stress (sweating, clenching one's fists, frowning, scowling).The SparkThe spark embodies a "thing" that ignites an outburst of anger. It can be internal or external, but both serve as a product of the buildup of anger. Internally, a child might think of something that causes them to be angry. Externally, a child might not hold their thoughts and speak them aloud angrily, including inward thoughts regarding self-worth, body image, or overall pessimistic thoughts based upon past experiences or one's environment. These thoughts can easily manifest into an angry, physical response, such as throwing things, breaking things, or physical violence.For example, let's say two children are playing a game that requires a competitive drive. One child grows up in a household that places high expectations on them, where any situation that involves losing makes them feel inferior and unworthy. On the other hand, the other child grows up in a household with parents that encourage them to do their best, regardless of winning or losing. The first child with the parents who have high expectations loses. Based on the expectations and low self-esteem of the child (both of which serve as the spark of their anger), they react with anger.

The ExplosionThis stage of anger illustrates the outburst of anger or the display of anger in a verbal and/or physical manner. It might entail yelling, screaming, physical violence, and cursing, among other aspects that cause an explosion of anger. In this stage, de-escalation is vital as the person who is displaying their anger might feel distressed if they hurt another person's feelings or cause physical harm to another in this stage. To ensure the intensity of this stage is taken down, the person who is having an outburst might be able to calm down by leaving the room or taking deep breaths to center themselves.

The AftermathWith anger comes issues that need to be resolved or discussed to ensure no harm is done to oneself or others. In the aftermath stage, problem-solving is incorporated to make sure that issues are addressed and that the buildup and spark of anger do not lead to an intense emotional reaction. If these problems are not addressed, a person who reacts angrily in outbursts may repeat this cycle of anger which only causes more harm than good.

13. Which stage of loss and grief is most closely associated with good mental and emotional health? A. Depression B. Anger C. Acceptance D. Denial


The stage of loss and grief that is most closely associated with good mental and emotional health is C. Acceptance. This is the last stage of the Kübler-Ross model, otherwise known as the Five Stages of Grief (trademarked) where it posits that a person who lost someone, undergoing grief, goes through five steps famously known as DABDA. This is described in a bit more detail below:

Stages of Grief

Denial - one does not accept that a loved one has died.

Anger - anger is shown, whether towards the self, others or a higher being

Bargaining - usually negotiating with God that the death does not happen

Depression - feelings of despair

Acceptance - finally coming to terms with the death

When undergoing grief, it is important that people seek help if help is needed, especially if depression is very evident as this could cause deeper mental problems later on.
