The Little Albert experiment violated ethical codes and participant's rights by using a nine-month-old infant without informed consent, causing undue harm, lacking beneficence, violating confidentiality, and lacking transparency. 24. B. Give the contributions of the following philosophers: 1. John Dewey 2. John Watson 3. William Bagley 4. Jean Paul Sartre 5. Robert Hutchins 6. Jurgen Habermas.​


Answer:

1. John Dewey: Dewey was an American philosopher who is widely regarded as one of the founders of pragmatism. He made significant contributions to the fields of education, philosophy, and psychology. Dewey believed in the importance of experiential learning and believed that education should be centered around the needs and interests of the student. He also argued that education should be seen as a means of social reform and that schools should be viewed as laboratories for democracy.

2. John Watson: Watson was an American psychologist who is known for his work in behaviorism. He believed that all behavior could be explained in terms of conditioning and that psychology should focus on observable behavior rather than mental processes. Watson's work had a significant impact on the field of psychology, and he is considered one of the founders of behaviorism.

3. William Bagley: Bagley was an American educator and philosopher who is known for his contributions to the field of educational essentialism. He believed that schools should focus on teaching students a core set of knowledge and skills, which he referred to as the "cultural heritage." Bagley believed that education should be centered around the transmission of knowledge from teacher to student and that schools should have a strong focus on discipline and order.

4. Jean Paul Sartre: Sartre was a French philosopher who is known for his work in existentialism. He argued that human beings are fundamentally free and that we create our own meaning and purpose in life. Sartre believed that individuals should take responsibility for their actions and that we must live with the consequences of our choices. His work had a significant impact on philosophy, literature, and politics.

5. Robert Hutchins: Hutchins was an American educator and philosopher who is known for his contributions to the field of liberal education. He believed that education should be centered around the liberal arts and that students should be exposed to a broad range of subjects. Hutchins argued that education should be focused on teaching students how to think critically and analytically.

6. Jurgen Habermas: Habermas is a German philosopher who is known for his work in critical theory and communicative action. He argued that society should be organized around the principles of democracy and that communication is the key to social change. Habermas believed that individuals should be free to express their opinions and that we must strive for an open and inclusive public sphere where all voices are heard. His work has had a significant impact on the fields of philosophy, sociology, and political science.

Who is Jean Watson? What is Watson's Theory in Nursing?​

Jean Watson was born on June 10, 1940. She is an American nurse theorist and nursing professor who is best known for her book "Transpersonal Caring - The Philosophy and Science of Caring," as well as the theory of Human Caring. Caring is what distinguishes nursing. Nursing at Redlands Community Hospital has incorporated Jean Watson's Caring Science approach. Caring Science assists us in embracing the wonderful energy that comes from a well-balanced mind, body, and spirit, and it benefits both the patient and the nurse in the long run. Nurses are uniquely positioned to be the heart of healing, according to Florence Nightingale's idea that the "job of a nurse is to place her patient in the greatest situation to be able to self-heal." The nurse is able to maximize her patient's potential to heal from within by actively participating in caring via true presence and intentionality. In an overstressed and pressured profession, how can we as nurses preserve emotional sensitivity and caring attitudes? Caring, according to Jean Watson, re-energizes our lives and enhances our skills. The advantages are immense, and they foster personal and professional self-actualization. Both the patient and the nurse, and among the other members of the health team, caring is a mutually rewarding experience. It's also worth remembering that Watson emphasizes the need of taking care of ourselves before we can take care of others; self-healing is a critical step in recharging our spiritual bank and renewing our energy reserves. You may live more truly in your relationships if you reveal your actual ideas, feelings, and attitudes. Patients and customers are happier when they are treated with compassion. It's contagious, and it makes people feel good about themselves. It evokes a sense of awe and intuition. It's uplifting and inspiring.
