2. . Some magnets have the letters N and S painted on either end. What is the name given to the ends of magnet?​


Answer:

The ends of a magnet that are labeled with the letters N and S are commonly referred to as the poles of the magnet. The end labeled with "N" stands for North pole, and the end labeled with "S" stands for South pole. These poles are fundamental properties of magnets and play a crucial role in their behavior and interactions with other magnets and magnetic materials. The North pole of one magnet attracts the South pole of another magnet, while like poles (North-North or South-South) repel each other. The concept of magnetic poles is essential in understanding the principles of magnetism and is widely used in various applications of magnets, such as in electric motors, generators, compasses, and magnetic storage devices.

In the periodic table above, black squares indicate elements which are solids at room temperature (about 22ºC)*, those in blue squares are liquids at room temperature, and those in red squares are gases at room temperature.

Most of the metals are solids under "ordinary" conditions (i.e., 25ºC, 1 atmosphere of pressure, etc.), with the exception of mercury (Hg, element 80), which solidifies at -39ºC, and is a freely-flowing liquid at room temperature. Gallium (Ga, element 31) melts at 30ºC, slightly above room temperature, but is often indicated as a liquid on periodic tables, since the solid metal literally melts when held in the hand (since body temperature is about 37ºC). (Since cesium melts at 28ºC, and francium at 27ºC, they are also indicated in blue on some tables, but anyone who holds cesium in their hands won't be holding much of anything afterwards! See the page on alkali metals for more on cesium's high reactivity.)

Several of the nonmetals are gases in their elemental form. Elemental hydrogen (H, element 1), nitrogen (N, element 7), oxygen (O, element 8), fluorine (F, element 9), and chlorine (Cl, element 17) are all gases at room temperature, and are found as diatomic molecules (H2, N2, O2, F2, Cl2). Bromine (Br, element 35), also found as a diatomic molecule (Br2), is a liquid at room temperature, solidifying at -7.2ºC. The noble gases of Group 8A (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, and Rn) are all gases at room temperature (as the name of the group implies); since they are all unreactive, monatomic elements, their boiling points are extremely low.

Justify your answer with both sentences and equations. Find the fundamental frequency of the pipe. Find the length of the pipe. Assume the speed of sound in air to be 343 m/s.


Answer:

[tex]L=0.327m, f_{1}=262Hz,[/tex], the pipe is closed at one end.

Step by step explanation:

The pipe is resonating thanks to a standing wave inside of it.

The frequencies, the pipe can resonate in, are given by the formulas:

1. [tex]f_{n}=nf_{1}, n=1,2,3,...[/tex] the harmonics in a pipe with both ends open or both ends closed.

2. [tex]f_{n}=(2n-1)f_{1}, n=1,2,3,...[/tex] the harmonics in a pipe with one end open and one closed(doesn't matter which).

where [tex]f_{1}[/tex] is the fundamental frequency.

Now we will test the values of the given frequencies and verify through trial and error:

We will choose the second case where the values given by the formula are:

[tex]f_{2}=3f_{1}[/tex] ⇔[tex]f_{1}=f_{2}/3[/tex]

[tex]f_{3}=5f_{1}[/tex] ⇔[tex]f_{1}=f_{3}/5[/tex]

[tex]f_{4}=7f_{1}[/tex] ⇔[tex]f_{1}=f_{4}/7[/tex]

etc...

For [tex]n=3,4,5[/tex] and we get:

[tex]1310/5=262,1834/7=262,2358/9=262[/tex]

So all of them are equal to the same number which verifies that:

The pipe is closed at one end.

The fundamental frequency is [tex]f_{1}=262Hz[/tex]

And the frequencies given are: [tex]f_{3}=1310Hz,f_{4}=1834Hz,f_{5}=2358Hz[/tex]


Now, we want to find the length of the pipe:

The formula for the pipe with one open end is:

L=(2n-1)λ/4, n=1,2,3,...

For n=3, L=5λ/4, where λ here is given by the wave equation and the respective frequency:

u=λf⇔λ=u/f

L=5λ/4 ⇔L=5u/(4f)=5*343/(4*1310)=0.327m
