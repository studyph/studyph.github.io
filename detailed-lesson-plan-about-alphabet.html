12. Research about the Korean Alphabet. ​


Answer:

The Korean alphabet, known as Hangul, was created during the 15th century under the rule of King Sejong. It was developed to help increase literacy rates among the common people, as the existing writing systems in Korea at the time, Hanja and Gugyeol, were difficult to learn and only accessible to the educated elite.

Hangul is a phonetic alphabet consisting of 14 consonants and 10 vowels. The consonants are written vertically or horizontally, while the vowels are written in a circle or a square depending on the shape of the syllable block. Each syllable block represents one sound, and the blocks are combined to form words.

The creation of Hangul had a significant impact on Korean society, as it allowed for greater access to education and helped to promote the spread of Korean culture and literature. Today, Hangul remains the official writing system of Korea, and is also used in the ethnic Korean communities in China, Japan, and Russia.

In 2009, UNESCO designated Hangul as a Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity, recognizing its cultural significance and contribution to the world's linguistic diversity.
