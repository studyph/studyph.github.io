12. 35-40. What is the Total Energy Requirement (TER) of a 16 years old student with a weight of 50 kg performing a moderate festival dancing activity? Show your solution C. 2000 cal D. 2500 cal A. 1000 cal B. 1500 cal


Answer:

The answer is B. 1500 cal, but the correct answer is 1418 calories.

Explanation:

To calculate the Total Energy Requirement (TER) of a 16-year-old student with a weight of 50 kg performing a moderate festival dancing activity, we can use the following formula:

TER = BMR x PAL

Where BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the amount of energy needed for the body to perform basic functions, and PAL (Physical Activity Level) is the factor that takes into account the energy needed for physical activities.

For a 16-year-old student with a weight of 50 kg, the BMR can be calculated using the Harris-Benedict equation:

BMR = 10 x weight (kg) + 6.25 x height (cm) - 5 x age (years) + 5 (for females) or + 161 (for males)

Assuming a height of 160 cm and a female student, the BMR can be calculated as:

BMR = 10 x 50 + 6.25 x 160 - 5 x 16 + 5 = 1050 calories

To determine the PAL for moderate festival dancing activity, we can consult the Compendium of Physical Activities, which lists the energy cost of different activities. For moderate festival dancing, the energy cost is estimated to be 4.5 METs (Metabolic Equivalent of Task).

Thus, the PAL can be calculated as:

PAL = 1.2 + 0.03 x MET = 1.2 + 0.03 x 4.5 = 1.35

Finally, the TER can be calculated as:

TER = BMR x PAL = 1050 x 1.35 = 1418 calories

Therefore, the answer is not in the choices provided. The closest answer is B. 1500 cal, but the correct answer is 1418 calories.

30. If a 1,000-kg car was moving at 30 m/s, what would be its kinetic energy expressed in the unusual (for kinetic energy) units of calories? (1 cal = 4.186 J)


Answer:

The kinetic energy is defined by

[tex]KE=\dfrac{1}{2} mv^2[/tex]

where

[tex]KE[/tex] = kinetic energy

[tex]m[/tex] = mass of an object

[tex]v[/tex] = speed of an object

Given: [tex]m[/tex] = 1000 kg, [tex]v[/tex] = 30 m/s

[tex]KE=\dfrac{1}{2} (1000\:\textrm{kg})(30\:\textrm{m/s})^2=450,000\:\textrm{J}[/tex]

Converting the unit, we get

[tex]450,000\:\textrm{J}\times\dfrac{1\:\textrm{cal}}{4.186\:\textrm{J}}=107,501.19\:\textrm{cal}[/tex]
