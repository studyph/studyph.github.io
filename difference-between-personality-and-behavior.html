5-9. Factors that affect a person's attitude.
1. Socialization: The process by which individuals learn and internalize the values, beliefs, and norms of their culture or society.

2. Personal experience: Personal experiences can shape a person's attitudes towards a particular person, thing, or event.

3. Education: Formal education can influence a person's attitudes by providing them with knowledge and information about different topics.

4. Culture: A person's culture can shape their attitudes towards a particular person, thing, or event.

5. Media: The media can influence a person's attitudes towards a particular person, thing, or event by shaping their perceptions and providing them with information and messages about the topic.

10-12. Nature refers to the genetic and biological factors that influence a person's development, while nurture refers to the environmental factors such as upbringing, education, and social experiences that shape a person's behavior and personality. Attitude formation is influenced by both nature and nurture, as a person's genetic makeup and upbringing can affect their predisposition to certain attitudes, while their environment and experiences can shape and reinforce those attitudes. For example, a person may have a genetic predisposition towards being more optimistic or pessimistic, but their upbringing and experiences can also influence their overall attitude towards life.

13-15. Attitudes can be characterized in several ways, including:

- Positive or negative: Attitudes can be characterized as positive or negative depending on a person's overall evaluation of an attitude object.
- Explicit or implicit: Attitudes can be explicit, which are conscious and deliberate, or implicit, which are unconscious and automatic.
- Direct or indirect: Attitudes can be directly related to the attitude object or indirectly related to beliefs about the attitude object.
- Evaluative, cognitive, or behavioral: Attitudes can be evaluative, involving a person's overall evaluation of an attitude object, cognitive, involving a person's beliefs and thoughts about an attitude object, or behavioral, involving a person's actions and behavior towards an attitude object.

Attitudes can also be characterized as being specific or general, enduring or temporary, and consistent or inconsistent. Specific attitudes are related to specific objects or situations, while general attitudes are more abstract and can be applied to a wide range of objects or situations. Enduring attitudes are relatively stable over time, while temporary attitudes may be more short-lived. Consistent attitudes are congruent with a person's beliefs and behaviors, while inconsistent attitudes may be contradictory or conflicting.
