9. God has revealed himself to Jacob ( Genesis 28 ). However, Jacob's faith in God depended on the fulfillment of certain conditions ( Genesis 28:20 - 22 ). Complete Jacob's faith responce to Yahweh God with that of his grandfather Abraham. How different are they?


Answer:

After commemorating the holiness of the place where the Lord visited him in a dream (Genesis 28:12–13), Jacob now acknowledges the content of what the Lord said to him. He does this in the form of a conditional vow, declaring that if the Lord will do these things, Jacob will respond with actions of his own.

Jacob's vow should be seen not as a testing of the Lord so much as a prayer of faith. Jacob seems to be saying that since the Lord can do these things, and has said He will do these things, Jacob is willing to trust and obey. Specifically, Jacob mentions God being with him and keeping him safe. He also adds two things God did not say explicitly: providing for him bread and clothing. In the following verses, he will mention one more condition and then the commitments he is binding himself to fulfill for God.

16. the difference of the story of creation of Genesis 1 and Genesis 2


Genesis Creation

Differences between Genesis 1 and Genesis 2

If you take a peek at Genesis 1, you will notice that God is always God. No surprises there. Consider the first day of creation in Genesis 1. First, “God created the heavens and the earth” (Genesis 1:1). Then, “God said, ‘Let there be light'” (Genesis 1:3). And, “God called the light Day, and the darkness he called Night” (Genesis 1:5). No surprises, right?

Now, flip the page to Genesis 2, and you will notice something different. First, “the LORD God made the earth and the heavens” (Genesis 2:4). Then, “the LORD God formed man from the dust of the ground” (Genesis 2:7). And, “the LORD God planted a garden in Eden” (Genesis 2:8).

Genesis 1 consistently refers to God with the simple, expected word for a/the deity: God. However, Genesis 2 consistently refers to God with the divine name, Yahweh, which Bible translations render as the the capitalized “LORD,” plus the expected word “God.” In other words, Genesis 1 calls God “God,” whereas Genesis 2 calls God “Yahweh God.”
