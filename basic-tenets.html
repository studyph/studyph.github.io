20. the basic tenets of confucianism and taoism have it's foundation ​


Answer:

There are three main tenets of Confucianism: ren, li, and zhong. Ren can be translated as “humaneness”, “compassion”, or “ love”. It is the principle of treating others with kindness and respect. Li is often translated as “ritual”, “etiquette”, or “propriety”. It refers to the importance of upholding social norms and customs. Zhong can be translated as “loyalty”, “duty”, or “obligation”. It is the principle of being faithful to one’s family, friends, and country.

The origins of Confucianism can be traced back to the Chinese philosopher Confucius (551-479 BCE). Confucius was born into a time of great political turmoil in China. He believed that the key to restoring order was for people to follow the principles of ren, li, and zhong.

Thaoism began with Lao Tzu, a man who lived in China around the 6th century BCE. Lao Tzu was a philosopher and is credited with writing the Tao Te Ching, the central text of Thaoism. The Tao Te Ching lays out the Thaoist philosophy, which is based on the idea of living in harmony with the Tao, or the “Way”.

The three main tenets of Taoism are: wu wei, ziran, and pu. Wu wei can be translated as “non-action” or “effortless action”. It is the principle of acting in accordance with the Tao, without forcing things to happen. Ziran can be translated as “naturalness” or “spontaneity”. It is the idea of living in harmony with the natural world. Pu can be translated as “simplicity” or “unpretentiousness”. It is the principle of living a humble and uncomplicated life.

21. How do the basic tenets of mahayana buddhism differ from theravada buddhism


How the Basic Tenets of Mahayana Buddhism Differ From Theravada Buddhism

The basic tenet of Mahayana Buddhism include their belief that an individual would reach enlightenment if, one, they would faithfully follow the teachings of Buddha and, two, they would stay in the cycle of samsara in order to help others who are in need. Meanwhile, this basic tenet of Mahayana Buddhism differs from Theravada Buddhism as the latter teaches its believers that enlightenment will be achieved as they reach Nirvana. If an individual reaches the state of Nirvana, he would be free from the cycle of samsara.

Additional Tenets of Mahayana Buddhism and Theravada Buddhism

Here are further characteristics that explain how the basic tenets of Mahayana Buddhism differ from Theravada Buddhism:

Mahayana BuddhismThis belief system is also called "The Greater Vehicle" as it teaches an individual to achieve not only self-enlightenment but the enlightenment of others.Mahayana Buddhists strive to become a Bodhisattva through Six Perfection.

Theravada BuddhismThis belief structure is also called "The School of Elders".Theravada Buddhists strive to become an Arhat. Arhats are believed to be fully awakened beings who follow the Noble Eightfold Path.
