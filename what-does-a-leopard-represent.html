14. What makes "The Leopard" folklore?​


Answer:

The Leopard Folklore from Central Africa

The Leopard Folklore from Central Africa is a story about courage among the characters. The three main characters in the story are Ki, the husband of Luesi; Luesi, the wife of Ki; and Ntio, the brother of Ki who are living with them. In the story, it was mentioned that the characters are Pygmy people that are short in stature. Luesi is from another village who wanted to visit her mother. Ki agreed and gave Luesi a piece of meat to take with her. He couldn't go with her but promised to fetch her after four weeks.

Rising Action, Climax, Falling Action, and Resolution of The Leopard Folklore

To summarize the events, here are the order of events happened in The Leopard Folklore from Central Africa:

Rising Action

Afterwards, Ki was bitten by a snake and couldn't fetch Luesi.

So, he asked his brother, Ntio, to go and get her instead.

Ntio and Luesi walked the way home when a leopard suddenly appeared.

Climax

The leopard made a stance to attack.

Ntio threw himself on the leopard and stabbed its heart.

The leopard fell but ripped Ntio's shoulder.

The leopard died and Luesi thought of testing the bravery and love of Ki for her.

She then made a story with Ntio.

Falling Action

Ntio ran to Ki's house and told him about the leopard's attack.

He relayed the story in the words of Luesi.

Ki ran into the scene and made a jump for the leopard with his foot still badly hurt.

Resolution

With what Ki has done, Luesi proved that her husband truly loves her and Ki is also courageous.

In the story, the three proved to be brave when faced with life or death situation.

Hyenas and wild dogs live in large groups called packs. They usually do everything together as a group, and that includes competing with and attacking rival predators such as leopards.

Leopards are solitary animals, meaning that live by themselves. Everything a leopard does to survive is done on its own - hunting, travelling, exploring, rearing young etc. If a leopard is badly injured as a result of a fight with another rival predator, it could spell certain doom for it, because it will not be able to hunt properly or even hunt at all, and it will eventually starve to death. Even worse, if it is a badly injured female mother leopard with cubs, the cubs will also starve to death too. The leopard cannot depend on another leopard to help it (or its cubs if a mothering female) hunt and catch food to eat, so if it gets into a fight with a pack of hyenas or wild dogs and gets badly injured, it will almost certainly be doomed. Why does the leopard need to waste valuable energy and time fight a pack or hyenas or wild dogs in a fight it would certainly lose since it is outnumbered ? The answer is that in most instances, the leopard would not bother to fight and it would be wise enough to flee unscathed to stay in a healthy and fit condition to carry on living. The way the leopard sees it, it is better to use it’s energy and time to hunt animals that it can easily overpower kill for its food.

One-on-one, a leopard is easily capable of killing a wild dog, and a fully-grown male leopard is certainly capable of killing an fully-grown adult hyena, both male and female. But even then most instances, a leopard would not bother do that and risk being badly injured, it would still rather wisely flee. And there is always a high chance that a lone wild dog or hyena has it’s pack members nearby that may hear it and come to its aid to mob the leopard (the leopard knows this too). However, leopards have been known to kill lone adult wild dogs and hyenas in one-on-one confrontations.

23. what is the conflict and theme of the leopard​


Answer:

Human killing is the decisive and most critical expression of human-leopard conflict and needs to be addressed sensitively to maintain local support for leopard conservation in India. This research was undertaken to investigate the ecological aspects of human killing and injury, spatial characteristic and pattern of such sites, temporal and seasonal trends of attacks and perception of local communities towards leopard in the Indian Himalayan region (IHR). We surveyed two sites i) Pauri Garhwal in the western part and ii) North Bengal (Dooars) in the eastern part of IHR, compiled secondary data on human-leopard conflict records and made field visits to (N = 101) conflict sites. We also conducted (N = 186) semi-structured questionnaire surveys in each of the sites to assess perception of local communities towards leopard. We analyzed the conflict data using rare events model in a binary logistic regression framework to understand spatial patterns of such incidents for Pauri Garhwal and North Bengal. The average number of injuries and deaths to leopard attacks in Pauri was estimated to be 11 (SE 1.13) and 3 (SE 0.6) per year between 2006–2016 whereas in North Bengal it was estimated to be 70 (SE 9.2) and 1.6 (SE 0.3) respectively between 2004–2016. About 97% of the leopard attacks in North Bengal and 60% of the leopard attacks in Pauri resulted in human injuries. Majority of the leopard attack victims in Pauri were children and young people, whereas in North Bengal it was middle aged tea estate workers. Attack on humans in Pauri were recorded mostly near areas with dense scrub cover whereas in North Bengal it was reported within tea-estates. The percentage of human deaths to leopard attacks in Pauri were higher (40%) compared to a mere (3%) in North Bengal. Forty-one percent of respondents in Pauri and 75% in North Bengal were positive towards presence and conservation of leopard. A predictive risk map revealed central and northern regions of Pauri Garhwal and protected areas, peripheral areas in central and south-western dooars (North Bengal) as high “human-leopard conflict risk zones”. This analytical procedure can be adopted in other sites to identify potential human-carnivore conflict risk zones.
