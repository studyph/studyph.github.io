1. Two point charges q1 and q2 are separated by a distance of 1.00 m. Find the neutral point if q1 = -3 nC and q2 = 4 nC.​


To find the neutral point, we need to locate the point between q1 and q2 where the electric field from q1 is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction to the electric field from q2. At that point, the two fields will cancel each other out, resulting in zero net electric field and hence no force on a test charge placed at that point.

We can use the principle of superposition to find the electric fields from each charge separately, and then equate them algebraically to find the neutral point. The electric field E from a point charge q at a distance r from it is given by:

E = k*q/r^2

where k is the Coulomb constant (k = 9.0 x 10^9 N*m^2/C^2).

For q1 = -3 nC at a distance of x from it, the electric field is:

E1 = k*q1/x^2

For q2 = 4 nC at a distance of (1.00-x) m from it, the electric field is:

E2 = k*q2/(1.00-x)^2

At the neutral point, these two fields are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction, so we have:

E1 = -E2

Substituting the expressions for E1 and E2, we get:

k*q1/x^2 = -k*q2/(1.00-x)^2

Simplifying and solving for x, we get:

x = sqrt(k*q2/(q1+q2)) = 0.378 m (approx)

Therefore, the neutral point is located at a distance of 0.378 m from q1, or equivalently at a distance of 0.622 m from q2.


2. What will be the charge of q1 if an 18-μC point charge is separated from it by a distance of 66.19 cm? Assume that the electric field between the two charges at point A 21-cm away eastward of q1 is equal to 4.67 X 108 N/C.


We can use Coulomb's law to solve this problem. Coulomb's law states that the magnitude of the electrostatic force between two point charges is proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. Mathematically, it can be expressed as:

F = k * q1 * q2 / r^2

where F is the magnitude of the electrostatic force, k is Coulomb's constant (8.99 x 10^9 N m^2/C^2), q1 and q2 are the charges of the two point charges, and r is the distance between them.

In this problem, we are given q2 = 18 μC, r = 66.19 cm = 0.6619 m, and the electric field at point A, which is located 21 cm = 0.21 m eastward of q1, is E = 4.67 x 10^8 N/C.

The electric field at point A is related to the force on a test charge q1 at that point through the equation:

E = F / q1

Therefore, we can solve for the magnitude of the electrostatic force F on q1 at point A:

F = E * q1 = 4.67 x 10^8 N/C * q1

Substituting this expression for F into Coulomb's law, we get:

4.67 x 10^8 N/C * q1 = k * q1 * q2 / r^2

Solving for q1, we get:

q1 = r^2 * E / k * q2

Plugging in the values, we get:

q1 = (0.6619 m)^2 * 4.67 x 10^8 N/C / (8.99 x 10^9 N m^2/C^2) * 18 x 10^-6 C

q1 ≈ 7.13 x 10^-8 C

Therefore, the charge of q1 is approximately 7.13 nC.

7. 1. Two-point charges, q1 = +9uC and q2 = 4uC, are separated by a distance r = 12 c⁸m. What is the magnitude of the electric force? 2. What must be the distance between point charge q1= 26.0µC and point charge q2=−47.0µCf or the electrostatic force between them to have a magnitude of 5.70 N?​


1. To calculate the magnitude of the electric force between the two point charges, we can use Coulomb's Law:

F = k * (q1 * q2) / r^2

where F is the electric force, k is Coulomb's constant (9 x 10^9 N m^2 / C^2), q1 and q2 are the magnitudes of the charges, and r is the distance between the charges.

Substituting the given values, we get:

F = (9 x 10^9 N m^2 / C^2) * [(+9 x 10^-6 C) * (4 x 10^-6 C)] / (12 x 10^-8 m)^2

F = 2.25 N

Therefore, the magnitude of the electric force between the two point charges is 2.25 N.

2. To calculate the distance between the two point charges, we can use Coulomb's Law and solve for the distance:

F = k * (q1 * q2) / r^2

r = sqrt(k * (q1 * q2) / F)

where r is the distance between the charges, F is the magnitude of the electrostatic force, k is Coulomb's constant (9 x 10^9 N m^2 / C^2), q1 and q2 are the magnitudes of the charges.

Substituting the given values, we get:

r = sqrt((9 x 10^9 N m^2 / C^2) * (26.0 x 10^-6 C) * (-47.0 x 10^-6 C) / 5.70 N)

r = 0.258 m

Therefore, the distance between the two point charges must be 0.258 meters for the electrostatic force between them to have a magnitude of 5.70 N.

E = k*q/r^2

where k is Coulomb's constant (9 x 10^9 N m^2/C^2).

Substituting the given values, we get:

E = (9 x 10^9 N m^2/C^2) * (20 x 10^-6 C) / (1 m)^2

F = k * q1 * q2 / r^2

where k is Coulomb's constant.

Substituting the given values, we get:

F = (9 x 10^9 N m^2/C^2) * (2 x 10^-6 C) * (32 x 10^-6 C) / (0.16 m)^2
