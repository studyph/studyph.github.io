6. All mathematicians are good in math. jake is mathematician. therefore, jake is good in math. this statement is


All mathematicians are good in math. jake is mathematician. therefore, jake is good in math. This statement is deductive reasoning.

About Deductive reasoning

Deductive reasoning is a logical conclusion based on existing premises.

Premises here are assumptions, thoughts, and the basis for conclusions that are considered true. The following is an example of deductively drawing conclusions from premises.

According to deductive reasoning, if the premises provided are true, then the conclusion must be true or valid. So, if the conclusion turns out to be incorrect or invalid, it means that there is something wrong with the premises.

History

The history of deductive reasoning can be traced back to ancient times, when Aristotle worked a lot.

This Greek philosopher is noted for documenting many deductive reasoning in the 4th century BC.

Aristotle is known as the Father of Deductive Reasoning, because he was the first to develop the formal system of reasoning.

He observed that in fact deductive validity can be seen from the structure we have discussed earlier. Here is the most classic example that Aristotle made:

Premise 1: All men are mortal. (All humans can die)

(All humans can die) Premise 2: Socrates is a man. (Socrates is human)

. (Socrates is human) Therefore, Socrates is mortal. (So, Socrates could die)

For the record, mortal has the meaning of mortal, not immortal, and can die

30. All mathematicians are good in math. jake is mathematician. therefore, jake is good in math.


All mathematicians are good at math. Jake is a mathematician. Therefore, Jake is good at math. This statement is may consider as deductive reasoning.

Deductive reasoning is a type of skill that we utilize frequently in our daily tasks. It has been a component of human thought for many years. Due to the fact that it is a special example of a more general statement that is known to be true, deductive reasoning aids in the conclusion that a given statement is true. Arguments using deductive reasoning or deductive logic are common in both academic settings and daily life. The method, also referred to as deduction, entails following a few factual assertions to their logical conclusion.

A deductive argument maintains that the conclusion will likewise be true if all the premises are true and the terminology are used appropriately. Alternative terminology for this is "top-down" logic because it typically begins with a broad assertion and finishes with a more focused, detailed conclusion. The Greek philosopher Aristotle is credited with developing the fundamental ideas of deductive reasoning. Computer programming and mathematics both rely heavily on deductive reasoning.
