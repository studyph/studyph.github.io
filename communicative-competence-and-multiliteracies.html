1. Slogan for communicative, competence, multiliteracies: bridge to appreciating diversity


Different countries have different languages. Within a country, there may be various languages as well. Hence, having the ability to communicate well, to be competent and to be multi-literate will lead to each person's appreciation of diversity of different nations. In line with this, here is an example of slogan for "Communicative, Competence, Multiliteracies: Bridge to Appreciating Diversity".

"Language barrier prevents us from having smooth communication,

but communicative, competence, and multi-literacies will lead us to appreciate the diversity of each nation."

7. how you can teach multiliteracy in the classroom.​


Answer:

Teaching multiliteracy in the classroom involves helping students develop skills in multiple forms of communication and media. Here are some ways to do this:

Integrating technology: Use technology such as laptops, tablets, and smartphones to help students explore different forms of media and develop digital literacy skills.

Encouraging critical consumption: Teach students how to critically evaluate information from different sources and in different forms, including text, images, and audio.

Promoting multimedia projects: Assign projects that require students to use multiple forms of communication and media to convey information, such as creating a podcast or a multimedia presentation.

By implementing these strategies, teachers can help students develop multiliteracy skills that are essential for success in the 21st century.

10. What is multiliteracies?


Answer:

Multiliteracies - is a term coined in the mid-1990s by the New London Group and is an approach to literacy theory and pedagogy. This approach highlights two key aspects of literacy: linguistic diversity, and multimodal forms of linguistic expression and representation.

15. How has globalization influenced multiliteracies?​


Answer:

The sweeping and dramatic impact of globalization is difficult to dispute. From the Tibetan Plateau to the Argentinean pampas, social change has transformed individuals, communities, and nation-states. The starting point for the discussion of Multiliteracies, originating in 1994, was the shape of social change. The authors of the New London Group explored contemporary social change and its implications on individual’s capacity to participate in public, community, and economic life (New London Group, 1996). It is therefore valuable to understand the source of this fundamental shift which has taken place in society, one which has significant implications for the ways we communicate, educate, and participate in our daily lives.  

 

In the simplest terms, globalization can be characterized by the increasingly interconnected and interdependent networks of people, ideas, capital, goods, and services around the world. The dominant ideology behind the phenomenon of global integration is driven by a neo-liberal model of economics, promoting a near universal trend of market deregulation and privatization. Following neo-liberal ideology, the unregulated integration of international financial markets advanced a seemingly unified world economic system (Lo Bianco, 2000). Beginning in the 1980’s, rising into the 1990’s and early 2000’s, neoliberalism dominated national economic policy throughout the majority of the world, leading to unparalleled economic integration in the shape of networked global financial markets. The implications and character of such a system are evidenced by the sweeping global economic collapse which, in 2009, we now find ourselves in.  

Arguably, the most influential element of the global economic system is facilitated by the revolution in information and communication technologies. Instantaneous and ubiquitous communication technologies have been a dominant force of global integration and interconnectivity, the implications of which are seen across cultural, social, political, and economic spheres. It is in this context that we can begin to understand the shape of social change and the influence for which globalization is accountable for such change.

In order for us to create a great slogan, let's keep in mind that the idea communicative competence and multiliteracies bridge to appreciating diversity is about the people's openness to learning first, second, or foreign languages and putting them into practice. Let's look closer by giving meaning to the two components.

First, let's define communicative competence.

Communicative competence is an individual's ability to express his/her ideas in oral and written form with the best sentence construction and diction. It can be achieved through extensive language learning (considering grammar and vocabulary), and regular practice. The significance of having communicative competence manifests whenever we speak and write as it helps the listeners or readers understand us easily and grasp the very purpose of our message. You may check this link for more information: https://brainly.ph/question/501601.

Next, let's define multiliteracies.

Multiliteracies is a concept that talks about the various ways of making meaning among individuals. That's why it has the prefix multi-, meaning many. And literacy is basically the skill of reading and writing. (Here's a broader definition: https://brainly.ph/question/1553058.)  Multiliteracies is important to be considered because we should always be aware that we have different ways of expression and understanding. Some of us understand better by seeing pictures, while some by hearing sounds. Some can learn easily by moving or practicing. We vary because of many factors as nationality, culture, age, gender, etc. And that's what we can call diversity.

When we combine the two, it makes us more globally open. It helps us avoid misunderstanding or misinterpretation, and mostly, culture shock. You may click this link to see what it means: https://brainly.ph/question/1025316.

All right, let's move to the slogan:
