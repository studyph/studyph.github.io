Answer:

Participatory democracy or participative democracy is a model of democracy in which citizens are provided power to make political decisions. Etymological roots of democracy (Greek demos and kratos) imply that the people are in power, making all democracies participatory to some degree. However, participatory democracy tends to advocate greater citizen participation and more direct representation than traditional representative democracy. For example, the creation of governing bodies through a system of sortition, rather than election of representatives, is thought to produce a more participatory body by allowing citizens to hold positions of power themselves.[1]


29. What are the similarities of participatory and representative democracy? Identify at least two.​


Participative Democracy is a form of government in which citizens participate directly or indirectly in making decisions that affect them, whereas Representative Democracy involves representatives elected by voters to make such decisions on their behalf. The main difference between these forms of governance lies in how they deal with issues concerning power distribution. In Participative Democracy, all members have equal say in decision-making processes; however, in Representative Democracy, some people (the majority) get more votes than others (minority). This means that there will be fewer opportunities for minorities to influence policy because only those who can win elections will hold positions of authority.
