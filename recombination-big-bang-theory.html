4. Recombinant DNA technology ​


Answer:

Recombinant DNA technology is the joining together of DNA molecules from two different species. The recombined DNA molecule is inserted into a host organism to produce new genetic combinations that are of value to science, medicine, agriculture, and industry. Since the focus of all genetics is the gene, the fundamental goal of laboratory geneticists is to isolate, characterize, and manipulate genes. Recombinant DNA technology is based primarily on two other technologies, cloning and DNA sequencing. Cloning is undertaken in order to obtain the clone of one particular gene or DNA sequence of interest. The next step after cloning is to find and isolate that clone among other members of the library (a large collection of clones). Once a segment of DNA has been cloned, its nucleotide sequence can be determined. Knowledge of the sequence of a DNA segment has many uses.

24. It is through recombination that biological diversity happens. What would be the current condition of all organisms if there were no recombination?


It is through recombination that biological diversity happens. What would be the current condition of all organisms if there were no recombination?  

Where there was no genetic recombination during meiotics, analyses of genes affecting such traits would reveal that the genes still inherit together; although other genes are transmitted separately, genetic recombination or crossing results in portions of homologous chromosomes becoming exchanged.

Genetic recombination (also known as genetic reshuffling) is the exchange of genetic material between various species, which contributes to the generation of offspring with combinations of characteristics that differ from those present in either parent. In eukaryotes, genetic recombination during meiosis can lead to a novel collection of genetic material that can be transmitted from parents to offspring. Most recombination occurs naturally.  Genetic recombination entails the pairing of homologous chromosomes during meiosis in eukaryotes. This can be accompanied by a transition of information between chromosomes. Recombination can also occur during mitosis in eukaryotes where it usually includes the two sister chromosomes produced after chromosomal replication. In this case, new combinations of alleles are not formed as the sister chromosomes are normally the same. In meiosis and mitosis, recombination occurs between identical DNA molecules (homologous sequences). In meiosis, non-sister homologous chromosomes pair with each other in such a way that recombination usually happens between non-sister homologues. Recombination between homologous chromosomes is a typical pathway used in DNA repair in both meiotic and mitotic cells.  Gene conversion-The mechanism through which homologous sequences are made identical often comes under the context of genetic recombination.  Genetic recombination and recombinant DNA repair also occur in bacteria and archaea that use asexual reproduction.  Recombination can be artificially induced in laboratory (in vitro) environments, generating recombinant DNA for use, including vaccine production.

Chromosomal crossover

In eukaryotes, recombination during meiosis is enabled by a crossover between chromosomes. The fusion mechanism leads to offspring possessing different gene combinations from their parents and may sometimes develop new chimeric alleles. Gene shuffling caused by genetic recombination results in increased genetic diversity. It also helps sexually reproducing species to escape Muller's ratchet, in which the genomes of the asexual population produce genetic deletions in an unsustainable manner.
