13. Construct a 3-paragraph Critical Essay on your judgment of Dr. Jose Rizal, whether he is guilty or not on the charges against him by the Spanish military tribunal. Justify your stand.


Answer:

Dr. Jose Rizal is a national hero and a prominent figure in Philippine history. He is known for his literary works that exposed the injustices and corruption of the Spanish colonial government during his time. However, he was charged with rebellion, sedition, and conspiracy by the Spanish military tribunal in 1896. The question of whether he is guilty or not on the charges against him remains a controversial issue up to this day.

On one hand, some argue that Rizal is guilty of the charges against him. His association with the Katipunan and his involvement in the revolution against the Spanish colonial government could be seen as evidence of his guilt. Moreover, his writings, particularly the novels Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo, were considered subversive and anti-colonial, which could further support the charges against him.

On the other hand, others argue that Rizal is not guilty of the charges against him. They point out that Rizal was not directly involved in the revolution and that his writings were not meant to incite rebellion but to expose the injustices and corruption of the colonial government. Furthermore, Rizal's peaceful and non-violent approach to social change is well-documented, which contradicts the charges of rebellion and sedition against him.

In conclusion, the question of whether Dr. Jose Rizal is guilty or not on the charges against him by the Spanish military tribunal is a complex issue that requires a thorough understanding of the historical context and the evidence presented. While there are arguments for both sides, I personally believe that Rizal is not guilty of the charges against him. His writings and actions were driven by a desire for social justice and reform, not rebellion and sedition. Rizal's legacy as a national hero and a champion of freedom and democracy in the Philippines remains strong to this day.

José Rizal

1861-1896

José Rizal, son of a Filipino father and a Chinese mother, came from a wealthy family. Despite his family's wealth, they suffered discrimination because neither parent was born in the peninsula. Rizal studied at the Ateneo, a private high school, and then to the University of St. Thomas in Manila. He did his post graduate work at the University of Madrid in 1882. For the next five years, he wandered through Europe discussing politics wherever he went. In 1886, he studied medicine at the University of Heidelberg and wrote his classic novel Noli me Tangere, which condemned the Catholic Church in the Philippines for its promotion of Spanish colonialism. Immediately upon its publication, he became a target for the police who even shadowed him when he returned to the Philippines in 1887. He left his country shortly thereafter to return to Spain where he wrote a second novel, El Filibusterismo (1891), and many articles in his support of Filipino nationalism and his crusade to include representatives from his homeland in the Spanish Cortes.

He returned to Manila in 1892 and created the Liga Filipina, a political group that called for peace change for the islands. Nevertheless, Spanish officials were displeased and exiled Rizal to the island of Mindanao. During his four years there, he practiced medicine, taught students, and collected local examples of flora and fauna while recording his discoveries. Even though he lost touched with others who were working for Filipino independence, he quickly denounced the movement when it became violent and revolutionary. After Andrés Bonifacio issued the Grito de Balintawak in 1896, Rizal was arrested, convicted of sedition, and executed by firing squad on December 30, 1896.

Following the revolution, Rizal was made a saint by many religious cults while the United States authorities seized on his non-violent stance and emphasized his views on Filipino nationalism rather than those of the more action-oriented Emilio Aguinaldo and Andrés Bonifacio.

Instead, they were focused on exposing the injustices and inequalities of the colonial system and advocating for reforms through peaceful means. He was not allowed to confront his accusers or defend himself adequately, and many of the witnesses against him were coerced or intimidated by the authorities. In conclusion, while Rizal's trial and execution remain a controversial and contested part of Philippine history, my judgment is that Rizal was not guilty of the charges against him. His legacy as a national hero and champion of Philippine independence is a testament to his courage, intelligence, and vision for a better and more just society.
