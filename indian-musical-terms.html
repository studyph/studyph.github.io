13. Indian musical instruments ​


Answer:

SHENAI

The shehnai is a musical instrument, originating from the Indian subcontinent. It is made out of wood, with a double reed at one end and a metal or wooden flared bell at the other end.

SARANGI

The sārangī is a bowed, short-necked string instrument from the Indian subcontinent, which is used in Punjabi Folk Music, Rajasthani Folk Music, and Boro folk music. It is said to most resemble the sound of the human voice – able to imitate vocal ornaments such as gamaks and meends

TAAL

The taal, manjira, jalra, or gini is a pair of clash cymbals, originating in the Indian subcontinent, which make high-pitched percussion sounds. In its simplest form, it consists of a pair of small hand cymbals. The word taal comes from the Sanskrit word Tālà, which literally means a clap.

Hindustani music, one of the two principal types of South Asian classical music, found mainly in the northern three-fourths of the subcontinent, where Indo-Aryan languages are spoken. (The other principal type, Karnatak music, is found in the Dravidian-speaking region of southern India.) The two systems diverged gradually, beginning in the 13th century, when the Islamic conquest of northern parts of the subcontinent introduced highly influential Arab and Persian musical practices that then merged with Hindu traditions. Indian music

South Asian music

Northern India shares with the south the use of ragas (melodic frameworks for improvisation and composition), the rhythmic principles of tala (cyclic metric patterns sometimes of great complexity), and the practice of nonmetric, rhythmically “free” improvisation. Although vocal music plays an important role, instrumental music is more important in Hindustani music than it is in Karnatak; there are some purely instrumental forms, such as the theme with variations known as gat.

The most prominent instruments of Hindustani music are the sitar (a long-necked fretted lute with about 30 melodic, drone, and sympathetic strings), sarod (a short-necked unfretted lute with sympathetic and drone strings), sarangi (a bowed fiddle), shehnai (an oboelike wind instrument), tabla (a set of two drums played by one musician, the right-hand drum carefully tuned), and tambura (a large long-necked lute with four strings, used only to play the supporting drone, a single repeated chord).

A typical Hindustani performance, which may last well over an hour, begins with a long, nonmetric improvisation (alapa, or alap) by the singer or melodic soloist, followed by jor, or improvisation without metric cycle but with a perceptible pulse, and eventually by the similar but faster jhala. Then follows the composed piece, which is performed with improvised variations—most typically khayal (a poetic form) in vocal music and gat, a short, rhythmically distinctive theme, in instrumental music. Here, the soloist is accompanied by the percussionist on tabla, and the improvisations often involve various kinds of virtuosic rhythmic competition and cooperation.

24. discuss the musical element of Indian classical music​


Answer:

Indian classical music is a rich and complex musical tradition with a long history. It is characterized by a number of musical elements that are unique to the Indian subcontinent.

Raga: Raga is the central musical element in Indian classical music. A raga is a melodic framework that outlines the rules for how a particular piece of music is played. It defines the scale, notes, and melody of the music, and is often associated with specific times of day, seasons, and emotions.

Tala: Tala is the rhythmic structure of Indian classical music. It defines the beat, meter, and tempo of the music, and is often performed by a percussionist. Talas can range from simple and straightforward to complex and intricate, and are used to create a variety of musical moods and styles.

Melody: Indian classical music is primarily a melodic tradition, with the melody being the most important aspect of the music. The melody is created through the use of various vocal and instrumental techniques, including slides, glides, vibrato, and ornamentation.

Improvisation: Improvisation is a key element of Indian classical music, allowing musicians to express their creativity and individuality within the framework of the raga and tala. Improvisation can take many forms, including variations on the melody, rhythm, and phrasing.

Texture: Indian classical music is often performed as a solo or small ensemble, with the musicians playing off each other to create a rich and complex musical texture. The use of drones, ragamala (a form of ornamentation), and the interplay between the vocalist and instrumentalists all contribute to the overall texture of the music.

In conclusion, the musical elements of Indian classical music are highly sophisticated and varied, reflecting the rich cultural and historical heritage of the Indian subcontinent. These elements are used to create a wide range of musical styles, from the meditative and introspective to the energetic and exuberant.
