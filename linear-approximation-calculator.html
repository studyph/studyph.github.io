1. In 1977, the price of a scientific calculator was $175. In 2004, the price was $15. Assuming the change in price was linear, what was the approximate price of a scientific calculator in 1998?


First, find the linear equation:

Let the x-axis represents the years and the y-axis represents the price.

1977 (y₁) = 0                                     Price in 1977 (x₁) = 175
2004 (y₂) = 2004 - 1977 = 27            Price in 2004 (x₂) = 15

The two points are:
(1997, price) = (0, 175)
(2004, price) = (27, 15)

y-y₁ = [tex] \frac{y _{2}-y _{1} }{x _{2}-x _{1} } [/tex] (x - x₁)

y - 175 = -160/27 (x - 0)

y = -160/27 (x) + 175

y = -5.93x + 175   (Linear Equation)
       
Note that the slope (-160/27 or -5.93) is negative because the price 
of the calculator is decreasing.      

To find the price of calculator in 1998:
x₃ = 1998 - 1977
x₃ = 21

Plug-in 21 to x₃:
y₃ = -5.92(x₃) + 175
y₃ = -5.92 (21) + 175
y₃ = -124.32 + 175
y₃ ≈ 50.68  or  $51

ANSWER:  The price of calculator in 1998 is $50.68 or $51.

9. As a student and a worker, why do you think it is neccessary to know the importance of measuring and calculating approximately? why?​


Answer:

Uncertainty based on best estimate

When scientists make a measurement or calculate a quantity from their data, they usually presume that there is an exact or "true value" depending on how they characterize what they are measuring (or calculated). Scientists who present their findings frequently include a range of values within which they expect this "real value" to fall. The most popular technique to display the range of values is to use the following formula: measurement = best estimate uncertainty.

Importance of measuring and calculating approximately

Without measurement, modern society would simply not exist. Without the vital measurement tools on which everyday life is based, twenty-first-century civilization would be unthinkable.

Time, size, distance, speed, direction, weight, volume, temperature, pressure, force, sound, light, energy: these are among the physical properties for which humans have developed accurate measures, without which we could not live our daily routines.

Every area of human life is influenced by measurement. However, we have a tendency to take measurement for granted, failing to see how much we need and rely on our measurement tools. We take measuring for granted since we are surrounded by it and have been accustomed to it. It's only when our measurement tools break down or become unavailable that we realize how critical they are. We truly don't realize what we have until it's gone.

Look at the importanceYou start to wonder how and when you'll get up the next morning to go to work as you go to bed. There would be no clocks or alarms to wake you up at a specific hour if measurement did not exist. On the plus side, if there were no timekeepers, no one would ever know if you were late for work. Without measurement, the concept of being on time would evaporate entirely.You won't be able to make breakfast or coffee with an electric stove, oven, or microwave after waking up because these devices rely on temperature or time measurements to heat food and beverages. You wouldn't be able to use a modern refrigerator either, because refrigeration necessitates measurement in order to keep food and beverages at a consistent temperature.You couldn't drive to work since modern cars have onboard computers that use measurements to control the ignition system, automatic transmission, brakes, engine temperature, throttle position, and fuel-oxygen mix. You wouldn't be able to tell how much fuel, oil, or transmission fluid your vehicle had because there would be no fuel gauge or dip stick to check these fluid levels without measurement.

28. how did the ancient people of egypt babylon and austria calculate the years approximate length​


Answer:

Ancient Babylonian, Assyrian, and Egyptian astronomers knew the approximate length of the year. The Egyptians of 3000 years ago, for example, adopted a calendar based on a 365-day year. They kept careful track of the rising time of the bright star Sirius in the predawn sky, which has a yearly cycle that corresponded with the flooding of the Nile River. The Chinese also had a working calendar; they determined the length of the year at about the same time as the Egyptians. The Chinese also recorded comets, bright meteors, and dark spots on the Sun. (Many types of astronomical objects were introduced in you are not familiar with terms like comets and meteors, you may want to review that chapter.) Later, Chinese astronomers kept careful records of “guest stars”—those that are normally too faint to see but suddenly flare up to become visible to the unaided eye for a few weeks or months. We still use some of these records in studying stars that exploded a long time ago.
