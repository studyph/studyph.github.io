5. Find the probability of selecting a boy or a blond-haired person from 12 girls, 5 of whom have blond hair, and 15 boys, 6 of whom have blond hair.


Solution:

Find the probability of selecting a boy or a blond-haired person from 12 girls, 5 of whom have blond hair, and 15 boys, 6 of whom have blond hair.

So it is non- mutually exclusive;

Let B for the boys and H for the blond hair.

P(B) = 15/27; P(H) 11/27; P(B and H) = 6/27

Then, we have,

P(B or H) = 15/27 + 11/27 - 6/27
P(B or H) = 20/27 

Therefore,  the probability of selecting a boy or a blond-haired person is 20/27.NOT MUTUALLY EXCLUSIVE

No. of Persons = 12 + 15 =27
Girls Only (G)= 12/27
Boys Only (B)= 15/27
Blond (L) = 5 + 6 = 11/27
Boy and Blond (B and L) = 6/27

P (B or L) = P (B) + P (L) - P (B and L)
P (B or L) = 15/27 + 11/27 - 6/27
P (B or L) = 26/27 - 6/27
P (B or L) = 20/27

Thus, the probability of selecting a boy or a blond-haired is 20/27.


6. Inductive reasoning: My mother is Irish. She has blond hair. Therefore, everyone from Ireland has blond hair.


Answer:

This is an example of a logical fallacy known as "hasty generalization," because it draws a broad conclusion about a group of people based on a limited sample. Just because one Irish person has blond hair does not mean that all Irish people have blond hair

Total number of people is 20Girls are 12Boys are 89 blonde11 browned hair

Formula for probability

P = possible outcomes / total of outcomes

=====================

Let G = girl , B = blonde

There are 9 total blonde people in the room and there are 5 girls that are blonde. So , we will get the number of girls that are not blonde.

P(G or B) = P(G) + P(B)

P(G or B) = (Girls - blonde G)/20 + blonde/20

P(G or B) = 12-5/20 + 9/20

P(G or B) = 4/5
