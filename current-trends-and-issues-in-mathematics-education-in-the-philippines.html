10. What are the current trends and issues in industrial arts


Current trends in industrial arts include the increasing efficiency of the labor force. The industrial revolution has made vast amounts of goods and machinery available to more people than ever before, meaning more people can participate in the workforce. Industrial arts teachers and trainers are always in demand to help train workers to be more efficient at their jobs, and to improve their work efficiency. With the demand for industrial arts comes the need for a more practical application to the curriculum. While the industrial revolution allows for a more practical, hands-on approach to the curriculum, it also reflects a growing concern for the well-being of workers and their families. The Industrial Arts Movement includes programs like the Works Progress Administration, which provides jobs and relief for workers and families. The Industrial Arts Movement also includes a concern for the environment. This is why LEARN is created. This is because it teaches children how to recycle, reuse, and repurpose materials, as well as how to reduce their own carbon footprints. Industrial arts is a hands-on, practical, and creative approach to education, and it is an approach that can be applied to any and every subject, even math and science.

15. give atleast 5 issues and trends in philippine educational systemâ€‹


Answer:

-Quality of Education.

-Budget for Education.

-Affordability of Education.

-Drop-out Rate (Out-of-school youth)

-Mismatch.

Quality of education

First of which, is the quality of education. In the year 2014, the National Achievement Test (NAT) and the National Career Assessment Examination (NCAE) results show that there had been a decline in the quality of Philippine education at the elementary and secondary levels. The students' performance in both the 2014 NAT and NCAE were excessively below the target mean score. Having said this, the poor quality of the Philippine educational system is manifested in the comparison of completion rates between highly urbanized city of Metro Manila, which is also happens to be not only the country's capital but the largest metropolitan area in the Philippines and other places in the country such as Mindanao and Eastern Visayas. Although Manila is able to boast a primary school completion rate of approximately 100 percent, other areas of the nation, such as Eastern Visayas and Mindanao, hold primary school completion rate of only 30 percent or even less. This kind of statistic is no surprise to the education system in the Philippine context, students who hail from Philippine urban areas have the financial capacity to complete at the very least their primary school education.

Budget for education

The second issue that the Philippine educational system faces is the budget for education. Although it has been mandated by the Philippine Constitution for the government to allocate the highest proportion of its government to education, the Philippines remains to have one of the lowest budget allocations to education among ASEAN countries.

Affordability of education

The third prevalent issue the Philippine educational system continuously encounters is the affordability of education (or lack thereof). A big disparity in educational achievements is evident across various social groups. Socioeconomically disadvantaged students otherwise known as students who are members of high and low-income poverty-stricken families, have immensely higher drop-out rates in the elementary level. Additionally, most freshmen students at the tertiary level come from relatively well-off families.

Drop out rate (Out-of-school youth)

France Castro, secretary of Alliance of Concerned Teachers (ACT), stated that there is a graved need to address the alarming number of out-of-school youth in the country. The Philippines overall has 1.4 million children who are out-of-school, according to UNESCO's data, and is additionally the only ASEAN country that is included in the top 5 countries with the highest number of out-of-school youth. In 2012, the Department of Education showed data of a 6.38% drop-out rate in primary school and a 7.82% drop-out rate in secondary school. Castro further stated that "the increasing number of out-of-school children is being caused by poverty. The price increases in prices of oil, electricity, rice, water, and other basic commodities are further pushing the poor into dire poverty." Subsequently, as more families become poorer, the number of students enrolled in public schools increases, especially in the high school level. In 2013, the Department of Education estimated that there are 38, 503 elementary schools alongside 7,470 high schools.

Mismatch

There is a large mismatch between educational training and actual jobs. This stands to be a major issue at the tertiary level and it is furthermore the cause of the continuation of a substantial amount of educated yet unemployed or underemployed people. According to Dean Salvador Belaro Jr., the Cornell-educated Congressman representing 1-Ang Edukasyon Party-list in the House of Representatives, the number of educated unemployed reaches around 600,000 per year. He refers to said condition as the "education gap".

28. Relate a story to the current trend of business in the Philippines regarding Ethical issues.â€‹


Answer:

Once upon a time, there was a businessman in the Philippines named Juan. Juan owned a small manufacturing company that produced plastic toys for children. He was known for his cheap prices and quick turnaround times, which made him very popular among local retailers.

However, one day, a news report surfaced, exposing the unethical practices of Juan's company. The report showed that his company was dumping toxic waste into nearby rivers, which was causing harm to the environment and nearby communities.

Juan was shocked when he saw the report. He had always believed in the importance of being a responsible corporate citizen, but he had never paid much attention to the impact of his business on the environment.

After reading the report, Juan decided to take action. He immediately halted all production at his company and called for a meeting with his employees. At the meeting, Juan explained the situation and asked his employees for their thoughts on how to rectify the issue.

Together, Juan and his employees came up with a plan to clean up the waste and reduce the company's environmental impact. They installed waste management systems, hired an environmental consultant, and began using biodegradable materials in their products.

Juan's efforts did not go unnoticed. Customers and retailers alike praised his company for taking responsibility and making a positive change. As a result, Juan's business grew even more successful, as more and more people recognized his commitment to ethical business practices.

In the end, Juan learned that being a responsible corporate citizen wasn't just the right thing to do - it was also good for business. By taking action to address ethical issues, he was able to earn the trust and loyalty of his customers and contribute to the betterment of his community.
