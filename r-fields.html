5. A very long insulating cylinder has radius R and carries positive charge distributed throughout its volume. The charge distribution has cylindrical symmetry but varies with perpendicular distance from the axis of the cylinder. The volume charge density is r(r) = a(1 - r>R), where a is a constant with units C>m3 and r is the perpendicular distance from the center line of the cylinder. Derive an expression, in terms of a and R, for E(r), the electric field as a function of r. Do this for r 6 R and also for r 7 R. Do your results agree for r = R?


To find the electric field as a function of r, we can use Gauss's law, which states that the flux of the electric field through any closed surface is equal to the electric charge enclosed by that surface divided by the permittivity of free space.

For r < R, we can consider a Gaussian surface that is a cylinder of radius r. The flux of the electric field through this surface is given by:

Flux = E * A = E * 2πrL

Where E is the electric field, A is the surface area of the cylinder, and L is the length of the cylinder.

The charge enclosed by this surface is given by the volume of the cylinder multiplied by the volume charge density:

Q = V * r(r) = πr^2L * a(1 - r/R)

Substituting these expressions into Gauss's law, we get:

E * 2πrL = πr^2L * a(1 - r/R) / ε₀

Solving for E, we find:

E = a(1 - r/R) / ε₀

For r > R, we can consider a Gaussian surface that is a cylinder of radius R. The flux of the electric field through this surface is given by:

Flux = E * A = E * 2πRL

The charge enclosed by this surface is given by the volume of the cylinder multiplied by the volume charge density:

Q = V * r(r) = πR^2L * a

Substituting these expressions into Gauss's law, we get:

E * 2πRL = πR^2L * a / ε₀

Solving for E, we find:

E = a / ε₀

At r = R, the electric field is given by:

E = a / ε₀ = a(1 - R/R) / ε₀ = a / ε₀

Therefore, the electric field is continuous at r = R and our results agree.

18. An infinitely long, very thin metal tube with radius R= 980 mm. The figure below shows a section of it. If the linear charge density of the cylinder is λ= 2.43 x10 -7 C/m, what is the appropriate magnitude of the electric field at radial distance r= 2R? The value of the permittivity constant (ε0= Epsilon naught or permittivity of free space) is ε0= 8.85 x 10 -12 C2/ N.m2.​


Answer:

The magnitude of the electric field of a metal tube is 2229.6 N/C

Explanation:

Here, we are to solve for the electric field of a line charge of a metal tube. To do this we will use the formula:

[tex]E = \frac{\lambda }{2\pi r\epsilon_0 }[/tex]

where

E     is the electric field, unit is in N/C

λ     is the linear charge density

r      is the radial distance, unit is in meter, m

ε₀    is the permittivity constant in free space, 8.85 x 10⁻¹² C²/ N.m²

For the given information

R= 980 mm = 0.98 m

r = 2R = 2(0.98 m) = 1.96 m

λ = 2.43 x 10⁻⁷ C/m

E = ?

Solving the problem

Let us solve for the electric field of the metal tube using the formula, we have:

[tex]E = \frac{\lambda }{2\pi r\epsilon_0 }[/tex]

Then substitute the given information, we have:

[tex]E = \frac{2.43x10^{-7}C/m }{2\pi (1.96m)(8.85 x 10^{-12} C^2/ N.m^2) }[/tex]

E = 2229.6 N/C

Therefore, the magnitude of the electric field of a metal tube is 2229.6 N/C

Direct variation describes a simple relationship between two variables . We say y varies directly with x (or as x , in some textbooks) if:

y=kx

for some constant k , called the constant of variation or constant of proportionality . (Some textbooks describe direct variation by saying " y varies directly as x ", " y varies proportionally as x ", or " y is directly proportional to x .")

This means that as x increases, y increases and as x decreases, y decreases—and that the ratio between them always stays the same.

The graph of the direct variation equation is a straight line through the origin.

   

Direct Variation Equation

for 3 different values of k

Write the direct variation equation.

y=13x

y=13⋅12y=4

y=kx
