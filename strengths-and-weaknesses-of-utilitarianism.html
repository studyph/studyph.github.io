4. Compare andcontrast act utilitarianismand rule utilitarianism​


Answer:

Utilitarianism is one of the best known and most influential moral theories. Like other forms of consequentialism, its core idea is that whether actions are morally right or wrong depends on their effects. More specifically, the only effects of actions that are relevant are the good and bad results that they produce. A key point in this article concerns the distinction between individual actions and types of actions. Act utilitarians focus on the effects of individual actions (such as John Wilkes Booth’s assassination of Abraham Lincoln) while rule utilitarians focus on the effects of types of actions (such as killing or stealing).

Utilitarians believe that the purpose of morality is to make life better by increasing the amount of good things (such as pleasure and happiness) in the world and decreasing the amount of bad things (such as pain and unhappiness). They reject moral codes or systems that consist of commands or taboos that are based on customs, traditions, or orders given by leaders or supernatural beings. Instead, utilitarians think that what makes a morality be true or justifiable is its positive contribution to human (and perhaps non-human) beings.

The most important classical utilitarians are Jeremy Bentham (1748-1832) and John Stuart Mill (1806-1873). Bentham and Mill were both important theorists and social reformers. Their theory has had a major impact both on philosophical work in moral theory and on approaches to economic, political, and social policy. Although utilitarianism has always had many critics,  there are many 21st century thinkers that support it.

The task of determining whether utilitarianism is the correct moral theory is complicated because there are different versions of the theory, and its supporters disagree about which version is correct. This article focuses on perhaps the most important dividing line among utilitarians, the clash between act utilitarianism and rule utilitarianism. After a brief overall explanation of utilitarianism, the article explains both act utilitarianism and rule utilitarianism, the main differences between them, and some of the key arguments for and against each view.

19. ACTIVITY 2 Compare and contrast the following: utilitarianism, deontology, and consequentialism. What are their weaknesses?​


Answer:

Utilitarianism, deontology, and consequentialism are ethical theories that provide frameworks for determining the rightness or wrongness of actions.

Utilitarianism is an ethical theory that holds that the moral worth of an action is determined by its ability to produce the greatest amount of happiness or pleasure for the greatest number of people. According to utilitarianism, the rightness or wrongness of an action is based on its consequences. The strengths of utilitarianism lie in its focus on the greatest good for the greatest number, its emphasis on outcomes, and its practical application. However, its weakness lies in its inability to consider individual rights and the potential for sacrificing the happiness of a minority for the greater happiness of the majority.

Deontology is an ethical theory that focuses on the moral duties or obligations of individuals, regardless of their consequences. According to deontology, some actions are inherently right or wrong, regardless of their consequences. The strengths of deontology lie in its emphasis on the inherent value of individual rights and its ability to provide clear moral guidelines. However, its weakness lies in its rigid adherence to rules, which may not always be applicable to all situations and may not always lead to the best outcomes.

Consequentialism is an ethical theory that focuses on the outcomes or consequences of actions. According to consequentialism, the rightness or wrongness of an action is based on the consequences it produces. The strengths of consequentialism lie in its focus on outcomes and its ability to provide flexibility in decision-making. However, its weakness lies in the potential for justifying immoral actions if they produce good consequences and its inability to consider the inherent value of individual rights.

In summary, utilitarianism, deontology, and consequentialism are ethical theories that offer different perspectives on the rightness or wrongness of actions. Utilitarianism emphasizes outcomes and the greatest good for the greatest number, deontology emphasizes individual rights and moral obligations, while consequentialism emphasizes outcomes and flexibility in decision-making. The weaknesses of each theory lie in their inability to fully consider all ethical factors in decision-making, such as individual rights, moral obligations, and the potential for unintended consequences.

30. 1.how does utilitarianism applied to business and war 2. what are the limitations of utilitarianism 3. what is your overall critique of utilitarianism ​


Answer:

1.how does utilitarianism applied to business and war

Utilitarianism is a moral theory that holds that the best action is the one that maximizes overall happiness or pleasure and minimizes overall unhappiness or pain. In business, this might mean that a company should try to produce products that will bring the most happiness to the greatest number of people. For example, a company might decide to donate a portion of its profits to charity or to invest in environmentally-friendly practices in order to maximize the overall happiness of society. In the case of war, utilitarianism would suggest that the decision to go to war should be based on a calculation of whether the expected benefits of the war (such as defending one's country or promoting a just cause) outweigh the expected costs (such as the loss of life and destruction of property).

2. what are the limitations of utilitarianism

One limitation of utilitarianism is that it can be difficult to accurately predict the consequences of an action. It is often difficult to predict how much pleasure or pain a particular action will cause, and it may be difficult to compare the pleasure or pain caused by different actions. Additionally, utilitarianism does not take into account the rights or moral worth of individuals. It may be the case that the greatest happiness for the greatest number of people can be achieved by sacrificing the well-being or happiness of a minority group. This can be seen as a violation of the principle of equality and can be considered unfair or unjust.

3. what is your overall critique of utilitarianism

Overall, utilitarianism is a useful ethical theory that can provide a framework for thinking about moral dilemmas and making decisions. However, it has some limitations and can be criticized for its focus on maximizing overall happiness or pleasure without taking into account other important moral considerations, such as the rights and moral worth of individuals. It is important to consider a range of ethical perspectives and to weigh the potential costs and benefits of different actions in order to arrive at a just and moral decision.
