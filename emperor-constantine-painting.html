14. discuss the significance of emperor constantine during the Roman Christian Empire.​


Answer:

First, Constantine decriminalized Christianity in the Roman Empire by the Edict of Milan (313 AD). Although Christianity was growing rapidly in the empire, it was still not acceptable to be a Christian. Constantine’s action allowed Christians to worship freely and tell others its message without state-sanctioned persecution. Granted, there were divergent understandings about what was true Christianity with the battle over , and Constantine used his political power to push what that would become, but even in that, it is important to understand that Constantine was a politician who was a believer in Christ; he had to run the empire, and could not afford to have a divided faith in the empire. The freedom to be a Christian opened the door for a greater expansion, which ultimately led Christianity to be the dominant faith in the west.

Second, Constantine moved the capital of the empire away from Rome in Italy to the tip of a peninsula on the western shore of the Bosphorus Strait on the Black Sea. By 325, the trading city of Byzantion had become Constantinople (or, New Rome). His motives were many, but the impact of doing so extended the life of the Roman Empire (at least the Eastern Roman Empire) for over another 1,000 years. Within 300 years of the move, Rome (the city) was merely a shell of its former self and Germanic warlords controlled it. But with Constantine’s move, the empire lived on far longer.
