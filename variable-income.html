13. Net operating income computed using absorption costing will always be less than net operating income computed using variable costing.


Absorption Costing

Absorption costing is necessary for external reporting. Until the product is sold, all production costs, whether fixed or variable, must be considered product costs and recorded in an inventory amount on the balance sheet.

TRUE

Net operating income determined by absorption costing will never exceed net operating income determined by variable costing.

Supporting details:

When production volume exceeds sales volume, absorption costing releases a portion of fixed manufacturing overhead cost from inventories.

Because absorption costing defers a portion of fixed manufacturing expenses in finished goods inventory, absorption revenue exceeds variable costing income when units produced exceed units sold, like when inventory increases.

The net operating income reported under absorption costing will be higher than the net operating income reported under variable costing since all of the existing fixed production overhead expenditures are expensed under variable costing.

Keep in mind that while fixed costs do not change as activity levels fluctuate, total variable costs do so proportionately. Direct materials, variable manufacturing overhead, and direct labor often make up these variable production expenses.

Direct materials, direct labor, and overhead would be included in the product costs (or cost of goods sold). The selling, general, and administrative costs would be included in the period costs.

23. In the income statement prepared using the variable costing method, fixed factory overhead would


Fixed factory overhead would be considered in the computation of operational income but not the computation of the contribution margin in an income statement prepared using the variable-costing method. Operational income, a word used in accounting, refers to the profit generated by a company's operations after operating expenses like salaries, depreciation, and cost of goods sold have been deducted (COGS).

A company's gross income, which is equal to total sales less COGS, is used to compute operating income, sometimes referred to as income from operations. Office supplies and utilities are examples of operating expenses, which a company incurs as part of normal business operations.

The amount of profit made from ongoing activities is reported as operating income. It is figured up by deducting operational costs from gross profit. Administrative, selling, or general expenses are examples of naturally recurring costs incurred to run a business.

As both measurements indicate profit before non-operating expenses like interest or taxes, operating income and EBIT are equivalent. However, because net income includes these additional expenses, operational income and net income are different.
