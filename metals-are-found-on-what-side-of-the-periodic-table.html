The periodic table, commonly known as the periodic table of (chemical) elements, is a table that displays the chemical elements in a tabular format. It's commonly used in chemistry, physics, and other sciences, and it's considered a chemistry icon. It is a graphic representation of the periodic law, which claims that the properties of chemical elements are related to their atomic numbers in a predictable manner. According to increasing atomic number, the periodic table divides all discovered chemical elements into rows (called periods) and columns (named groups).

Period

Periods are the horizontal rows of the periodic table. Each period represents the consecutive occupation of orbitals in an atom's valence shell, with the long periods representing the occupation of orbitals in a d subshell.

Family/Group

Families are groups of elements that share similar features in the periodic table. Alkali metals, alkaline earth metals, transition metals, post-transition metals, metalloids, halogens, noble metals, and noble gases are the members of these families.

Three Elements in Periodic Table

The Periodic Table of Elements is divided into groups and periods. Metals, nonmetals, and metalloids are the three primary groups on the Periodic Table. Physical and chemical properties are comparable among the elements in each group.

Periodic Table Metal Group

Metals make up the majority of elements. Alkali metals, alkaline earth metals, transition metals, basic metals, lanthanides (rare earth elements), and actinides are all members of this group. Lanthanides and actinides are two distinct types of transition metals, despite their separation on the periodic table.
