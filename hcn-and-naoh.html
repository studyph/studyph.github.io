4. About analytical chemistry: Titration Calculate the pH of the titration of 50 mL 0.05 M HCI with 0.10 M NaOH ( Express the answers in 3 SF) give the step by step solution of each of the following: a.) 0mL NaOH b.) 10 mL NaOH C.) 20 mL NaOH d.) 24 mL NaOH e.) 24.10 mL NaOH f.) 25 mL NaOH g.) 25.10 mL NaOH h.﻿﻿) 26 mL NaOH


Answer:

The balanced chemical equation for the reaction between HCl and NaOH is:

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H2O

a.) 0 mL NaOH

At the beginning of the titration, no NaOH has been added yet, so the solution only contains HCl. The HCl is a strong acid, so it completely dissociates in water:

HCl → H+ + Cl-

The concentration of HCl is 0.05 M, so the concentration of H+ is also 0.05 M. Since there are no other sources of H+ or OH- ions, the solution is acidic with a pH of:

pH = -log[H+]

pH = -log[0.05]

pH = 1.30 (rounded to 3 SF)

b.) 10 mL NaOH

At this point, 10 mL of 0.10 M NaOH has been added to the solution. The moles of NaOH added can be calculated as:

moles of NaOH = concentration x volume

moles of NaOH = 0.10 M x 0.010 L

moles of NaOH = 0.0010 mol

Since the reaction between HCl and NaOH is a 1:1 stoichiometric ratio, this means that 0.0010 mol of H+ ions have been neutralized by the NaOH. The remaining moles of H+ can be calculated as:

moles of H+ = initial moles - moles of NaOH added

moles of H+ = (0.050 M) (0.050 L) - 0.0010 mol

moles of H+ = 0.0025 mol

The new concentration of H+ can be calculated as:

concentration of H+ = moles of H+ / total volume of solution

concentration of H+ = 0.0025 mol / 0.060 L

concentration of H+ = 0.0417 M

The pH of the solution can then be calculated as:

pH = -log[H+]

pH = -log[0.0417]

pH = 1.38 (rounded to 3 SF)

c.) 20 mL NaOH

Following the same steps as in part (b), the moles of NaOH added is:

moles of NaOH = 0.10 M x 0.020 L

moles of NaOH = 0.0020 mol

The moles of H+ remaining is:

moles of H+ = (0.050 M) (0.050 L) - 0.0020 mol

moles of H+ = 0.0025 mol

The new concentration of H+ is:

concentration of H+ = 0.0025 mol / 0.070 L

concentration of H+ = 0.0357 M

The pH of the solution is:

pH = -log[H+]

pH = -log[0.0357]

pH = 1.45 (rounded to 3 SF)

d.) 24 mL NaOH

The moles of NaOH added is:

moles of NaOH = 0.10 M x 0.024 L

moles of NaOH = 0.0024 mol

The moles of H+ remaining is:

moles of H+ = (0.050 M) (0.050 L) - 0.0024 mol

moles of H+ = 0.0026 mol

The new concentration of H+ is:

concentration of H+ = 0.0026 mol / 0

f.) 25 mL NaOH

At this point, the volume of NaOH added is equal to the volume of HCl in the flask, and they react in a 1:1 ratio. Therefore, the moles of HCl that have reacted are:

moles HCl = (0.05 mol/L) x (25 mL / 1000 mL/L) = 0.00125 mol

The total volume of the solution is 50 mL HCl + 25 mL NaOH = 75 mL. Thus, the concentration of H+ ions can be calculated as follows:

[H+] = moles HCl / total volume = 0.00125 mol / 0.075 L = 0.0167 mol/L

Taking the negative log of the [H+] gives the pH:

pH = -log([H+]) = -log(0.0167) = 1.78

Therefore, the pH at the point of adding 25 mL NaOH is 1.78.

g.) 25.10 mL NaOH

Similar to the previous step, the moles of HCl that have reacted can be calculated:

moles HCl = (0.05 mol/L) x (25.10 mL / 1000 mL/L) = 0.001255 mol

The total volume of the solution is now 75.10 mL, so the concentration of H+ ions can be calculated as follows:

[H+] = moles HCl / total volume = 0.001255 mol / 0.07510 L = 0.0167 mol/L

Taking the negative log of the [H+] gives the pH:

pH = -log([H+]) = -log(0.0167) = 1.78

Therefore, the pH at the point of adding 25.10 mL NaOH is also 1.78.

h.) 26 mL NaOH

At this point, the amount of NaOH added is greater than the amount of HCl in the flask, so the excess NaOH will determine the concentration of OH- ions in the solution. The moles of NaOH added can be calculated as follows:

moles NaOH = (0.10 mol/L) x (26 mL / 1000 mL/L) = 0.0026 mol

The total volume of the solution is now 50 mL HCl + 26 mL NaOH = 76 mL. Thus, the concentration of OH- ions can be calculated as follows:

[OH-] = moles NaOH / total volume = 0.0026 mol / 0.076 L = 0.0342 mol/L

To calculate the concentration of H+ ions, we use the fact that Kw = [H+][OH-], where Kw is the ion product constant of water (1.0 x 10^-14 at 25°C):

[H+] = Kw / [OH-] = (1.0 x 10^-14) / 0.0342 = 2.92 x 10^-13 mol/L

Taking the negative log of the [H+] gives the pH:

pH = -log([H+]) = -log(2.92 x 10^-13) = 12.54

Therefore, the pH at the point of adding 26 mL NaOH is 12.54.

Answer:

First, we need to determine the amount of HCl in moles:

moles HCl = Molarity x Volume (in liters)

moles HCl = 0.4 M x 0.035 L

moles HCl = 0.014 moles

Since HCl and NaOH react in a 1:1 ratio, we need the same number of moles of NaOH to neutralize the HCl:

moles NaOH = moles HCl

moles NaOH = 0.014 moles

Now, we can use the volume and molarity of NaOH to calculate how much we need:

Volume NaOH = moles NaOH / Molarity NaOH

Volume NaOH = 0.014 moles / 0.4 M

Volume NaOH = 0.035 L or 35 mL

Therefore, we need 35 mL of 0.4 M NaOH to neutralize 35 mL of 0.4 M HCl to a pH of 7.

14. What volume can be prepared from 1.0M NaOH from 2.54 g NaOH?​


Molarity (M)Discussion:

Molarity is one of the commonly used units in chemistry. Molarity is defined as the number of moles of solute per liter of solution --- that is,

[tex]\boxed{\text{molarity} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{volume of solution (L)}}}[/tex]

For example, a 1.46 [tex]M[/tex] glucose solution contains 1.46 mol of the solute (glucose) in 1 L of the solution. Of course, we do not always work with solution volumes of 1 L. Thus, a 500-mL solution containing 0.730 mol of glucose also has a concentration of 1.46 [tex]M.[/tex]

[tex]\\[/tex]

Problem:

What volume can be prepared from 1.0 M NaOH from 2.54 g NaOH?

Given:

[tex]\text{mass of solute = 2.54 g}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molarity} = 1.0 \: M[/tex]

[tex]\text{solute = NaOH}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]\text{volume of solution}[/tex]

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of solute.

molar mass of solute = (22.99 g/mol × 1) + (16.00 g/mol × 1) + (1.008 g/mol × 1)

molar mass of solute = 39.998 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{molar mass of solute}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \frac{\text{2.54 g}}{\text{39.998 g/mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute = 0.063503 mol}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the volume of solution.

[tex]\text{molarity} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{volume of solution (L)}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{volume of solution} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{molarity}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{volume of solution} = \frac{\text{0.063503 mol}}{1.00 \: M}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{volume of solution = 0.0635 L or 63.5 mL}}[/tex]

30. 1. How many moles of NaOH are present in 45.0g NaOH?​


PROBLEM

How many moles of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) are present in 45.0 g NaOH?

ANSWER: There are 1.125 moles.

SOLUTION:

» Find the atomic mass of the constitutent elements, and the molar mass of the compound.

mass of sodium (Na) = 23 g/molmass of oxygen (O) = 16 g/molmass of hydrogen (H) = 1 g/mol

The molar mass of a substance is the sum of the mass of the atoms present in the chemical formula of the said compound. Thus, the formula mass for NaOH is

[tex] \quad \Rightarrow \rm{ (23 \:g/mol ) + (16 \:g/mol) + (1 \:g/mol)} [/tex]

[tex]\quad \large \Rightarrow \rm 40\:\:g/mol [/tex]

» Find the number of moles

[tex] \text{no. of moles} = \frac{\text{mass of substance in grams}}{\text{molar mass of substance}} [/tex]

[tex] \text{no. of moles} = \rm \frac{ 45.0\:g }{40.0\:g\cdot mol^{-1}} [/tex]

[tex] \text{no. of moles} = \rm \frac{ 45.0\:\cancel{g} }{40\:\cancel{g}\cdot mol^{-1}} [/tex]

[tex] \large \green{ \text{no. of moles} = \rm 1.125\:mol} [/tex]

Therefore, 1.125 moles of NaOH are present in 45.0g NaOH.
