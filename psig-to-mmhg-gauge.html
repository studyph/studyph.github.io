2. The gauge on a 15.0 L oxygen gas (O2) tank reads 4750 mmHg. How many liters of oxygen can you get from the tank at a pressure of 800 mmHg?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & P_1 = \text{4750 mmHg} \\ & V_1 = \text{15.0 L} \\ & P_2 = \text{800 mmHg} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the final volume by using Boyle's law.

[tex]\begin{aligned} P_1V_1 & = P_2V_2 \\ P_2V_2 & = P_1V_1 \\ \frac{P_2V_2}{P_2} & = \frac{P_1V_1}{P_2} \\ V_2 & = \frac{P_1V_1}{P_2} \\ & = \frac{(\text{4750 mmHg})(\text{15.0 L})}{\text{800 mmHg}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{89.1 L}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, there are 89.1 L of oxygen in the tank.

Given:

[tex]P_{1} = \text{300 mmHg}[/tex]

[tex]V_{1} = \text{5.0 L}[/tex]

Unknown:

[tex]V_{2}[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]V_{2} = V_{1} × \frac{P_{1}}{P_{2}}[/tex]

7. A gas occupies 12.3 liters at a pressure of 40.0 mmHg. What is the volume when the pressure is increased to 60.0 mmHg?


Given:

[tex]P_{1} = \text{40.0 mmHg}[/tex]

[tex]V_{1} = \text{12.3 L}[/tex]

[tex]P_{2} = \text{60.0 mmHg}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]P_{2}[/tex]

Strategy:

This is a gas law problem. What gas law should we use?

Since the given quantities are pressure and volume, we will use Boyle's law. According to this gas law, the pressure of a gas is inversely proportional to its volume keeping the amount of gas and its temperature constant.

The formula used for Boyle's law is

[tex]\boxed{P_{1}V_{1} = P_{2}V_{2}}[/tex]

where:

[tex]P_{1} = \text{initial pressure}[/tex]

[tex]V_{1} = \text{initial volume}[/tex]

[tex]P_{2} = \text{final pressure}[/tex]

[tex]V_{2} = \text{final volume}[/tex]

Solution:

Starting with the formula of Boyle's law

[tex]P_{1}V_{1} = P_{2}V_{2}[/tex]

Dividing both sides of the equation by P₂ to solve for V₂

[tex]V_{2} = V_{1} \times \dfrac{P_{1}}{P_{2}}[/tex]

Substituting the given values and solving for V₂

[tex]V_{2} = \text{12.3 L} \times \dfrac{\text{40.0 mmHg}}{\text{60.0 mmHg}}[/tex]

Therefore, the final volume is

[tex]\boxed{V_{2} = \text{8.20 L}}[/tex]

Answer:

[tex]V_{2} = \text{8.20 L}[/tex]

Problem:

In a hospital respiratory unit, the gauge in a 10-L tank of compressed oxygen gas reads 4500 mmHg how many liters of oxygen gas can you get from the tank at a pressure of 750 mmHg?

Given Data:

P1 (initial pressure) = 4500 mmHG      P2 (final pressure) = 750 mmHg

V1 (initial volume) = 10 L                        V2 (final volume) = ? unknown

Solution:

We can solved this problem using Boyle's Law which states that volume is inversely proportional to pressure if temperature and the amount of gas are constant.

The formula for Boyle's Law is P1V1 = P2V2

To find V2 we will use V2 = P1V1 / P2

V2 = P1V1 / P2  

     = 4500 mmHG × 10 L / 750 mmHg   (cancel the unit mmHg)

     = 45,000 L / 750

V2 = 60 L

24. The volume of a gas at 27°C and 700.0 mmHg is 600.0mL.What is the volume of the gas at 20°C and 500.0 mmHg?


The volume of the gas at 20 ° C and 500.0 mmHg: 820.4 ml

Further explanation

There are several gas equations used, with various conditions

Boyle's Law

At a constant temperature, the gas volume is inversely proportional to the pressure applied

[tex]\rm P1.V1 = P2.V2[/tex]

Gay Lussac's Law

When the volume is not changed, the gas pressure in the tube is proportional to its absolute temperature

[tex]\rm \dfrac {P1} {T1} = \dfrac {P2} {T2}[/tex]

Boyle-Gay-Lussac Law

Combined with Boyle's law and Gay Lussac's law

[tex]\rm \dfrac {P1.V1} {T1} = \dfrac {P2.V2} {T2}[/tex]

P1 = initial gas pressure (N / m² or Pa)

V1 = initial gas volume (m³)

P2 = final gas pressure

V2 = final gas volume

T1 = initial gas temperature (K)

T2 = final gas temperature

initial condition

volume of 600 mL

the pressure 700 mmHg

temperature 27 ° C = 27 + 273 = 300 K

final conditions

the pressure 500 mmHg

temperature 20 ° C = 20 +273 = 293 K

we use Boyle-Gay-Lussac Law  to find Volume (V2)

[tex]\rm \dfrac {P1V1} {T1} = \dfrac {P2.V2} {T2} \\\\\dfrac {700,600} {300} = \dfrac {500.V2} {293} \\\\ V2 = \boxed {\bold {820.4 \: mL}}[/tex]

25. A tank is filled with fresh water until there is a depth of 35 ft. Determine the pressure, psig, at the bottom of the tank​


Answer:

The pressure at the bottom of the tank is 15.17 psig

Explanation:

In this problem, we will use the concept of atmospheric pressure to solve the requirement and use psig as its unit of measurement.

Formula to use

P = ρgh

where

P     is the pressure, the given unit should be in psig (pounds per square

      inch, gauge)

ρ      is the density of freshwater

g      is the gravitational constant, 9.8 m/s²

Given information

h = 35 ft = 10.67 meters

ρ = 1000 kg/m³   (density of freshwater)

P = ?

Solving the problem

Let us solve the atmospheric pressure using the given formula

P = ρgh

P = (1000 kg/m³)(9.8 m/s²)(10.67 m)

P = 104,566 Pascal (Pa)

Now, let us convert pascal to psi using the conversion 1 Pa = 0.000145038 psig, we have:

P = 15.17 psig

Therefore, the pressure at the bottom of the tank is 15.17 psig
