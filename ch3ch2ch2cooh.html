The following compound is an isomer of CH3CH2CH2CH2OH? D. none of the above​.

Definition of Isomer:

Isomers are combinations of chemical compounds or more with the same formula but different structures and configurations—for example, propanol compounds (CH3CH2CHO) and dimethyl ketone compounds (CH3COCH3).

Conditions Isomer:

The condition for geometric and optical isomerism, respectively, is to have a rigid part of the molecule and a chiral C atom. One type of isomerism in carbon compounds is geometric isomerism and optical isomerism. Both are isomers in 3D space.

Types of Isomer:

Structural isomers have the same molecular formula and different atomic arrangements.Molecular isomers are groups of compounds with the same molecular formula but different atomic structures.Space isomers are compounds with the same molecular formula and structure, differing in their spatial or three-dimensional placement.

Position isomers have the same molecular formula and different positions of the functional groups. So that the composition of the framework of the compound is the same, but the position of the functional group of the compound is different. For example, butene (C₄H₈) can have position isomers of 1-butene and 2-butene. Based on the atom's position or atomic group, the geometric isomers are divided into cis and trans forms.

A cis isomer is an isomer in which atoms or groups of similar atoms are located on the same side.Trans isomers are isomers in which atoms or groups of similar atoms are located on opposite sides.

There are three isomers of butanol, namely 1-butanol (primary alcohol), 2-butanol (secondary alcohol), and 2-methyl-2-propanol (tertiary alcohol). So, the answer to this question is D. D. none of the above.

An electrolyte is a substance in an aqueous or molten form which is decomposed by passing an electric current through it. Electrolytes ionize to ions which are responsible for the conduction of electric charge.

Non-electrolytes are substances that do not ionize into cations and anions and thus do not conduct. They include molecular compounds such as gases.

Problem 1:

What is the molarity of a solution containing 25 grams of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution in 950 mL of solution?

Given:

[tex]\text{mass of solute = 25 g}[/tex]

[tex]\text{volume of solution = 950 mL = 0.950 L}[/tex]

[tex]\text{solute = NaOH}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]\text{molarity}[/tex]

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of solute.

molar mass of solute = (22.99 g/mol × 1) + (16.00 g/mol × 1) + (1.008 g/mol × 1)

molar mass of solute = 39.998 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{molar mass of solute}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \frac{\text{25 g}}{\text{39.998 g/mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute = 0.625031 mol}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molarity of solution.

[tex]\text{molarity} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{volume of solution (L)}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molarity} = \frac{\text{0.625031 mol}}{\text{0.950 L}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{molarity} = 0.66 \: M}[/tex]

[tex]\\[/tex]

Problem 2:

A solution of propanol (CH3CH2CH2OH) is prepared by dissolving 97 mL in enough water to have a final volume of 450 mL. What is the volume percent of the ethanol?

Given:

[tex]\text{volume of solute = 97 mL}[/tex]

[tex]\text{volume of solution = 450 mL}[/tex]

[tex]\text{solute} = \text{propanol}[/tex]

[tex]\text{solvent} = \text{water}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]\text{volume percent}[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]\text{volume percent} = \frac{\text{volume of solute}}{\text{volume of solution}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{volume percent} = \frac{\text{97 mL}}{\text{450 mL}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{volume percent} = 21.6\%}[/tex]

[tex]\\[/tex]

Problem 3:

If you need to make 5000.0 g of a 9.00% solution of sodium chloride, the mass of solute needs to be determined.

Given:

[tex]\text{mass of solution = 5000.0 g}[/tex]

[tex]\text{percent by mass = 9.00\%}[/tex]

[tex]\text{solute} = \text{NaCl}[/tex]

[tex]\text{solvent} = \text{water}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]\text{mass of solute}[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]\text{percent by mass} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solution}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{mass of solute} = \frac{\text{percent by mass}}{100} × \text{mass of solution}[/tex]

[tex]\text{mass of solute} = \frac{9.00}{100} × \text{5000.0 g}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass of solute = 450. g}}[/tex]

[tex]\\[/tex]

Problem 4:

Suppose that a solution was prepared by dissolving 45.0 g sugar into 250.0 g the water.

Given:

[tex]\text{mass of solute = 45.0 g}[/tex]

[tex]\text{mass of solvent = 250.0 g}[/tex]

[tex]\text{solute} = \text{sugar}[/tex]

[tex]\text{solvent} = \text{H}_{2}\text{O}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]\text{mass percent}[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]\text{mass percent} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solute + mass of solvent}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{mass percent} = \frac{\text{45.0 g}}{\text{45.0 g + 250.0 g}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass percent} = 15.25\%}[/tex]
