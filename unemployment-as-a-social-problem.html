17. What can be a lasting solution to the problems of inadequate wage and unemployment?​


Explanation:

There are a few ways to look at these problems.

First, the government solution:

Let’s think through how a government solution would exist. Government might create a bill which forces wages up (minimum wage statutes). But this is temporary since inflation (which occurs with continued government intervention like the minimum wage statutes just discussed) drives up the cost of living creating the feeling of wage inadequacy once again. So maybe just continuous minimum wage increases for eternity.

Next we’d have to create jobs by forcing employers to hire more. The interesting part of this is the minimum wage hikes will undoubtedly put many more out of a job, since they can’t be afforded by employers. Anyway, back to a supposed government solution. Some might say government could lower taxes on businesses allowing them to hire more. This might work. But let’s be serious, might doesn’t create a lasting solution for the problem of unemployment. The employer has too many choices now we’ve lower his taxes. Could we lower his taxes only if he hires more employees. This deduction wouldn’t work since too high of a deduction couldn’t be given or the government would cease to exist (too little income and the debt to GDP ratio too much making it very unfavorable to lend further causing default) while too little would cause employers to do nothing different because of the high cost of another employee. Neither the government nor employers would be satisfied with a deduction like this. Forcing them to not be so greedy and have a minimum increase each year in hiring based on the growth of the population.

We can imagine how any of these interventions create a quick trip to super-depression level disasters. Companies would quickly go out of business as they are forced to pay more for wages and an entire workforce to maintain government minimums. Government can’t seem to maintain the right balance. Friedrich Hayek taught us the which explains why central planners can’t figure out how to balance the ins and outs of the economy. There’s just no way to gather and act upon the knowledge needed to run the economy. The missing point in Hayek’s article is the specific kind of knowledge needed for healthy economic action. Free market prices allow us to get what we need to know to make decisions which will, as a society, keep bubbles and busts from happening again and again. Private property which allows, among other things, full control over a thing (e.g. a company, etc.) is what allows accurate pricing to exist. Hans-Hermanne Hope outlines this need in .

The only lasting solution I am aware of (there may be some other undiscovered method out there to make life better and easier) to the problems of inadequate wages and unemployment is allow the free market to work to its full potential. It will open up plenty of jobs and allow for everyone to be paid adequately for the job they perform. There is no guarantee there will be “enough” employment with companies looking to employ, but in a free market there is a greater likelyhood of opportunities for side hustles or entrepreneurial actions to make a living where there is much less of this with government. There’s no guarantee of wages which will make everyone wealthy, but they will at least be accurate. Then employees can know how they can maximaize their labor and time to make enough money to live to their desired potential.

A native of Lubao, Pampanga, Macapagal graduated from the University of the Philippines and University of Santo Tomas, both in Manila, after which he worked as a lawyer for the government. He first won election in 1949 to the House of Representatives, representing the 1st district in his home province of Pampanga. In 1957, he became vice president under the rule of President Carlos P. As President, Macapagal worked to suppress graft and corruption and to stimulate the Philippine economy. He introduced the country's first land reform law, placed the peso on the free currency exchange market, and liberalized foreign exchange and import controls. Many of his reforms, however, were crippled by a Congress dominated by the rival Nacionalista Party. He is also known for shifting the country's observance of Independence Day from July 4 to June 12, commemorating the day President Emilio Aguinaldo unilaterally declared the independence of the First Philippine Republic from the Spanish Empire in 1898. He stood for re-election in 1965, and was defeated by Ferdinand Marcos, who subsequently ruled for 21 years.

Under Marcos, Macapagal was elected president of the Constitutional Convention which would later draft what became the 1973 Constitution, though the manner in which the charter was ratified and modified led him to later question its legitimacy. He died of heart failure, pneumonia, and renal complications, in 1997, at the age of 86.

Macapagal was also a reputed poet in the Chinese and Spanish language, though his poetic oeuvre was eclipsed by his political biography.

30. How do we address unemployment using a multidisciplinary approach in the social sciences?​


Addressing unemployment through a multidisciplinary approach in the social sciences can involve the following steps:

Conducting research: To understand the causes and consequences of unemployment, research can be conducted in fields such as economics, sociology, psychology, and anthropology. This research can provide insights into the factors driving unemployment and its impact on individuals, communities, and the economy as a whole.

Developing policies: Based on the research findings, policymakers can develop programs and policies aimed at reducing unemployment. These policies can include job creation programs, workforce development initiatives, and training programs.

Collaborating with private sector: The public and private sectors can work together to create job opportunities, particularly in industries with a high demand for labor. This can include tax incentives for businesses that create jobs, and partnerships between businesses, government, and education institutions to provide training and job placement services.

Providing support services: Social welfare programs, such as unemployment benefits and job placement services, can provide financial and practical support to individuals who are unemployed. Mental health and counseling services can also be provided to support those who are struggling with the emotional impact of unemployment.

Addressing broader social and economic issues: To sustainably reduce unemployment, it may be necessary to address broader social and economic issues, such as income inequality, education and skills training, and the availability of affordable housing.

By utilizing a multidisciplinary approach, policies and programs can be developed that are based on a thorough understanding of the causes and consequences of unemployment and are tailored to the needs of individuals, communities, and the economy as a whole.

Answer:

To address unemployment using a multidisciplinary approach in the social sciences, different areas of study would need to work together to understand and tackle the issue from multiple angles. For example, economists could analyze labor market data and propose policies to increase job creation, sociologists could study the impact of unemployment on individuals and communities, and psychologists could examine the mental health consequences of unemployment and develop support systems. By combining insights from various disciplines, a more comprehensive solution to unemployment can be crafted.
