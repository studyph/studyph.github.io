3. Please answer seriously Fermentation is a complex chemical process of making wine by converting glucose into ethanol and carbon dioxide: C6H12O6 -> 2 C2H5OH + 2 CO2 a. Calculate the mass of ethanol (C2H5OH) produced if 200.0 grams of glucose reacts completely. b. Calculate the number of moles of carbon dioxide gas produced if 100.0 grams of glucose reacts. c. If 500.00 grams of ethanol were produced, how many grams of carbon dioxide would be formed? d. Calculate the number of moles glucose needed to produce 10.00 moles of ethanol. e. Calculate the mass in grams of glucose needed to produce 4.00 moles carbon dioxide.


Solution: (a)

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of substances needed.

For C₆H₁₂O₆

molar mass = (12.01 g/mol × 6) + (1.008 g/mol × 12) + (16.00 g/mol × 6)

molar mass = 180.156 g/mol

For C₂H₅OH

molar mass = (12.01 g/mol × 2) + (1.008 g/mol × 6) (16.00 g/mol × 1)

molar mass = 46.068 g/mol

Step 2: Determine the mole ratio needed.

mole ratio = 1 mol C₆H₁₂O₆ : 2 mol C₂H₅OH

Step 3: Calculate the mass of C₂H₅OH produced using the mole ratio.

[tex]\text{mass of C₂H₅OH = 200.0 g C₆H₁₂O₆} × \frac{\text{1 mol C₆H₁₂O₆}}{\text{180.156 g C₆H₁₂O₆}} × \frac{\text{2 mol C₂H₅OH}}{\text{1 mol C₆H₁₂O₆}} × \frac{\text{46.068 g C₂H₅OH}}{\text{1 mol C₂H₅OH}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass of C₂H₅OH = 102.3 g}}[/tex]

Solution: (b)

Step 1: Determine the mole ratio needed.

mole ratio = 1 mol C₆H₁₂O₆ : 2 mol CO₂

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of CO₂ used using the mole ratio.

[tex]\text{moles of CO₂ = 100.0 g C₆H₁₂O₆} × \frac{\text{1 mol C₆H₁₂O₆}}{\text{180.156 g C₆H₁₂O₆}} × \frac{\text{2 mol CO₂}}{\text{1 mol C₆H₁₂O₆}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{moles of CO₂ = 1.11015 mol}}[/tex]

Solution: (c)

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of substances needed.

For CO₂

molar mass = (12.01 g/mol × 1) + (16.00 g/mol × 2)

molar mass = 44.01 g/mol

Step 2: Determine the mole ratio needed.

mole ratio = 2 mol CO₂ : 2 mol C₂H₅OH

Step 3: Calculate the mass of CO₂ formed using the mole ratio.

[tex]\text{mass of CO₂ = 500.00 g C₂H₅OH} × \frac{\text{1 mol C₂H₅OH}}{\text{46.068 g C₂H₅OH}} × \frac{\text{2 mol CO₂}}{\text{2 mol C₂H₅OH}} × \frac{\text{44.01 g CO₂}}{\text{1 mol CO₂}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass of CO₂ = 477.66 g}}[/tex]

Solution: (d)

[tex]\text{moles of C₆H₁₂O₆ = 10.00 mol C₂H₅OH} × \frac{\text{1 mol C₆H₁₂O₆}}{\text{2 mol C₂H₅OH}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{moles of C₆H₁₂O₆ = 5.000 mol}}[/tex]

Solution: (e)

[tex]\text{mass of C₆H₁₂O₆ = 4.00 mol CO₂} × \frac{\text{1 mol C₆H₁₂O₆}}{\text{2 mol CO₂}} × \frac{\text{180.156 g C₆H₁₂O₆}}{\text{1 mol C₆H₁₂O₆}}[/tex]

Write a balanced chemical equation of the decomposition of glucoseb. What is the mole-mole ratio of glucose (C2H,20) to ethanol (CHEOH)c. What is the molar mass of glucose?d. What is the molar mass of ethanol?e. How much ethanol is produced from the starting material of 987.1 g of glucose?​


Answer:

a. C6H12O6 → 2C2H5OH + 2CO2

b. mole ratio = 1 mol C6H12O6 : 2 mol C2H5OH

c. MM glucose = 180.15 g/mol

d. MM ethanol = 46.068 g/mol

e. mass ethanol = 504.84 g

Explanation:

a. Write a balanced chemical equation of the decomposition of glucose.

C6H12O6 → 2C2H5OH + 2CO2

b. What is the mole-mole ratio of glucose (C6H12O6) to ethanol (C2H5OH)?

mole ratio = 1 mol C6H12O6 : 2 mol C2H5OH

c. What is the molar mass of glucose?

MM glucose = 6(12.011) + 12(1.0079) + 6(15.999)

MM glucose = 180.15 g/mol

d. What is the molar mass of ethanol?

MM ethanol = 2(12.011) + 6(1.0079) + 15.999

MM ethanol = 46.068 g/mol

e. How much ethanol is produced from the starting material of 987.1 g of glucose?

mass ethanol = 987.1 g glucose × (1 mol glucose / 180.15 g glucose) × (2 mol ethanol / 1 mol glucose) × (46.068 g ethanol / 1 mol ethanol)

mass ethanol = 504.84 g

26. Carbon Compound Chemical Formula Elements bonded to the carbon atom in the structure 1. Methane 2 Carbon dioxide 3 Ethanol 4. Glycine 5. Acetone 6. Trimethylphosphine 7. Chloroform​


Answer:

Opening Essay

Hydrocarbons are the simplest organic compounds, but they have interesting physiological effects. These effects depend on the size of the hydrocarbon molecules and where on or in the body they are applied. Alkanes of low molar mass—those with from 1 to approximately 10 or so carbon atoms—are gases or light liquids that act as anesthetics. Inhaling (“sniffing”) these hydrocarbons in gasoline or aerosol propellants for their intoxicating effect is a major health problem that can lead to liver, kidney, or brain damage or to immediate death by asphyxiation by excluding oxygen.

Swallowed, liquid alkanes do little harm while in the stomach. In the lungs, however, they cause “chemical” pneumonia by dissolving fatlike molecules from cell membranes in the tiny air sacs (alveoli). The lungs become unable to expel fluids, just as in pneumonia caused by bacteria or viruses. People who swallow gasoline or other liquid alkane mixtures should not be made to vomit, as this would increase the chance of getting alkanes into the lungs. (There is no home-treatment antidote for gasoline poisoning; call a poison control center.)

Liquid alkanes with approximately 5–16 carbon atoms per molecule wash away natural skin oils and cause drying and chapping of the skin, while heavier liquid alkanes (those with approximately 17 or more carbon atoms per molecule) act as emollients (skin softeners). Such alkane mixtures as mineral oil and petroleum jelly can be applied as a protective film. Water and aqueous solutions such as urine will not dissolve such a film, which explains why petroleum jelly protects a baby’s tender skin from diaper rash.

We begin our study of organic chemistry with the alkanes, compounds containing only two elements, carbon and hydrogen, and having only single bonds. There are several other kinds of hydrocarbons, distinguished by the types of bonding between carbon atoms and by the properties that result from that bonding. Later in this chapter we will examine hydrocarbons with double bonds, with triple bonds, and with a special kind of bonding called aromaticity.
