26. Differentiate the Fundamental Counting Principle (Repetition and Not Repetition is Required)?​


Answer:

The Fundamental Counting Principle is a method used to determine the total number of possible outcomes for a combination of events.

For repetition allowed, the principle states that if there are m ways to do one thing and n ways to do another thing, then there are m x n ways to do both things.

For repetition not allowed, the principle states that if there are m ways to do one thing and n ways to do another thing, then there are m x (n-1) ways to do both things. This is because once one thing has been done, there is one less option for the second thing.

Answer:

Explanation:

anaphora is a rhetorical device that consists of repeating a sequence of words at the beginnings of neighboring clauses, thereby lending them emphasis. In contrast, an epistrophe is repeating words at the clauses' ends. The combination of anaphora and epistrophe results in symploce.
