2. state the pairing rule in DNA​


The rules of base pairing (or nucleotide pairing) are:
A with T: the purine adenine (A) always pairs with the pyrimidine thymine (T)
C with G: the pyrimidine cytosine (C) always pairs with the purine guanine (G)
This is consistent with there not being enough space (20 Å) for two purines to fit within the helix and too much space for two pyrimidines to get close enough to each other to form hydrogen bonds between them.

But why not A with C and G with T?

The answer: only with A & T and with C & G are there opportunities to establish hydrogen bonds (shown here as dotted lines) between them (two between A & T; three between C & G). These relationships are often called the rules of Watson-Crick base pairing, named after the two scientists who discovered their structural basis.

The rules of base pairing tell us that if we can "read" the sequence of nucleotides on one strand of DNA, we can immediately deduce the complementary sequence on the other strand.

The rules of base pairing explain the phenomenon that whatever the amount of adenine (A) in the DNA of an organism, the amount of thymine (T) is the same (called Chargaff's rule). Similarly, whatever the amount of guanine (G), the amount of cytosine (C) is the same.

The C+G:A+T ratio varies from organism to organism (particularly among the bacteria), but within the limits of experimental error, A = T and C = G

17. What evidence is it, where organisms have the most DNA sequences in common.​


Answer:

The evidence that organisms have the most DNA sequences in common comes from the field of molecular biology and genetics. Scientists have conducted extensive studies on the DNA sequences of various organisms and have found that all living things share a significant amount of DNA.

One of the most compelling pieces of evidence for this is the fact that the genetic code, which determines the sequence of amino acids in proteins, is universal across all living things. This means that the same DNA code is used to create proteins in all organisms, from bacteria to humans.

Furthermore, studies of DNA sequences have shown that organisms that are more closely related in evolutionary terms have more similar DNA sequences. For example, humans and chimpanzees share about 98% of their DNA, while humans and bacteria share far less.

Finally, scientists have also used DNA sequence comparisons to construct phylogenetic trees, which show the evolutionary relationships between different organisms. These trees are based on the idea that the more similar the DNA sequences between two organisms, the more closely related they are.

Overall, the evidence for common DNA sequences among living things is strong and has been extensively studied and documented by molecular biologists and geneticists.
