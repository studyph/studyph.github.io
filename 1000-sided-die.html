Step-by-step explanation:

To calculate the probability of getting a perfect square, we need to first find the probability of getting each number on the die. We are told that 1 and 5 occur twice as often as 2 and 4, and 2 and 4 occur three times as often as 3. Let's call the probability of rolling a 1 or 5 "p1", the probability of rolling a 2 or 4 "p2", and the probability of rolling a 3 "p3".

Since 1 and 5 occur twice as often as 2 and 4, we know that:

p1 + p5 = 2(p2 + p4)

Similarly, since 2 and 4 occur three times as often as 3:

p2 + p4 = 3p3

We also know that the sum of all probabilities must equal 1:

p1 + p2 + p3 + p4 + p5 = 1

We can use these equations to solve for the probabilities of each number:

Starting with the second equation:

p2 + p4 = 3p3

Since we know that p1 + p5 = 2(p2 + p4), we can substitute p2 + p4 = (p1 + p5)/2:

(p1 + p5)/2 = 3p3

Solving for p3:

p3 = (p1 + p5)/6

Using the first and last equations, we can also solve for p1 + p5:

p1 + p5 = 1 - p2 - p3 - p4

Substituting p3 = (p1 + p5)/6 and p2 + p4 = (p1 + p5)/2:

p1 + p5 = 1 - (p1 + p5)/2 - (p1 + p5)/6 - (p1 + p5)/2

Solving for p1 + p5:

p1 + p5 = 2/5

Now we can solve for each individual probability:

p2 + p4 = 3p3 = (p1 + p5)/2 = 2/15

p3 = (p1 + p5)/6 = 1/15

Finally, we can calculate the probability of rolling a perfect square:

There are two perfect squares on the die: 1 and 4. The probability of rolling a 1 is p1/5, and the probability of rolling a 4 is p4/5.

So the probability of rolling a perfect square is:

p1/5 + p4/5

2(p2 + p4)/5 + 2(p2 + p4)/5
