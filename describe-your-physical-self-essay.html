Physical and Social Changes

Some Physical Changes that happen to learners

(Choose what's applicable to you)

Physical changes are visible to everybody, emphasizing the scope and speed of change. As a result, certain teens may feel more or less mature than others. Girls have physical development growth spurts two years earlier than boys.

Girls

The formation of breast buds is the first puberty alteration in girls. When the breast and nipple rise, this is what it means. At this period, the areola (the black region of skin that surrounds the breast nipple) grows in size. The breasts continue to grow in size after that. The nipples and areolas will eventually rise again. On the breasts, they then produce a new protrusion. Only the nipple remains raised above the rest of the breast tissue in adulthood.

Boys

The expansion of the scrotum and testes is the first puberty alteration in boys. The penis does not enlarge at this point. The penis then becomes longer as the testes and scrotum grow larger. The penis will then continue to expand in size and length.

Both

Both girls and boys develop pubic hair in the same way. Hair grows long and soft in the beginning, but just in a small area surrounding the genitals. As the hair becomes longer and thicker, it becomes darker and coarser. Pubic hair eventually resembles adult hair in appearance, but in a smaller region. It can extend to the thighs and up the stomach in certain cases.

Some Social Changes that happen to learners

(Choose what's applicable to you)

Young people are busy trying to figure out who they are and where they belong in the world. Gender, peer group, cultural background, media, school, and family expectations can all impact this search.desiring greater independence: this is likely to have an impact on your child's decisions and connections with family and friends.a desire to take on extra responsibilities at home and at schoollooking for new experiences: because of the nature of adolescent brain development, teenagers are more prone to seek out new experiences and take greater risks. However, kids are still learning to manage their impulses. begin to acquire a stronger individual set of values and morals as a result of thinking more about "right" and "wrong." Teenagers also learn that they are in charge of their own behaviors, decisions, and outcomes. They are asking more questions. Your child's concept of "right" and "wrong" is shaped by your words and deeds.Friends have a greater influence on their behavior, sense of self, and self-esteem.Beginning to develop and explore a sexual identity: your child may begin to date or have romantic connections. These aren't necessarily close friendships. Intimate or sexual relationships do not occur until later in life for some young people.
