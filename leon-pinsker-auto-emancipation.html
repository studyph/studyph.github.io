Abstract This paper explores a contrast between the Marxist and feminist traditions of emancipatory social theory: whereas in the Marxist tradition theorists have spent considerable time and energy discussing the problem of the viability of classlessness as an emancipatory project, feminists have spent relatively little time defending the viability of a society without male domination. The paper argues that this difference in preoccupations reflects, at least to some extent, differences in the relationship between prefigurative egalitarian micro experiences and macro institutional change with respect to gender oppression and class oppression. The paper also explores the implications of this contrast for the kinds of explanatory theory developed within the two traditions. Marxists' greater tendency than feminists to seek relatively deterministic accounts of the demise of the form of oppression on which they focus is viewed as at least partially a way of contending with the difficulty in establishing the viability of the emancipatory project of  classlessness.

22. evidence in the Emancipation Proclamation itself


Answer:

It was a presidential proclamation: The Emancipation Proclamation was issued by President Abraham Lincoln, who had the constitutional authority to issue such a proclamation.It declared that all slaves in Confederate states were free: The Proclamation declared that all slaves in states that had seceded from the Union and were currently in rebellion against the United States would be "forever free" from January 1, 1863, onwards.It was a military measure: The Proclamation was issued as a military measure to help the Union army in the Civil War. By declaring the slaves in Confederate states to be free, it was hoped that they would join the Union army, thus weakening the Confederate forces.It did not apply to slaves in non-rebellious states: The Proclamation did not apply to slaves in non-rebellious states, such as the border states, which remained loyal to the Union.It did not abolish slavery entirely: The Emancipation Proclamation did not abolish slavery entirely in the United States. It only applied to slaves in Confederate states that were in rebellion against the Union.
