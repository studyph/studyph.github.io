9. what are the gaps between the data units?​


Answer:

The gaps between data units refer to the differences in values between consecutive data points in a set of data. For example, if a data set includes the values 1, 3, 5, 7, and 9, the gaps between the data units would be 2, 2, 2, and 2. These gaps can be used to analyze the distribution and dispersion of the data, as well as to identify patterns or trends in the data.

In some cases, the gaps between data units may be irregular, meaning that the differences between consecutive data points are not constant. For example, a data set that includes the values 1, 3, 6, 10, and 15 would have gaps of 2, 3, 4, and 5. In this case, the gaps between the data units are not constant and may indicate that there are underlying patterns or trends in the data that are not immediately apparent.

It's important to note that the size of the gaps between data units does not necessarily indicate the importance or significance of the data itself. Instead, the gaps between data units should be analyzed in context and in relation to other characteristics of the data, such as the mean, median, and standard deviation.

20. too much gap between joints​


ANSWER:

TOO MUCH GAP BETWEEN JOINTS:

Joint cartilage allows your joints to move freely and absorb impact. As you get older, the cartilage in your joints can start to become worn, especially in your knees, hips, and hands. Losing this cartilage makes it much harder for your joints to handle everyday movements and tasks.

After much of the cartilage has worn away, you may start to feel pain. Moving your joints may be more difficult. The pain can also mean that the space between the bones of the joint has narrowed enough to change the joint’s range of motion.

When joint space narrowing occurs, the cartilage no longer keeps the bones a normal distance apart. This can be painful as the bones rub or put too much pressure on each other.

Joint space narrowing can also be a result of conditions such as osteoarthritis (OA) or rheumatoid arthritis (RA). If you feel abnormal pain in your joints, your doctor may want to order X-rays or other imaging tests.

These will help the doctor look for any narrowing in the painful joint. Then, based on your results, your doctor may suggest a treatment plan or lifestyle changes to address the cause and reduce the pain.
