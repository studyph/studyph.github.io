Then comes the knot. Of course, we are not talking about sailing knots, such as Figure-8 Knot. A nautical knot is a unit of speed equal to one nautical mile per hour (1 knot = 1.15 miles per hour) or approximately 1.15078 mph.

d = r * t

where d is the displacement (the distance and direction the ship has traveled), r is the distance traveled (38 nautical miles), and t is the direction (75 degrees).

We can use this formula to calculate the ship's displacement as follows:

d = 38 nautical miles * (75 degrees)

= 2,850 nautical miles

Next, we can calculate the position of ship B using the same formula:

d = r * t

where d is the displacement (the distance and direction the ship has traveled), r is the distance traveled (22 nautical miles), and t is the direction (30 degrees).

We can use this formula to calculate the ship's displacement as follows:

d = 22 nautical miles * (30 degrees)

= 660 nautical miles

Finally, we can use the positions of the two ships to calculate the distance between them. To do this, we will subtract the position of ship B from the position of ship A using vector subtraction:

d = 2,850 nautical miles - 660 nautical miles

= 2,190 nautical miles

Therefore, the distance between the two ships is 2,190 nautical miles.

As of 2019, China controls 20 outposts in the Paracel Islands and 7 in the Spratlys.[11]
