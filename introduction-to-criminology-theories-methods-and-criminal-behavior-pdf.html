10. Write the explanation of cultural theory in relation to crime and criminal behavior.​


Answer:

Cultural theory in criminology posits that criminal behavior is influenced by cultural values, beliefs, and norms. This theory suggests that individuals who engage in criminal behavior have been exposed to and internalized cultural messages that support and justify such behavior. These cultural messages may include glorification of violence, materialism, and the pursuit of pleasure without regard for the consequences. Cultural theorists argue that the social and cultural context in which individuals live plays a critical role in shaping their attitudes towards criminal behavior. For instance, people who live in high-crime neighborhoods may be more likely to view criminal behavior as a legitimate means of achieving their goals or solving problems. Cultural theory also emphasizes the importance of subcultures in shaping attitudes towards criminal behavior. In some subcultures, criminal behavior may be viewed as a way of gaining respect, status, or protection. Overall, cultural theory suggests that criminal behavior cannot be understood without considering the cultural context in which it occurs.

Cultural theory, also known as cultural criminology, is a theoretical perspective that explains crime and criminal behavior as a result of cultural factors. According to cultural theorists, criminal behavior is influenced by a wide range of cultural factors such as values, beliefs, attitudes, and norms, which vary across different societies and social groups.

Cultural theorists argue that criminal behavior is not simply a matter of individual choice or biology, but is rather shaped by larger cultural forces. For instance, cultural theorists point out that some forms of criminal behavior are considered acceptable or even desirable in certain cultural contexts. They suggest that some individuals may engage in criminal behavior because it is seen as a way to achieve status or respect within their cultural group.

Furthermore, cultural theorists argue that the criminal justice system is also influenced by cultural factors. They argue that the laws, policies, and practices of the criminal justice system reflect the values and beliefs of the society in which they operate. For example, some cultural theorists have pointed out that the criminal justice system in Western societies is focused on punishing individuals for their crimes, whereas in other societies, criminal justice may be more focused on rehabilitating offenders.

Overall, cultural theory provides a useful framework for understanding the complex relationship between culture and criminal behavior. By examining the cultural factors that influence criminal behavior, cultural theorists hope to provide a more comprehensive understanding of crime and develop more effective strategies for preventing and addressing it.

18. Write the explanation of Differential Association Theory in relation to crimes and criminal behavior.​


Answer:

Differential Association Theory is a criminological theory that explains how individuals learn criminal behavior through their interactions with others. The theory proposes that criminal behavior is not innate or predetermined, but rather, it is learned through social interactions with others who engage in criminal behavior.

According to this theory, individuals who are exposed to criminal behavior and attitudes through their social networks are more likely to engage in criminal behavior themselves. The theory suggests that individuals are more likely to engage in criminal behavior if they associate with others who have favorable attitudes towards such behavior, and less likely to engage in criminal behavior if they associate with individuals who have unfavorable attitudes towards it.

Furthermore, the theory states that the frequency, intensity, and duration of an individual's exposure to criminal behavior and attitudes will determine the likelihood of their engaging in criminal behavior. If an individual is exposed to criminal behavior and attitudes more frequently, for a longer period of time, and in more intense settings, they are more likely to engage in criminal behavior themselves.

In summary, Differential Association Theory suggests that criminal behavior is learned through social interactions with others who engage in criminal behavior, and the frequency, intensity, and duration of exposure to criminal behavior and attitudes will determine an individual's likelihood of engaging in criminal behavior.

1. Features of Criminal Law

The life of the criminal law begins with willingly take the prescribed legal consequences. As far as the law is concerned, criminal conduct is to be avoided. This is so whether or not we are willing to take the consequences.

It is possible to imagine a world in which the law gets its way in which people uniformly refrain from criminal conduct. Obviously enough this is not the world in which we live. Imagine D is about to . If ing is a crime, reasonable force may permissibly be used to prevent Doing. Police officers and private persons alike have powers to arrest D, and reasonable force may permissibly be used to make arrests effective.

These powers and permissions exist ex ante—prior, that is, to the commission of crime. We can add those that exist ex post—once crime has been committed. Imagine now that D has oed. As well as the power to arrest D, the criminal law confers a set of investigative powers designed to help generate evidence of D’s criminality: these include powers to stop and search, to carry out surveillance, and to detain suspects for questioning. If sufficient evidence is produced, and it is in the public interest to do so, D may be charged with a crime. To exercise these powers is to impose new duties on DD must submit to the search, remain in detention, and turn up in court when required. For D to do otherwise absent justification or excuseis itself criminal. So reasonable force can permissibly be used against D if she refuses to co-operate.

The powers and permissions mentioned so far help D’s accusers put together their case against D. By the time cases reach the courts those accusers are typically state officials (or those to whom the state has delegated official power). Some legal systems do make space for private prosecutions. But such prosecutions can be discontinued or taken over by state officials (and their delegates). Those officials (and delegates) can also continue proceedings in the face of private opposition, even when the opposition comes from those wronged by D. In this way, the state exercises a form of control over criminal proceedings that is absent from legal proceedings of other kinds (Marshall and Duff 1998).

It may seem from the above that criminal proceedings are tilted heavily in favour of the accusing side. But the criminal law also confers rights on the accused that help protect D against her accusers (Ashworth and Zedner 2010, 82). These typically include the right to be informed of the accusations in question, the right to confidential access to a lawyer, and the privilege against self-incrimination. Most important of all, perhaps, is the right to be tried before an independent court that respects the presumption of innocence—that requires the prosecution to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that D committed the crime.

At least on paper, the procedural protections on offer in criminal proceedings are more robust than those available to the accused in legal proceedings of other kinds. This is explained in large part by the consequences of criminal conviction. If D is found guilty in a criminal court, D gains a criminal record. Depending on the crime, D may be disenfranchised, banned from certain professions, refused entry to other countries, and declined access to insurance, housing, and education (Hoskins 2014; 2016). This is to say nothing of criminal sentences themselves. Those sentences are typically punishments: the object of the exercise is that D suffer some harm or deprivation; D is to be made worse off than she otherwise would have been. This is not to say that suffering or deprivation must be the ultimate end of those who punish.

The Italian criminologist Cesare Lombroso (1835-1909) devised the now-outmoded theory that criminality is determined by physiological traits. Called the father of modern criminology, he concentrated attention on the study of the individual offender.

Born in Verona on Nov. 6, 1835, Cesare Lombroso studied medicine at the universities of Pavia, Padua, Vienna, and Genoa. His interests in psychology and psychiatry merged with his study of the physiology and anatomy of the brain and ultimately led to his anthropometric analysis of criminals. While he was in charge of the insane at hospitals in Pavia, Pesaro, and Reggio Emilia (1863-1872), his interest in physiognomical characteristics of the mentally disturbed increased.

In 1876 Lombroso became professor of legal medicine and public hygiene at the University of Turin. That year he wrote his most important and influential work, L'uomo delinquente, which went through five editions in Italian and was published in various European languages but never in English. A deep and lasting friendship developed between Lombroso and his chief student, Enrico Ferri, who became Italy's leading criminologist.

30. Write the explanation of Containment Theory in relation to crimes and criminal behavior.​


The Containment Theory is a theory in criminology that seeks to explain criminal behavior by examining factors that push individuals towards criminal activity and factors that pull individuals away from criminal activity. The theory was first proposed by sociologists Walter Reckless and Simon Dinitz in the 1960s.

According to the Containment Theory, there are two types of factors that influence criminal behavior: external and internal. External factors include social and environmental influences, such as poverty, unemployment, and exposure to crime. Internal factors include psychological and personal characteristics, such as self-esteem, self-control, and resilience.

The theory suggests that individuals who are able to resist these external and internal factors are able to "contain" their criminal impulses and avoid engaging in criminal behavior. Reckless and Dinitz identified two types of containment: outer containment and inner containment.

Outer containment refers to the social and environmental factors that prevent individuals from engaging in criminal behavior. These factors include family, school, and community structures that provide positive role models, social support, and opportunities for legitimate activities. Outer containment also includes the criminal justice system, which provides a deterrent to criminal behavior through the threat of punishment.

Inner containment refers to the psychological and personal factors that prevent individuals from engaging in criminal behavior. These factors include a strong sense of self-esteem, self-control, and the ability to resist negative influences and peer pressure.

According to the Containment Theory, individuals who lack outer and inner containment are more likely to engage in criminal behavior. They may lack positive role models, social support, and opportunities for legitimate activities, and they may also have low self-esteem, poor impulse control, and a tendency to succumb to negative influences.

The Containment Theory has been influential in shaping modern criminology and has helped to inform policies and interventions aimed at preventing and reducing criminal behavior. By understanding the factors that push individuals towards criminal activity and the factors that pull individuals away from criminal activity, researchers and practitioners can develop strategies that promote positive social and psychological development and reduce the risk of criminal behavior.
