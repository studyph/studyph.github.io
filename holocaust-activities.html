2. Where did the holocaust happen Saan nangyari ang holocaust


Answer:

German Reich (N@zi Germany) and German-occupied Europe

Explanation:

The Holocaust, also known as the Shoah, was the genocide of European Jews during World War II. Between 1941 and 1945, Nazi Germany and its collaborators systematically murdered some six million Jews across German-occupied Europe, around two-thirds of Europe's Jewish population. The murders were carried out in pogroms and mass shootings; by a policy of extermination through labor in concentration camps; and in gas chambers and gas vans in German extermination camps, chiefly Auschwitz, Bełżec, Chełmno, Majdanek, Sobibór, and Treblinka in occupied Poland.

5. Explain the History and the aftermath of the holocaust


Answer:

History:

The Holocaust was the systematic persecution and murder of 6 million Jews, organized by the Nazi State and its collaborators from 1933 to 1945.

In addition to committing genocide against the Jews, the Nazis committed genocide against the Roma and the Sinti.

Other marginalized groups were also persecuted during this period: people with disabilities, homosexuals, Slavic people, political opponents, and Jehovah’s Witnesses.

Aftermath:

For survivors, the prospect of rebuilding their lives after the Holocaust was daunting. Many feared to return to their former homes.

7. Why was the poem titled" Holocaust"?​


Answer:

Early poets of the Holocaust, including survivors Paul Celan and Nelly Sachs, wrestled with a variety of interrelated subjects based on their experiences, including profound loss and the nature of victimhood. These issues manifested themselves in poems of stark power and beauty. Later poets have explored themes of memory and Jewish identity in a world “after Auschwitz.” That so many of these works are accessible to many readers only in translation compounds the issues raised by these poems.

13. how will you describe the Holocaust?​


Answer:

The word ‘holocaust’ comes from ancient Greek and means ‘burnt offering’. Even before the Second World War, the word was sometimes used to describe the death of a large group of people, but since 1945, it has become almost synonymous with the murder of the European Jews during the Second World War. That's why we use the term 'the Holocaust'. Jews also refer to it with the word ‘Shoah’, which is Hebrew for 'catastrophe.

19. Nazi Germany and the Holocaust


Answer: Nazi Germany (most commonly stated as being mainly between 1933-1945) was a totalitarian dictatorship, originally built on the founding principles of nationalism. Documents such as the Weimar Constitution laid out an establish federal government, which would later grow into an industrialized superpower with ambitions of global domination. “Holocaust” is a Greek word meaning “sacrifice by fire”. For the Nazis, the objective of the so-called “Final Solution” was the killing of all Jews in Europe to create a "better, white society". This quickly opened up to a variety of different groups, including but not limited to; people of African descent, homosexuals, mentally and physically disabled people, soviet prisoners, communists, socialists, and many other ethnic groups, such as "Romas". These people were horrifically treated in Nazi Germany and were either murdered, brutalized, or eventually both if they were sent to concentration camps- where they were subjected to some of the worst treatment humans have ever experienced. It is estimated that around 11 million people were killed during the Holocaust
