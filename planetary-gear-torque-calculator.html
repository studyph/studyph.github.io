4. Torque can be calculated by multiplying the force (N) applied at 90∘ to the lever arm at a distance (m) from the pivot point (point of rotation), the compound SI unit for the torque is N⋅m. If the force (at 90∘ to the lever arm) applied is 15 N and it is applied at 2.0 m from the pivot point (point of rotation), what is the torque on the lever?


Hi Fianmark4582,

You are indeed correct that Torque is the product of force applied at an angle (but not just 90 degrees) to the lever arm at a distance from the pivot point. But we cannot just multiply 90 degrees to the equation. It is because there is a different torque applied to the lever arm even the force and the distance is still the same. Here's why:

Torque formula

The equation for computing torque is described as

[tex]T=Fd sin\theta[/tex]

such that:

T = Torque (expressed in Nm)

F = Force applied to the lever arm

sin = trigonometric function sine

[tex]\theta[/tex] = the angle through which the force is applied

Sine is one of the trigonometric functions that is essential in solving physics problems like this one.

if the angle changes so as the torque even if the force applied and the distance from the pivot point did not change.

To solve your problem, we need to apply the said formula.

[tex]T=Fd sin\theta[/tex]

[tex]T=(15N)(2.0m) sin(90)[/tex]

[tex]T=(30) (1)[/tex]

[tex]T=30 Nm[/tex]

Keep in mind that we need to express the torque with the appropriate units which is Newton-meter (Nm). This is one of the common mistake made by students who forgot to place/write the appropriate unit to their solutions.

Now the torque on the lever is 30 Nm.

25. 30 Newton•meters of torque is required to close a door 1.5 meters wide. What force is needed to cause this torque?


Answer:

The force is 20N.

Explanation:

In our case, this problem is related to Moment of a Force. The moment here is also called torque which is the rotational effect to open the door.

For the formula, we are going to use

T = Fr

where

T     is the moment of a force or torque, unit is in N.m (but not Joules)

F     is the force, unit is in Newtons, N

r      is the distance perpendicular to the force from a pivot, unit is in meter

Given information

T = 30 N.m

r = 1.5 m

F = ?

Solving the problem

To solve for the force, use the formula for torque, we have:

T = Fr

30 N.m = F (1.5 m)

Divide both sides of the equation by 1.5 m, we have:

[tex]\frac{30N.m}{1.5m}=\frac{F}{1.5m}[/tex]

F = 20 N

Therefore, the force to open the door is 20N.

29. Is it possible (a) for a large force to produce a small, or even zero, torque and (b) for a small force to produce a large torque?


Torque is a rotational force to an object or twisting force in simple terms.
One good example is you and your friend riding a seesaw.

Torque must have a force (you and your friends' weight) and a moment arm or radius (in this case the length of the seesaw from the fulcrum/pivot to your position). Formula is T = F x r, where T is the Torque, F is force and r is radius.

To answer your questions:
(a) Yes it is possible to produce small/zero torque with a large force. In the seesaw case, the more you get near closer to the pivot or fulcrum, the smaller the torque no matter how heavy you are. Zero torque is possible if you sit in the pivot point, thus your radius is zero also.
(b) Yes again. Even though you have a small force, if your radius is large, it can directly affect your torque. In the seesaw case, try sitting on the end part of the seesaw, even if your weight (force) is small, it can greatly increase your torque.

30. Define torque qualitatively and quantitatively


Quantitatively, it is define as

T = F * r * sin(theta)
T = torque
F = linear force
r = distance from the axis of rotation to where the linear force is applied
theta = the angle between F and r
