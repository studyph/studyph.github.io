8. Why alchemy is not considered as a field of science?


Answer:

Alchemy is built on a false premise: by applying chemical operations (distilling, burning, mixing with acids, filtering, etc.) to various substances, elements can be transmuted. In particular, lead can be transmuted into gold. But that’s not how it works. Gold and lead are elements, which cannot be transformed by chemical processes. Lead can be turned into gold, but it requires nuclear, not chemical means. Alchemy might be regarded as a proto-science, since it employed techniques later used in the actual science of chemistry and rudimentary scientific processes every now and again, but as an enterprise, alchemy was not a science.

15. analysis about alchemy​


Explanation:

The word “alchemy” brings to mind a cauldron-full of images: witches hovering over a boiling brew, or perhaps sorcerers in smoky labs or cluttered libraries. Despite these connotations of the mythic and mystical, alchemical practice played an important role in the evolution of modern science.

Historically, alchemy refers to both the investigation of nature and an early philosophical and spiritual discipline that combined chemistry with metal work. Alchemy also encompassed physics, medicine, astrology, mysticism, spiritualism, and art. The goals of alchemy were:

to find the “elixir of life” (it was thought that this magical elixir would bring wealth, health, and immortality);

to find or make a substance called the “philosopher’s stone,” which when heated and combined with “base” (nonprecious metals such as copper and iron) would turn it into gold, thought to be the highest and purest form of matter; and

to discover the relationship of humans to the cosmos and use that understanding to improve the human spirit.

Alchemy was scientific but it was also a spiritual tradition. Some of its practitioners had altruistic intentions. For instance, if alchemists could learn the secret of “purifying” base metals into gold, they might gain the ability to purify the human soul. At the same time, alchemy has often been seen as a get-rich-quick scheme and many alchemists as charlatans and pretenders. But many alchemists were in fact serious-minded practitioners whose work helped lay the groundwork for modern chemistry and medicine

23. Compare alchemy and metallurgy


What is alchemy?

Natural philosophy's earliest branch, alchemy, is a philosophical and early kind of science that has traditionally been practiced in China, India, the Muslim world, and Europe. An age-old art, alchemy is steeped in secrecy and mystery.

The primary goal of its practitioners was to transform lead into gold, an endeavor that has sparked human curiosity for thousands of years. Alchemy's objectives, however, went much beyond just producing few golden nuggets. Metals were thought to be both alive and growing within the Earth, and alchemy was based on a complex metaphysical worldview in which everything around us possesses a kind of global spirit.

What is metallurgy?

The study of the physical and chemical characteristics of metallic elements, their intermetallic compounds, and the mixtures of these elements known as alloys is done by the discipline of materials science and engineering known as metallurgy. Metallurgy is the process of removing metals from their natural condition.

Soil, rocks, limestone, sand, and metal complexes are the components of minerals. Mining for commercial metals from minerals is simple and affordable. Ores are the name for these minerals. In general, metallurgy encompasses the entire process of producing metallic materials, from the discovery of minerals to mining, concentration, smelting, and material engineering. The extraction of metals from ores, concentrates, or other resources is the restricted definition of metallurgy.
