11. 7. A solution is made containing 14.6 g of CH3OH in 184 g of H2O. Calculate (a) the mole fraction of CH3OH, (b) the mass percent of CH3OH, (c) the molality of CH3OH.


(a) SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & mass_{\text{solute}} = \text{14.6 g} \\ & MM_{\text{solute}} = \text{32.042 g/mol} \\ & mass_{\text{solvent}} = \text{184 g} \\ & MM_{\text{solvent}} = \text{18.015 g/mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of each component.

• For solute (CH₃OH)

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solute}} & = \frac{mass_{\text{solute}}}{MM_{\text{solute}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{14.6 g}}{\text{32.042 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{0.45565 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For solvent (water)

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solvent}} & = \frac{mass_{\text{solvent}}}{MM_{\text{solvent}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{184 g}}{\text{18.015 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{10.214 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mole fraction of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} X_{\text{solute}} & = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{n_{\text{solute}} + n_{\text{solvent}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.45565 mol}}{\text{0.45565 mol + 10.214 mol}} \\ & = \boxed{0.0427} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the mole fraction of CH₃OH is 0.0427.

------------------------------------------------------------

(b) SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & mass_{\text{solute}} = \text{14.6 g} \\ & mass_{\text{solvent}} = \text{184 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the mass percent of solute (CH₃OH)

[tex]\begin{aligned} \%(m/m) & = \frac{mass_{\text{solute}}}{mass_{\text{solute}} + mass_{\text{solvent}}} \times 100\% \\ & = \frac{\text{14.6 g}}{\text{14.6 g + 184 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \boxed{7.35\%} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the mass percent of CH₃OH is 7.35%.

------------------------------------------------------------

(c) SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & mass_{\text{solute}} = \text{14.6 g} \\ & MM_{\text{solute}} = \text{32.042 g/mol} \\ & mass_{\text{solvent}} = \text{184 g = 0.184 kg} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solute}} & = \frac{mass_{\text{solute}}}{MM_{\text{solute}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{14.6 g}}{\text{32.042 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{0.45565 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molality of solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} m & = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{mass_{\text{solvent}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.45565 mol}}{\text{0.184 kg}} \\ & = \text{2.48 mol/kg} \\ & = \boxed{2.48 \: m} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the molality of the solution is 2.48 m.

12. Methanol (CH3OH) burns according to the equation 2CH3OH(l) + 3O2(g) → 2CO2(g) + 4H2O(l), ΔH°rxn = –1454 kJ/mol. How much heat, in kilojoules, is given off when 75.0 g of methanol is burned


Heat released in the Methanol combustion reaction : 1.701.10³ kJ

Further explanation

The change in enthalpy in the formation of 1 mole of the elements is called enthalpy of formation

The enthalpy of formation measured in standard conditions (25 ° C, 1 atm) is called the standard enthalpy of formation (ΔHf °)

Determination of the enthalpy of formation of a reaction can be through a calorimetric experiment, based on the principle of Hess's Law, enthalpy of formation table, or from bond energy data

Delta H reaction (ΔH) is the amount of heat / heat change between the system and its environment

(ΔH) can be positive (endothermic = requires heat) or negative (exothermic = releasing heat)

The value of ° H ° can be calculated from the change in enthalpy of standard formation:

∆H ° rxn = ∑n ∆Hf ° (product) - ∑n ∆Hf ° (reactants)

We complete the data values for ∆Hf° of compounds

Combustion reaction that occurs  :

CH₃OH (ℓ) + 3O₂ (g) -> 2CO₂ (g) + 4H₂O (g)  ΔH°rxn = –1454 kJ/mol

molar mass Methanol = 32.04 g/mol

mol of 75 g Methanol :

[tex]\rm mol=\dfrac{mass}{molar~mass}\\\\mol=\dfrac{75}{32.04}\\\\mol=2.3408[/tex]

Because from the reaction above, for every 2 mol CH₃OH ,  ΔH°rxn = –1454 kJ/mol, then for 2.3408 mol the heat is

[tex]\rm Q=\dfrac{2.3408}{2}\times -1454~kJ/mol\\\\Q=\boxed{\bold{1.701.10^3~kJ}}[/tex]

17. 2. A solution is made containing 14.6 g CH2OH in 184 g H20. Calculate(a) the mole fraction of CH3OH.(b) the mass percent of CH3OH,(c) the molality of CH3OH.​


Given:

mass of solute = 14.6 g

mass of solvent = 184 g

solute: CH₃OH

solvent: H₂O

Required:

mole fraction of solute

mass percent

molality

Solution: (a)

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of each component.

For solute

molar mass of solute = (12.01 g/mol × 1) + (1.008 g/mol × 4) + (16.00 g/mol × 1)

molar mass of solute = 32.042 g/mol

For solvent

molar mass of solvent = (1.008 g/mol × 2) + (16.00 g/mol × 1)

molar mass of solvent = 18.016 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of each component.

For solute

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{molar mass of solute}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \frac{\text{14.6 g}}{\text{32.042 g/mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute = 0.45565196 mol}[/tex]

For solvent

[tex]\text{moles of solvent} = \frac{\text{mass of solvent}}{\text{molar mass of solvent}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solvent} = \frac{\text{184 g}}{\text{18.016 g/mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solvent = 10.2131439 mol}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mole fraction of solute.

[tex]\text{mole fraction of solute} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{moles of solute + moles of solvent}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{mole fraction of solute} = \frac{\text{0.45565196 mol}}{\text{0.45565196 mol + 10.2131439 mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mole fraction of solute} = 0.0427}[/tex]

Solution: (b)

Step 1: Calculate the mass percent of solute by direct substitution.

[tex]\text{mass percent} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solute + mass of solvent}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\text{mass percent} = \frac{\text{14.6 g}}{\text{14.6 g + 184 g}} × 100[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass percent} = 7.35\%}[/tex]

Solution: (c)

Step 1: Calculate the molality of solution by using direct substitution.

Since the moles of solute is already obtained in Step 2 for solving the mole fraction of solute, which is 0.45565196 mol, and the mass of solvent is already given, which is 184 g or 0.184 kg, the molality of the solution is

[tex]\text{molality} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent (kg)}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{molality} = \frac{\text{0.45565196 mol}}{\text{0.184 kg}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{molality} = 2.48 \: m}[/tex]
