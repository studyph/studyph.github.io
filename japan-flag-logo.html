The Japanese flag is made up of a red circle, symbolizing the sun, against a white background. It is known as the hinomaru in Japanese, meaning "circle of the sun." Because Japan lies at the far West of the Pacific Ocean, the sun rises spectacularly over the sea to the East.

Area = πr²

Area = π(0.3125 ft)²

c. To find the percentage of the flag that is occupied by the red disc, we can divide the area of the red disc by the area of the rectangular-shaped flag and multiply by 100:

Percentage = (Area of red disc / Area of flag) x 100%

In this situation, one may utilize the formula:

Probability = Number of desired outcomes ÷ Number of possible outcomes

As a result, if the color of green, the probability of rolling that particular color is as follows:

Probability = 6 ÷ 10 = 0.6

Numbers ranging from zero to certainty can be used to represent probabilities. Additionally, certainty resembles 1 and no chance resembles 0. This can be multiplied by 100 to get a percentage. Consequently, the chance of getting a red flag is 60 percent.

Probability

Mathematics' study of random events is known as probability, and there are four primary types of probability: axiomatic, classical, empirical, and subjective. Since probability is the same as a possibility, you could say that it is the likelihood that a specific event will occur.

These are the four practical laws of probability:Either it occurs or it doesn't. The probability of an event occurring when added to the likelihood that it won't occur is always 1.

P(A occurs) + P(A does not occur) = 1

Exclusivity. If A and B cannot occur simultaneously (in which case A and B are said to be mutually exclusive), then

P(either A or B occurs) = P(A occurs) + P(B occurs) (B happens)

Independence. If B is equally likely to occur regardless of whether or not A occurs (in which case we say that A and B are independent), then

P(either A or B occurs) = P(A occurs) * P (B happens)

Sub-Events. If B must occur anytime A occurs, then B must have a probability at least equal to that of A.

P(A occurs) <= P (B happens)
