4. write an essay discussing the evolution and changes of art forms from traditional art to contemporary art​


Well it depends on you definition of “traditional”. If you are talking about everything before the 1600’s in Europe as “traditional”, then one thing I would point out is the evolution of self determination for artists. At in point art was a trade and the artist was commissioned to paint what the patrons wanted. Usually the patrons were the wealthy or members of the christian clergy. With the Renaissance there were a lot of paintings of holy subjects and another subject called “myth”, which was basically an excuse to have paintings of half naked people in ones private quarters. There were also the ideas of the individual. No one signed their paintings before this time. So it was a humanist idea to place your mark on the painting in order to say “this is my work, I did this”.

Another point

Michelangelo was a sculptor by trade. But the Pope loved his work so much that he commissioned him to paint the Sistine Chapel.

Think about this. How much cussing and swearing was involved in learning how to paint frescos while laying on a scaffold? Then getting off of the scaffold to discover that the paintings looked tiny from the floor.

My Point is that artists were told what to create. It wasn’t until later periods that Artists had more freedom to create what they wanted.

Later artists did a lot of work with allegory up until the very tale end of the 19th century. Then you had Artists that started doing their own thing like Fauvism, which was experimenting with non-local colours (colours that we wouldn’t use to depict realism), artists like Rosa Bonheur that chose to represent everyday subject matter in large scale painting usually reserved for religious and mythical paintings.

23. 1. Enumerate and write the meaning of the elements and principles of digital artElements of ArtPrinciples of Art​


Elements of Art
1.Line- Lines are mark moving in space between two points whereby a viewer can visualize the stroke movement,Direction and intention based in how a line is oriented.
2.Shape- A shape is a two dimensional design encased by lines to signify it’s height and width structure, and can have different values of color within it to make it appear three-dimensional
3.Form- Is a three-dimensional object with volume of height,width and depth.
4.Color- Color is an element consisting of hues,chroma or intensity and value. Color is preset when light strikes an object and it is reflected back into the eye, a reaction to a hue arising in the optic nerve.
5.Space-Space refers to to perspective(distance between and around) and proportion(size) between shapes and objects and how their relationship with the foreground or background is perceived.
6.Texture- Texture is used to describe the surface quality of the work,referencing the types of lines the artist created. The surface quality describes how the eye perceives the texture based on visual cues.
7.Value-Value refers to the degree of perceivable lightness of tones within an image.

29. Compare the characteristics of artworks produced in the various artmovements. Write one feature in each art movement. Write your answer inside the box.1. Mannerism and Renaissance Art2. Pre-historic Arts and Ancient Art3. Medieval Art and Renaissance Art​


Mannerism and Renaissance Art: Mannerism art was characterized by distorted figures, exaggerated poses, and unusual colors, while Renaissance art emphasized balance, proportion, and realism.

Pre-historic Arts and Ancient Art: Prehistoric art was characterized by the use of natural materials, such as stone and bone, to create simple representations of animals and humans, while ancient art, such as Egyptian and Greek art, used more advanced techniques to create highly detailed and stylized representations of gods, heroes, and everyday life.

Medieval Art and Renaissance Art: Medieval art was characterized by flat, two-dimensional representations, often with religious themes and symbolic meanings, while Renaissance art emphasized perspective, realism, and secular themes, such as humanism and the beauty of the natural world.
