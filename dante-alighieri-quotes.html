1. who is dante alighieri


Durante degli Alighieri, simply called Dante(1265 – 1321), was a major Italian poet of the Late Middle Ages. His Divine Comedy, originally calledComedìa (modern Italian: Commedia) and later christened Divina by Boccaccio, is widely considered the greatest literary work composed in the Italian language and a masterpiece of world literature.[1]

In the late Middle Ages, the overwhelming majority of poetry was written in Latin, and therefore accessible only to affluent and educated audiences. InDe vulgari eloquentia (On Eloquence in the Vernacular), however, Dante defended use of the vernacular in literature. He himself would even write in the Tuscan dialect for works such asThe New Life (1295) and the aforementionedDivine Comedy; this choice, although highly unorthodox, set a hugely important precedent that later Italian writers such as Petrarch and Boccacciowould follow. As a result, Dante played an instrumental role in establishing the national language of Italy. Dante's significance also extends past his home country; his depictions of Hell, Purgatory, andHeaven have provided inspiration for a large body of Western art, and are cited as an influence on the works of John Milton, Geoffrey Chaucer andLord Alfred Tennyson, among many others. In addition, the first use of the interlocking three-line rhyme scheme, or the terza rima, is attributed to him.

Dante has been called "the Father of the Italian language" and one of the greatest poets of world literature.[2] In Italy, Dante is often referred to as il Sommo Poeta ("the Supreme Poet") and il Poeta; he, Petrarch, and Boccaccio are also called "the three fountains" or "the three crowns".

23. when did Dante alighieri wrote the divine comedy? When did it started and when did it last?


The Divine Comedy (Italian: Divina Commedia [diˈviːna komˈmɛːdja]) is an epic poem by Dante Alighieri,

begun c. 1308 and

completed 1320

a year before his death in 1321. It is widely considered the preeminent work of Italian literature and is seen as one of the greatest works of world literature. The poem's imaginative vision of the afterlife is representative of the medieval world-view as it had developed in the Western Church by the 14th century. It helped establish the Tuscan language, in which it is written, as the standardized Italian language. It is divided into three parts:

Inferno, Purgatorio, and Paradiso.

Ipinanganak si Dante Alighieri noong tagsibol ng 1265 sa Florence, Italya, pamilyar siyang kilala bilang Dante. Ang Il Vita Nuova ay mayroong ligtas na lugar sa kasaysayan ng panitikan: bago ang wikang katutubo at halo ng tula sa prosa; at nagsisilbi itong panimula sa obra maestra ni Dante, Ang Banal na Komedya, kung saan kitang-kita ang Beatrice. Ang Banal na Komedya ay ang pangitain ni Dante tungkol sa kabilang buhay, na pinaghiwalay sa isang trilogy ng Inferno, Purgatory, at Paradise. Answer:

Here's my pick, I'll list four.

1. The encounter with Beatrice when both were 9 yeas old (sic!). The event directs the entire erotic dynamic of Dante's life, in forms that are difficult for us to understand today. Beatrice, with her death transforms Dante into a philosopher, to seek consolation. And later in a theologian and a poet of religious subjects, because the fascination of Beatrice's memory is for Dante inseparable to the supreme good of celestial truths.

2. Participation in the Florentine cavalry in the battle of Campaldino (1289), between Florence and Siena. The event confirms Dante in his Florentine patriotism, but at the same time inspires him with disgust towards the discords between Italians, or even between citizens of the same city. And more generally towards war, that was an abomination to the conscience of a Christian.

3. The accusation of "baratteria" (corruption). Dante, a man of profound integrity, sees the opposing faction that has taken power in his beloved Florence (the black Guelphs), resorting to the baseness of an accusation so infamous, which will force him to go into a sorrowful exile for life.

4. The encounter with Heinrich VII Von Luxembourg, Emperor of the Holy Roman Empire, who gives him hope of a moral restoration of the whole Christian Europe. Dante suggests Heinrich VII to move the throne to Rome and claim the full pre-Costantinian temporal sovereignty of the Roman Empire, thus expropriating also the temporal power of the Church that in Dante's mind was an usurpation. Heinrich VII was an enthusiastic youth, very happy to see himself in the attire that Dante had taylored for him, but the task was unattainable.
