7. 1 situational example ng gross national product​


Explanation:

One example of the use of Gross National Product (GNP) is in measuring a country's economic growth. The GNP is the total value of all goods and services produced by a country's residents, regardless of their location. By comparing the GNP of a country from one year to the next, economists can determine whether the country's economy is growing or contracting. For example, if a country's GNP increases from one year to the next, this would indicate that the economy is growing, while a decrease in GNP would indicate a contracting economy.

Another example is to compare the GNP of different countries. This can give an idea of the relative economic strength of different nations and help to identify areas where one country may have an advantage over another. For example, if the GNP of one country is much higher than that of a neighboring country, this may indicate that the first country has a more developed economy and a higher standard of living.

It's also important to note that GNP doesn't take into account factors such as inequality and poverty, Therefore, it's not a perfect measure of the well-being of a country's residents.

In conclusion GNP is a broad measure of a country's economic activity and is often used as an indicator of a country's overall economic health.

13. gross profit pooo example with solution thank uuuu ​


Answer:

Let’s look at an example. By comparing two competing businesses’ profits, you can see which spends more efficiently.

Mechanic Shop 1

A mechanic shop reports $15,000 in revenue for April. They spent $10,000 on the cost of goods sold.

Revenue: $15,000

Cost of Goods Sold: $10,000

You need to subtract the COGS from the revenue. For Mechanic Shop 1, you calculate $15,000 minus $10,000.

$15,000 – $10,000 = $5,000

The gross profit is $5,000.

Mechanic Shop 2

There is another mechanic in the area. Mechanic Shop 2 also reports $15,000 in revenue for April. But, their cost of goods sold is $7,000.

Revenue: $15,000

Cost of Goods Sold: $7,000

Again, you subtract the COGS from the revenue. For the second mechanic shop, you calculate $15,000 minus $7,000.

$15,000 – $7,000 = $8,000

The gross profit is $8,000 for April.

Here is a comparison of the two mechanic shops:

Mechanic Shop 1: $5,000

Mechanic Shop 2: $8,000

Step-by-step explanation:

Mechanic Shop 2 has $3,000 more in gross profit. Looking at both mechanic shops’ figures, the second mechanic uses money more efficiently. That means that Mechanic Shop 2 spends less money to produce a similar level of revenue as Mechanic Shop 1.

Gross profit helps you record the costs required to produce revenue. When the cost of goods sold increases, gross profit decreases. You are left with less money for operating expenses. And, when the cost of goods sold decreases, your gross profit increases. You are left with more spending money for business operations.
