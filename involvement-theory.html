16. Scientist involved in light theory​


1.Isaac Newton: Newton is perhaps the most famous scientist associated with light theory. In the late 17th century, he conducted experiments on the nature of light, demonstrating that it is composed of different colors that can be separated and recombined. He also proposed that light travels in straight lines and that it is reflected and refracted.
2.James Clerk Maxwell: In the 19th century, Maxwell developed a unified theory of electromagnetism that explained the behavior of light as an electromagnetic wave. He demonstrated that light is a form of energy that travels at a constant speed through a vacuum, and that it can be described using mathematical equations.
3.Albert Einstein: Einstein is famous for his theory of relativity, which has important implications for the behavior of light. He showed that the speed of light is an absolute constant that cannot be exceeded, no matter how fast an object is moving relative to an observer.
4.Max Planck: Planck made significant contributions to the development of quantum mechanics, which is a theory that describes the behavior of particles on a very small scale. He proposed that light is composed of discrete packets of energy called photons, which can interact with matter in predictable ways.
These are just a few examples of the many scientists who have contributed to our understanding of light theory over the years.

24. does the theory of evolution involves only living things? why??​


Answer:

yes

Explanation:

Evolution is a change in the characteristics of living things over time. As described by Darwin, evolution occurs by a process called natural selection. In natural selection, some members of a species, being better adapted or suited to their environment, produce more offspring than others, so they pass "advantageous traits" to their offspring. Over many generations, this can lead to major changes in the characteristics of the species. Evolution explains how living things are changing today and how modern living things have descended from ancient life forms that no longer exist on Earth. As living things evolve, they generally become better suited for their environment. This is because they evolve adaptations. An adaptation is a trait that helps an organism survive and reproduce in a given environment.

The current theory to explain the generation of life is known as biogenesis, which simply states that living things come from other living things.

Biogenesis is the production of new living organisms. That is, life does not spontaneously arise from non-living material, which was the position held by spontaneous generation.[1] Pasteur's claim followed Virchow's doctrine Omnis cellula e cellula (all cells from cells),[2] itself derived from work of Robert Remak.[3]
