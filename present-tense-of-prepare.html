Action verbs could be interchangeably used in different tenses to denote the time wether it happened in the past, currently happening in the present, and will happen in the future.

In the case of the action verb "eat":

To denote past tense, you can change this to ate, the past tense of eat. To indicate future tense, simply add will to eat.

For present tense, you can also say, "I am eating." Note that you cannot use "is" for both I and you unlike in "My sister is catching butterflies."

Instead, say,

"I am catching butterflies."

"You are catching butterflies."

PAST PERFECT TENSE

- A verb tense used to talk about actions that were completed before some point in the past.

- Is for talking about something that happened before something else.

PRESENT PERFECT TENSE

- A verb tense that is traditionally formed in English with have and a past participle and that expresses an action or state begun in the past and completed at the time of speaking or continuing in the present.

FUTURE PERFECT TENSE

- A verb tense or construction used to describe an event that is expected or planned to happen before a time of reference in the future.

PAST PROGRESSIVE TENSE

- Indicates continuing action, something that was happening, going on, at some point in the past.

PRESENT PROGRESSIVE TENSE

- Indicates continuing action, something going on now. This tense is formed with the helping verb, in the present tense, plus the present participle of the verb.

Answer:

1.Definition of the present perfect tense. The present perfect is used to indicate a link between the present and the past. The time of the action is before now but not specified, and we are often more interested in the result than in the action itself.

2.Using Present Perfect Tense. To create the present perfect tense of any verb, you will combine the present tense of the verb "to have" plus the past participle of the main verb of the sentence. The past participle of a regular verb is the base word plus –ed.

3.The present perfect continuous is formed with have/has been and the -ing form of the verb. We normally use the present perfect continuous to emphasise that something is still continuing in the present: She has been living in Liverpool all her life.

4.You form the present perfect using has or have followed by a past participle verb. For example, “I have seen Star Wars.” The use of the present perfect here gives us three pieces of information. First, it tells us that the event is finished.
