7. What is ironic or humorous about the following sentence?"Gilray kept pestering me with letters about his chrysanthemum. He seemed to have no faith in me--a detestable thing in a man who calls himself your friend.


The ironic or humorous aspect of the sentence is that Gilray, who calls himself the speaker's friend, seems to have no faith in the speaker. This is ironic or humorous because typically friends trust and have faith in each other. The fact that Gilray does not trust the speaker, despite calling them a friend, is unexpected and can be seen as ironic or humorous.

The sentence is not specifically funny but it's ironic, because it's a situation where someone who is supposed to be a friend does the opposite of what friends are supposed to do. It's showing a contrast between the speaker's expectation of a friend and the reality of Gilray's actions which is not in line with the speaker's expectation.

20. The country that use the first insecticide in the form of powdered chrysanthemum


Answer:

China

Explanation:

It is believed that the Chinese crushed chrysanthemum plants and used the powder as an insecticide as early as 1000 BC. It was widely known that the Chou Dynasty in China widely used pyrethrin for its insecticide properties. For centuries, crushed Chrysanthemum flowers have been used in Iran to produce Persian Powder, an insecticide for household use. Pyrethrins were identified as the potent chemical in the Chrysanthemum plants responsible for the insecticidal properties in the crushed flowers around 1800 in Asia. In the Napoleonic Wars, French soldiers used the flowers to keep away fleas and body lice.

The irony or humor in the sentence is that the speaker is complaining about their friend, Gilray, not having faith in them, while at the same time expressing their lack of faith in Gilray's ability to grow chrysanthemum.
