3. what is the mass percent of sodium hydroxide in a solution that is made by dissolving 8g sodium hydroxide in 50 g


Answer:

percent by mass = 13.8%

Explanation:

Solve for the mass of the solution.

mass solution = mass solute + mass solvent

mass solution = 8 g + 50 g

mass solution = 58 g

Solve for the percentage by mass.

percent by mass = (mass solute / mass solution) × 100

percent by mass = (8 g / 58 g) × 100

percent by mass = 13.8%

5. The fertilizer ammonium sulfate, (NH4)2SO4, reacts with sodium hydroxide to produce sodium sulfate, water and ammonia. Determine the mass of sodium hydroxide needed to react with 15.0 g of ammonium sulfate.​


Solution:

Step 1: Write and balance the chemical equation.

(NH₄)₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O + 2NH₃

Step 2: Calculate the molar mass of substances needed.

For (NH₄)₂SO₄

molar mass = (14.01 g/mol × 2) + (1.008 g/mol × 8) + (32.07 g/mol × 1) + (16.00 g/mol × 4)

molar mass = 132.15 g/mol

For NaOH

molar mass = (22.99 g/mol × 1) + (16.00 g/mol × 1) + (1.008 g/mol × 1)

molar mass = 40.00 g/mol

Step 3: Determine the mole ratio needed.

mole ratio = 1 mol (NH₄)₂SO₄ : 2 mol NaOH

Step 4: Calculate the mass of NaOH needed by using the mole ratio.

[tex]\text{mass of NaOH = 15.0 g (NH₄)₂SO₄} × \frac{\text{1 mol (NH₄)₂SO₄}}{\text{132.15 g (NH₄)₂SO₄}} × \frac{\text{2 mol NaOH}}{\text{1 mol (NH₄)₂SO₄}} × \frac{\text{40.00 g NaOH}}{\text{1 mol NaOH}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass of NaOH = 9.08 g}}[/tex]

30. What are the common uses of sodium hydroxide?


Answer:

Sodium hydroxide, also known as lye or caustic soda, is a highly alkaline substance that has a number of uses. It is used in the manufacture of soap and detergents, paper, textiles, drinking water, and other products. It is also used in the process of refining petroleum and in the production of biodiesel. In addition, sodium hydroxide is used as a cleaning agent and is sometimes used in the food industry as a pH adjuster. It is also used in the treatment of water to remove heavy metals and other impurities.
