9. Tetraphosphorus hexaoxide (MM = 219.9 g/mol) is formed by the reaction of phosphorus (MM = 30.97 g/mol) with oxygen gas (MM = 32g/mol). P4(s) + 3O2(g) → P4O6(s) If a mixture of 75.3 g of phosphorus and 38.7 g of oxygen produce 43.3 g of P4O6, a. What is the limiting reactant? b. What is the excess reactant? c. What is the theoretical yield? d. What is the percent yield?


Answer:

a. O2 is the limiting reactant.

b. P4 is the excess reactant.

c. theoretical yield = 88.6 g

d. percent yield = 48.87%

Explanation:

Compute the mass of P4O6 produced.

Using 75.3 g of P4

mass P4O6 = 75.3 g P4 × (1 mol P4 / 123.9 g P4) × (1 mol P4O6 / 1 mol P4) × (219.9 g P4O6 / 1 mol P4O6)

mass P4O6 = 133.6 g

Using 38.7 g of O2

mass P4O6 = 38.7 g O2 × (1 mol O2 / 32.00 g O2) × (1 mol P4O6 / 3 mol O2) × (219.9 g P4O6 / 1 mol P4O6)

mass P4O6 = 88.6 g

a. What is the limiting reactant?

O2 is the limiting reactant.

b. What is the excess reactant?

P4 is the excess reactant.

c. What is the theoretical yield?

theoretical yield = 88.6 g

d. What is the percent yield?

percent yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100%

percent yield = (43.3 g / 88.6 g) × 100%

percent yield = 48.87%

10. 1. Consider the equation below. (a) Write the balanced chemical equation. (b) Calculate how many moles of CO and P byproducts will be produced together with 50.7 grams of CaSiO3. Ca3(PO4)2(s) + Si2(s) + C(s) ---> CaSiO3 (s) + CO(g) + P(s) 2. A reaction vessel holds 10.5 g of P4 and 3.50 g of O2 to start the combustion of tetraphosphorus forming P4O10 based on the following reaction: Determine (a) the mass of P4O10 produced in the reaction vessel and (b) the amount of the excess reactant being used in the reaction. ​


[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{1a. \: Answer:}}}[/tex]

Balanced Chemical Equation:

Ca₃(PO₄)₂(s) + 3 SiO₂(s) + 5 C(s) → 3 CaSiO₃(s) + 5 CO(s) + 2 P(s)

[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{1b. \: Solution:}}}[/tex]

For moles of CO

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 3 moles of CaSiO₃ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 5 moles of CO. Note that the molar mass of CaSiO₃ is 116.17 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of CO} & = 50.7 \: \cancel{\text{g} \: \text{CaSiO}_3} \times \frac{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{CaSiO}_3}}{116.17 \: \cancel{\text{g} \: \text{CaSiO}_3}} \times \frac{\text{5 mol CO}}{3 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{CaSiO}_3}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{0.727 mol CO}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

For moles of P

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 3 moles of CaSiO₃ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 2 moles of P. Note that the molar mass of CaSiO₃ is 116.17 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of P} & = 50.7 \: \cancel{\text{g} \: \text{CaSiO}_3} \times \frac{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{CaSiO}_3}}{116.17 \: \cancel{\text{g} \: \text{CaSiO}_3}} \times \frac{\text{2 mol P}}{3 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{CaSiO}_3}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{0.291 mol P}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{2a. \: Solution:}}}[/tex]

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation

P₄ + 5 O₂ → P₄O₁₀

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of P₄O₁₀ formed by each reactant.

• Using P₄

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mole of P₄ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mole of P₄O₁₀. Note that the molar mass of P₄ is 123.88 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10} & = 10.5 \: \cancel{\text{g} \: \text{P}_4} \times \frac{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{P}_4}}{123.88 \: \cancel{\text{g} \: \text{P}_4}} \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10}}{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{P}_4}} \\ & = \text{0.08476 mol} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• Using O₂

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 5 moles of O₂ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mole of P₄O₁₀. Note that the molar mass of O₂ is 32.00 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{moles of} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10} & = 3.50 \: \cancel{\text{g} \: \text{O}_2} \times \frac{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{O}_2}}{32.00 \: \cancel{\text{g} \: \text{O}_2}} \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10}}{5 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{O}_2}} \\ & = \text{0.021875 mol} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Determine the limiting reagent.

Since O₂ produced less number of moles of P₄O₁₀ than P₄, O₂ is the limiting reagent.

Step 4: Calculate the mass of P₄O₁₀ that can be produced.

Note that the (maximum) mass of a product is dictated by the limiting reagent. Since O₂ is the limiting reagent, the mass of P₄O₁₀ (molar mass = 283.9 g/mol) that can be formed is

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10} & = 0.021875 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10}} \times \frac{\text{283.9 g} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10}}{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10}}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{6.21 g} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{2a. \: Solution:}}}[/tex]

Note that P₄ is the excess reactant.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of} \: \text{P}_4 \: \text{used} & = 0.021875 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10}} \times \frac{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{P}_4}}{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{P}_4\text{O}_{10}}} \times \frac{\text{123.88 g} \: \text{P}_4}{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol} \: \text{P}_4}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{2.71 g} \: \text{P}_4} \end{aligned}[/tex]

17. Strike anywhere matches contain the compound tetraphosphorus trisulfide, which burns to form tetraphosphorus decaoxide and sulfur dioxide gas. How many milliliters of sulfur dioxide, measured at 770. torr and 22.3°C, can be produced from burning 0.404 g of tetraphosphorus trisulfide?


Answer:

V = 132 m L

Explanation:

Writing the balanced chemical equation

P4S3 + 8O2 → P4O10 + 3SO2

Calculating the number of moles of SO2

n = 0.404 g P4S3 × (1 mol P4S3 / 220.09 g P4S3) × (3 mol SO2 / 1 mol P4S3)

n = 0.0055068 mol

Converting °C to K

T = 22.3°C + 273.15 = 295.45 K

Converting to rr to atm

P = 770 to rr × (1 atm / 760 to rr) = 1.0132 atm

Calculating the volume

V = nRT / P

V = (0.0055068 mol)(0.082057 L•atm/mol•K)(295.45 K) / 1.0132 atm

V = 0.132 L

V = 132 m L
