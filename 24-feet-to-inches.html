13. In a scale drawing, inch represents 3 feet. If the same scale is used, how many inches will be needed to represent 24 feet?


8 inches because

1 in = 3 ft.

24 ÷ 3 = 8

8 inches The word scale, in an engineer's language, is the ratio or proportion of the dimensions being adopted for the drawing and the real life dimensions of the object.  We can simply say that scaling in drawings are used to represent real life objects in the form of reduced sizes drawings.  

Say we have a stick that is 1 meter long, and we wanted to draw it on our short bondpaper, we can use the scale 1:100. That means, we are to reduced the size of the object by 100%. This will give us 1 cm in the drawing because there are 100cm in a meter.

In the problem, the scale given is 1 inch : 3 feet. This means that for every inch measured in the drawing, there will be a corresponding 3 feet on real life. So if this scale is used to represent 24 feet in drawing, we just need to provide the ratio and proportion.

We are to assume that the variable x represents the unknown length in inches.

[tex]\frac{1in}{3ft}[/tex]t =[tex]\frac{x}{24ft}[/tex]

We then cross multiply 24 to 1, and 3 to x to get

[tex](1in)(24ft)=(x)(3ft)[/tex]

Simplifying and cancelling out the unit "feet", we get

[tex]24in=3x[/tex]

Thus, [tex]x=8in[/tex]

24 feet, using the scale, will be represented by 8 inches length.

Find/read the markings. On a standard tape measure, the biggest marking is the inch mark (which generally has the biggest number, if it has them).

As the increments decrease, so does the length of the mark. For example, ½" has a bigger mark than ¼" which has a bigger mark than ⅛", and so on.

tape measure inch marking

Read 1 inch. The space from the largest mark to another is 1 inch.

tape measure one inch

Read one-half inch. Same principle as reading one inch, only this time the space between the second-biggest mark and the biggest is read. You can think of a half-inch mark as half way between a full inch.

read tape measure half inch

The remaining markings follow a similar pattern. ¼" is half of ½". ⅛" is half of ¼". Most tape measure markings go as small as 1⁄16;". This tape divides one more time, down to 1⁄32".
