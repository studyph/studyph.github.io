7. explain the qoutes the ultimate tragedy is not the oppression and cruetly by the bad people but the silence over that by the good people about social stratificationpa help​


Answer:

The quote "the ultimate tragedy is not the oppression and cruelty by the bad people but the silence over that by the good people" is referring to the idea that when people who are considered "good" or morally upright do not speak out against injustice and oppression, it is just as harmful as the actions of those who perpetrate these injustices. The quote highlights the idea that social stratification, or the unequal distribution of power and resources within a society, is perpetuated not only by those who actively engage in oppression, but also by those who stand idly by and do nothing to address it.

The quote is emphasizing the importance of speaking out against injustice and taking action to address social stratification, rather than remaining silent or indifferent. It is suggesting that it is not enough for individuals to simply be good people, but that they must actively work to challenge and dismantle systems of oppression and inequality in order to create a more just and equitable society. It implies that the failure of good people to speak out and take action against social injustice is a tragedy because it allows the oppression to continue and perpetuates the suffering of marginalized groups.

What do we mean when we say that God is the ultimate norm of our goodness?

• Reflection on perfect goodness is most commonly carried out as part of the project of philosophical theology. One prominent methodological strand of philosophical theology is perfect being theology, in which the nature of God is made more explicit by identifying God as an absolutely perfect being and working out what features an absolutely perfect being must exhibit (Morris 1989c; Rogers 2000; Nagasawa 2008). As it is a commonplace that one of the perfections that would have to be exhibited by any being that would qualify as absolutely perfect is perfect goodness, it is obvious why philosophical theologians employing perfect being theology would be motivated to reflect in some detail on the character of perfect goodness and to consider various challenges to the very idea.

I believe you have presented a statement rather than a question. However, I can provide some additional information about consumer and organizational buying behavior.

Consumer buying behavior refers to the process by which individuals or households make decisions to purchase goods or services for their own use or consumption. The purpose of buying may vary depending on the individual's needs or wants, such as to satisfy basic needs (e.g. food, clothing, shelter), social needs (e.g. status, belongingness), or personal desires (e.g. entertainment, leisure).

The decision-making process for consumers is influenced by various factors such as market knowledge, types of goods, and effects of the buying process, among others. For example, consumers may consider factors such as price, quality, brand reputation, and past experiences before making a purchase decision.

On the other hand, organizational buying behavior refers to the process by which businesses or organizations acquire goods or services for their own use or to sell to others. The purpose of buying for organizations is typically to support their business operations and generate profits.

Organizational buying behavior is typically more complex than consumer buying behavior, as it often involves multiple individuals or departments within the organization, and may require the consideration of various factors such as supplier reputation, cost-benefit analysis, and long-term strategic goals.
