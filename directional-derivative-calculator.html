14. Find the directional derivative of ƒ at P in the direction of a. f(x, y) = 2x ^ 7 * y ^ 3 P(1, 5); a = 7i - 24j


Answer:

To find the directional derivative of f at P in the direction of a, we first need to calculate the gradient of f at P, which will give us the rate of change of f at that point in the direction of maximum increase.

The gradient of f is given by:

∇f = (df/dx)i + (df/dy)j

where i and j are the unit vectors in the x and y directions, respectively.

Taking partial derivatives of f with respect to x and y, we get:

df/dx = 14x^6 y^3

df/dy = 6x^7 y^2

Substituting the coordinates of P, we have:

df/dx(P) = 14(1)^6 (5)^3 = 24500

df/dy(P) = 6(1)^7 (5)^2 = 1500

Therefore, the gradient of f at P is:

∇f(P) = 24500i + 1500j

Next, we need to find the projection of a onto the unit vector in the direction of ∇f(P). This will give us the direction of a that maximizes the rate of change of f at P.

The unit vector in the direction of ∇f(P) is given by:

u = ∇f(P)/||∇f(P)||

where ||∇f(P)|| is the magnitude of ∇f(P), which is:

||∇f(P)|| = sqrt((24500)^2 + (1500)^2) = 24521.67

So, the unit vector in the direction of ∇f(P) is:

u = (24500/24521.67)i + (1500/24521.67)j

The projection of a onto u is given by:

proj_a = a . u

where "." denotes the dot product.

Substituting the values of a and u, we get:

proj_a = (7i - 24j) . ((24500/24521.67)i + (1500/24521.67)j)

proj_a = 0.663i - 0.051j

Finally, the directional derivative of f at P in the direction of a is given by:

D_a(f)(P) = ∇f(P) . proj_a

Substituting the values of ∇f(P) and proj_a, we get:

D_a(f)(P) = (24500i + 1500j) . (0.663i - 0.051j)

D_a(f)(P) = 16193.5

Therefore, the directional derivative of f at P in the direction of a is approximately 16193.5.

24. Find the directional derivative of ƒ at P in the direction of a. f(x, y) = 2x ^ 7 * y ^ 3 P(1, 5); a = 7i - 24j


The directional derivative of a function ƒ at point P in the direction of a can be calculated using the formula:

D_ƒ(P;a) = ∇ƒ(P) · a

where ∇ƒ(P) is the gradient of the function ƒ at point P.

To find the gradient of ƒ, we first calculate its partial derivatives with respect to x and y:

∂ƒ/∂x = 14x^6 y^3

∂ƒ/∂y = 6x^7 y^2

Therefore, the gradient of ƒ is:

∇ƒ = (14x^6 y^3) i + (6x^7 y^2) j

At point P(1, 5), the gradient of ƒ is:

∇ƒ(P) = (14(1)^6 (5)^3) i + (6(1)^7 (5)^2) j

= 3500 i + 150 j

The direction of a is given by:

a = 7i - 24j

Therefore, the directional derivative of ƒ at point P in the direction of a is:

D_ƒ(P;a) = ∇ƒ(P) · a

= (3500 i + 150 j) · (7i - 24j)

= (3500)(7) + (150)(-24)

= 21,750 - 3,600

= 18,150

So the directional derivative of ƒ at P in the direction of a is 18,150.

27. Find the directional derivative of f at P in the direction of a. f(x, y) = 2x^7y^3; P(1, 5); a = 7i-24j​


Answer:

To find the directional derivative of f at P in the direction of a, we first need to find the gradient vector of f at P.

The gradient vector of f(x, y) is given by:

∇f(x, y) = ( ∂f/∂x , ∂f/∂y )

Taking partial derivatives of f(x, y) with respect to x and y, we get:

∂f/∂x = 14x^6y^3

∂f/∂y = 6x^7y^2

Evaluating these partial derivatives at P(1, 5), we get:

∂f/∂x(P) = 14(1)^6(5)^3 = 4900

∂f/∂y(P) = 6(1)^7(5)^2 = 1500

Therefore, the gradient vector of f at P is:

∇f(P) = ( ∂f/∂x(P) , ∂f/∂y(P) ) = (4900, 1500)

Now, we need to find the unit vector in the direction of a. The magnitude of a is:

|a| = sqrt((7)^2 + (-24)^2) = 25

So, the unit vector in the direction of a is:

u = a/|a| = (7i - 24j)/25

Finally, the directional derivative of f at P in the direction of a is given by:

Daf(P,a) = ∇f(P) · u

where · denotes the dot product.

Substituting the values we obtained earlier, we get:

Daf(P,a) = (4900, 1500) · (7i - 24j)/25

= (34300/25)i - (36000/25)j

= 1372i - 1440j

Therefore, the directional derivative of f at P in the direction of a is 1372i - 1440j.
