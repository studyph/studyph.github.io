1. What is the value of sin(3pi/2)?


Trigonometry

Trigonometry studies the relationship between the sides and angles in triangles. In Cartesian coordinates, the values of sin, cos, and tan can differ depending on the quadrant. By using the principle of related angles, then the value of sin(3π/2) = -1

Further explanation

Cartesian coordinates divided into 4 quadrants :

quadrant 1: 0° - 90°quadrant 2: 90° - 180°quadrant 3: 180° - 270°quadrant 4: 270° - 360°

The general formula for trigonometric functions is :

sin α = y/rcos α = x/rtan α = y/r

the value of each x and y:

quadrant 1: x+, y+quadrant 2: x - , y+quadrant 3: x - , y-quadrant 4: x + , y-

so the value of the trigonometric function is +:

quadrant 1: all +quadrant 2: sinquadrant 3: tanquadrant 4: cos

the value of the trigonometric function for angles 90+α and 270+α is the same as the angle function α, with the function changing.

example:

sin(90+α) = cos α

tan(270+α)=cot α

the value of the trigonometric function for angles 180+α and 360+α is the same as the function of angle α, with the same function.

example:

sin(180+α) = sin α

tan(360+α)=tan α

28. 3. Using the figure below, derive a formula in finding the surface area of a cylinder. A. SA=4/3pi x r B. SA-2lw+2lh+2wh C. SA= 2TTr2 +2Trh D. SA=6S2​


Answer:
C. SA = 2TTr2+2Trh

Step-by-step explanation:
The surface area of the top and bottom circles is equal to the area of one circle: A = πr^2 x 2 = 2πr^2

The lateral surface area is equal to the area of the rectangle formed by the height of the cylinder and the circumference of the circle: A = 2πrh

Therefore, the formula for finding the surface area of a cylinder is:

SA = 2πr^2 + 2πrh

Here's the equation for finding the surface of a cylinder:
SA = 2πr^2 + 2πrh

Where:

SA is the surface area of the cylinder

r is the radius of the base

h is the height of the cylinder

This formula takes into account the circular base of the cylinder (2πr^2) and the curved surface area of the cylinder (2πrh). By adding these two areas together, we can find the total surface area of the cylinder.
