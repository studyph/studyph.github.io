3. Identify practical applications of visible light, UV radiation, X-ray and gamma ray​


gamma rays application

Gamma rays are ionizing electromagnetic radiation, obtained by the decay of an atomic nucleus. Gamma rays are more penetrating, in matter, and can damage living cells to a great extent. Gamma rays are used in medicine (radiotherapy), industry (sterilization and disinfection) and the nuclear industry.

visible light application

Potential applications of VLC include Li-Fi, vehicle to vehicle communication, robots in hospitals, underwater communication and information displayed on sign boards. The Li-Fi uses visible light for communication to provide high speed internet up to 10Gbits/s.

UV radiation application

UV radiation is widely used in industrial processes and in medical and dental practices for a variety of purposes, such as killing bacteria, creating fluorescent effects, curing inks and resins, phototherapy and suntanning. Different UV wavelengths and intensities are used for different purposes.

x-rays application

X rays are widely used for diagnostic purposes in these fields. Examples might include the observation of the broken bones and torn ligaments of football players, the detection of breast cancer in women, or the discovery of cavities and impacted wisdom teeth.

1. Radio: Your radio captures radio waves emitted by radio stations, bringing your favorite tunes. Radio waves are also emitted by stars and gases in space.

2. Microwave: Microwave radiation will cook your popcorn in just a few minutes, but is also used by astronomers to learn about the structure of nearby galaxies.

3. Infrared: Night vision goggles pick up the infrared light emitted by our skin and objects with heat. In space, infrared light helps us map the dust between stars.

4. Visible: Our eyes detect visible light. Fireflies, light bulbs, and stars all emit visible light.

5. Ultraviolet: Ultraviolet radiation is emitted by the Sun and are the reason skin tans and burns. "Hot" objects in space emit UV radiation as well.

6. X-ray: A dentist uses X-rays to image your teeth, and airport security uses them to see through your bag. Hot gases in the Universe also emit X-rays.

7. Gamma ray: Doctors use gamma-ray imaging to see inside your body. The biggest gamma-ray generator of all is the Universe.

5. Despite its numerous useful medical applications, why is frequent exposure to X- rays is not advisable for humans?

The electromagnetic (EM) spectrum is the range of all types of EM radiation. Radiation is energy that travels and spreads out as it goes – the visible light that comes from a lamp in your house and the radio waves that come from a radio station are two types of electromagnetic radiation. THE WAVE

A disturbance in a medium that transports energy without the net movement of particles is a wave. It can manifest as elastic deformation, a change in pressure, electric or magnetic intensity, electric potential, or temperature.

These are the seven types of waves: Radio Waves, Microwaves, Infrared, Visible, UV, X-Ray, and Gamma Rays. Gamma rays have the shortest wavelength and highest frequency, while radio waves have the longest and lowest wavelength.

Waves may be longitudinal, transverse, or a combination of the two. The waves on the strings of musical instruments are transverse, as are electromagnetic waves such as visible light. Air and water sound waves are longitudinal.

The primary characteristics of waves are described here. Amplitude is the wave's height, measured in meters. The distance between neighboring crests, measured in meters, is the wavelength. Period: the number of seconds it takes for one complete wave to pass a specific spot.

A wave is a disturbance in a medium that moves while transmitting momentum and energy without the medium itself moving. A traveling wave is one in which the points of maximum and minimum amplitude migrate through the medium.
